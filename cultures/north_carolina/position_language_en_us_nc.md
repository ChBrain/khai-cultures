# Position: North Carolina English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Southern American English keeps a second-person plural, y'all, that standard English lost when thou and ye fell away and left a single "you" to cover one listener or a whole room; the Southern dialect filled a gap General American never did. It holds the double modal, might could, useta could, stacking two modal verbs to hedge a possibility more finely than a single modal allows, and in its Appalachian register it keeps a-prefixing, a-going, a-fishing, an old form of the language most of English has long since dropped. Its Southern Vowel Shift turns the diphthong in ride and time into a single long vowel, rahd, tahm, a monophthong other American dialects still pronounce as two vowels in sequence.

## Orders

Wherever the dialect is fully Southern, the pronoun for more than one listener must be y'all and not you, a distinction General American speakers have no grammatical way to make at all. The Appalachian a-prefix, where it survives, attaches only to a verb already carrying -ing and only when the clause is not led by another unstressed syllable, a rule a speaker follows without being able to state it. And the Southern Vowel Shift collapses the ride/time diphthong to a single long vowel automatically, a speaker inside the shift cannot produce the two-part glide other Americans use without sounding as if putting on another dialect.

## Loses

The monophthong in ride and time erases the very distinction, two vowel targets in sequence, that most American English still keeps, so an outside ear sometimes cannot tell whether the vowel is meant to glide at all. The double modal and a-prefixing are also the features prestige pressure strips first, marked as backward by the same broadcast standard that lets the flatter Piedmont accent pass, so the oldest, most historically continuous parts of the dialect are the first pushed toward the margin. And positive anymore, "I eat out a lot anymore," meaning nowadays rather than no longer, is unintelligible outside the dialect precisely because standard English uses the same word only in negative sentences.

## Drives

It trains a speaker to keep singular and plural address grammatically distinct in a way General American cannot, y'all doing work "you" alone cannot do. It rewards a hedge stacked on a hedge, might could, rather than a single flat modal, weighing possibility in finer degrees than a one-modal grammar allows. And in its oldest mountain register it keeps a speaker's grammar closer to an earlier stage of English than the leveled speech of the university town thirty miles down the road, so the same state's English holds two different distances from the language's own past inside one border.
