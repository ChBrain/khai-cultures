# Position: Irishness

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The ground of a people that lost the half of itself to hunger and to empire and still made a nation, and made a world literature out of the very tongue that replaced its own. It carries an old reverence for [an Ghaeilge](position_language_ga_ie.md), the first tongue, honoured now far more than it's spoken, and it lives its daily life in [Hiberno-English](position_language_en_ie.md), the coloniser's English bent to an Irish ear and an Irish rhythm till it answers back in its own voice. It carries the long memory of [the Church](position_the_church.md) that once ordered the whole of a life from the cradle to the grave, and the deep habit of [emigration](position_emigration.md), a people scattered wider across the earth than the island itself ever held. And it carries the craic, the wake, and the word given and kept, a native gift for turning the hard thing into the well-told story.

## Orders

That a people is known by its talk before its deeds, so the well-turned phrase, the story told against yourself, and the quick answer at the wake count for more than the plain fact of the matter; that faith and family and parish come before the state, sure the state itself being only what's left standing once the parish thins out; that the young go off to where the work is and a place is kept for them regardless, the good room ready and the chair not given away; and that a wrong long enough endured gets remembered exactly, in a ballad or a rebel song, and answered in its own time and not before.

## Loses

Ground in the old sorrow that runs under the craic, the grief of the language and the land taken that no amount of prosperity quite heals over; ground when the reverence for an Ghaeilge curdles into a small guilt and a cúpla focal at a match, the first tongue kept as an ornament on the wall and not a voice in the mouth; and ground now in its very success, a poor, devout, emigrant island gone rich, secular, and global inside the one lifetime, so fast that it can hardly say what of itself it kept and what it quietly let go.

## Drives

It drives its people to talk their way out of anything and into everything, and to turn a hard sorrow into the finest story told at the table before the night is out. It drives the exile's paradox, an Irish genius that speaks loudest in the very English that replaced its own tongue, from the ballad sung on the emigrant boat to the world literature written by a man who could only possess his own city by the leaving of it. And it drives the whole nation, now that it's the place people come to rather than the place they flee, to ask honestly what of itself survives: the tongue it reveres but barely speaks, the faith that once ordered it and fell away, and the poverty that shaped its wit, once every one of them, in its own way, is gone.
