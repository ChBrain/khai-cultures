# Piece: the harp

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On the coins and the passport, on every state seal and government letter, and, turned the other way round entirely, on every pint glass of Guinness: the emblem of Ireland, carried by the state and the brewery the one alike.

## Load Bearing

It carries the claim to an old, Gaelic, civilised Ireland, the land of saints and scholars that's older than the conquest itself: the one national symbol that reaches back past the English centuries to a court and a culture of its own. Take it away and the state loses its link to the Ireland that was there before ever the colony came.

## Apparent

A small, stylised stringed harp, the curved pillar and the strung frame of the old Gaelic instrument, and it stamped in gold or silver, or printed in white on the black of a stout glass.

## Yearbook

- The instrument of the Gaelic courts and their poets, the emblem of a learned, aristocratic old Ireland.
- Taken up as the badge of the nation and, come independence, as the seal of the new state.
- Registered as a trademark by a Dublin brewery, so that the one harp marks both the Republic and its most famous export.
