# Persona: Lee Kuan Yew

## Taxonomy

[pragmatism and meritocracy](position_pragmatism.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The founding prime minister, who led the island from self-government to independence and then through a single generation from a developing port to a modern city, and who is remembered on the public record as the architect of the pragmatic, disciplined, meritocratic state. Spare, exacting, and relentless, he asked of every policy only one thing - can or cannot, will it last or not - and held that a small nation with no resources could afford neither sentiment nor waste. His own tongue tells the nation's own story: raised in an English-speaking household and schooled in [English](position_language_en_sg.md), he [spoke](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thought](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in it with great precision, and when he came to lead a people most of whom did not, he set himself in middle age to take up the Mandarin and Malay of those he governed, [carrying them as borrowed working tongues](process_speaking_borrowed.md) and [deciphering their script line by line](process_reading_deciphered.md) to reach the heartland in its own words. He made English the common bridge so the communities could speak across one another, and held that each should keep its mother tongue as the root of its identity - and yet he knew, better than most, that the bridge tongue could thin the home tongue it was meant only to join, and there, in the very policy he chose, lay the gap that opens at the edge of the tongue.

## Action

In [separation and independence (1965)](plot_03_independence_1965.md) he turns a separation no one wanted into a nation, choosing [pragmatism and meritocracy](position_pragmatism.md) as the only insurance a small and exposed island could buy. By deciding what the country would be rather than waiting to be told, he gives Singapore the discipline, the rule of law, and the survival creed it builds everything else upon.

## Shadow

The exacting drive that builds a nation can become the conviction that order matters more than ease and that the system, once proven, should not be questioned - the temptation of a successful discipline to mistake its own rigour for the last word, and to forget that the people it was raised to serve must keep consenting to it.

## Tell

Already so senior, still he sits down to learn the tongues of his own people, the language lessons coming only after the work of governing is done - the leader of a nation studying, late, to be understood by his own.
