# Position: South African English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

What English holds in South Africa that no import brings with it: a vocabulary borrowed wholesale from Afrikaans and the Nguni and Sotho languages it lives among, lekker, braai, indaba, ubuntu, robot for a traffic light, so a single sentence can carry four language families without a seam, and a three-step scale of nearness in time, now, now now, just now, that grades urgency into shades a plain English clock cannot mark. It carries, in loanwords and names, click consonants borrowed straight from the Nguni languages beside it, sounds English elsewhere has no way to make.

## Orders

It obliges the tag question is it? onto almost any statement regardless of the verb that came before it, turning agreement or surprise into one fixed formula rather than a fitted reply, and it fixes a set of raised, centralised short vowels that mark a speaker as local from the first syllable spoken. Among multilingual speakers it makes switching mid-sentence into Afrikaans or an African language the expected, unmarked way to speak, not an exception that needs excusing.

## Loses

The clicks themselves rarely survive intact, since English speech habits flatten a borrowed click word toward the nearest sound the mouth already knows, and the fine social distinctions the Nguni languages carry in address and kinship do not survive translation into the one borrowed English word standing in for them. A newcomer's ear misses the graded urgency of just now against now now entirely, hearing only a vague future where the local ear hears a precise and short window of time.

## Drives

It keeps the speaker reaching, sentence to sentence, across three or four languages at once, so borrowing rather than resisting becomes the ordinary way a new word enters daily speech, and it trains the ear to hold a fine gradient of immediacy that other English varieties leave as one flat word. Growing up inside it teaches a speaker to read, in a single loanword, exactly which community and which register a sentence is reaching toward.
