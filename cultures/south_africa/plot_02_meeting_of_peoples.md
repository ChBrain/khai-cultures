# Plot: the meeting of peoples

## Cue

At the tip of the continent, where the two oceans meet below [Table Mountain](place_table_mountain.md), the Cape became a crossroads. Ships rounding the bottom of Africa put in for water and supplies, and over the centuries peoples came from every direction to a ground that already held many, until the foot of the continent carried more nations and more tongues than almost anywhere on earth.

## Action

Wave after wave came and stayed. The San and Khoe were already here; the Bantu-speaking nations, Zulu and Xhosa, Sotho and Tswana and the rest, had long settled the land; and now came settlers from Europe, the Dutch and later the British, and labourers carried from the East, from Malaya and from India, who became the Cape Malay and the Indian community. Then gold was struck on the Witwatersrand and diamonds at Kimberley, and the under-the-veld riches pulled the whole world in at once, putting men from a dozen peoples and a dozen tongues shoulder to shoulder down the mines and in the new cities. Out of that crowded meeting came a country of many peoples, mixed at the table and at the workface, the raw makings of a nation that would one day have to learn to be one.

## Stage

- [Table Mountain](place_table_mountain.md): the Cape crossroads where the peoples and the oceans meet at the foot of the continent.
- [the Rainbow Nation](position_rainbow_nation.md): the many peoples gathered on one ground, the makings of the bow of light to come.

## Tension

The meeting of peoples made the country rich beyond measure in nations and tongues and gifts, the very feast that the Rainbow would one day celebrate, and it also crowded onto one ground peoples who had not chosen one another, with the mines and the cities throwing them together long before the law would let them be equal. The gathering that made the nation many also set the hard question it would have to answer, how so many peoples might share one home, and the riches drawn from the veld were paid for in labour soberly remembered.
