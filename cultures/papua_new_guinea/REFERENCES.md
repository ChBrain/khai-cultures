# Papua New Guinea: References

The provenance behind this culture: the cultural framing that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-record historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Cultural framing

The pitch [pitch_tpi.md](pitch_tpi.md) is written from Papua New Guinea's cultural
profile and names no dimension and carries no number. Papua New Guinea is
under-represented in Geert Hofstede's published country scores, so the pitch is
written from the widely described features of Melanesian society rather than from a
score table: a strongly communal, collectivist orientation; the wantok system of
language-group kinship and reciprocal obligation; the centrality of kastom (the
custom of the ancestors); leadership earned by feast-giving and oratory (the
big-man) alongside village consensus; and a deep attachment to place, land, and
clan. The framing is recorded here so the basis is on the record without entering
the prose.

## Historical sources

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public historical record. They are credited here and never claimed; the
staging is original work. Contact, the colonial era, WWII, and Bougainville are
staged soberly, from within Papua New Guinea's own self-understanding, with no
atrocity detail.

The two defining personas, [the big-man](persona_bikman.md) and [the Sepik
carver](persona_sepik_kava.md), are archetypes, not historical individuals:
composites that carry the Highlands tradition of leadership by feast and oratory
and the Sepik tradition of carving the haus tambaran, and the wantok and kastom
that bind both.
