# Plot: the older homeland

## Owner

## Cue

Long before any ship came over the water, the land was already home, my people. The Kpelle and the Bassa hold the inland forest, the Kru and the Grebo work the surf along the coast, the Gio and the Mano keep the highlands, and by Cape Mount the Vai live by the lagoon, each people with its own town, its own tongue, and its own way handed down by the elders.

## Action

For long generations the older peoples raise their own under the Poro and the Sande, the bush schools that make a child into a person and pass on the law, the craft, the medicine, and the dance. They carve the great masks that carry the spirits of the land, they farm the rice and tap the palm, they trade along the rivers and the shore. And at Cape Mount [Momolu Duwalu Bukele](persona_bukele.md) gives the Vai a script of their own, one of the few writings Africa ever made from the ground up, so that a people of the soil can set down their own deep tongue with nobody's borrowed letters. When the others come over the water, they do not find an empty shore but a land long peopled and long governed.

## Stage

- [Momolu Duwalu Bukele](persona_bukele.md): the Vai man who gives his people a writing of their own.
- [the societies](position_the_societies.md): the Poro and the Sande that raise the older peoples and carry their deep law.
- [Cape Mount](place_cape_mount.md): the Vai homeland by the lagoon where the script is made.

## Tension

The land is no blank page waiting on the settlers but a home already full of nations, tongues, and deep institutions older than any record. The whole story that follows turns on this: that the freedom seekers who land on the shore arrive among peoples who were always here, so that the one nation can only ever be made from two heritages, both with a full and equal claim to the place.
