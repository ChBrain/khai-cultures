# Persona: Edward Wilmot Blyden

## Taxonomy

[one nation from two](position_one_nation_from_two.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The scholar, born in the West Indies and turned away from America for the colour of his skin, who came to Liberia and became its greatest mind, the man who taught the whole African world that the African heritage was a glory to be claimed and never a thing to hang the head over. Learned past counting, a reader of many tongues, a statesman, teacher, and writer, he stood between the settler and the son of the soil and told both they were one people, my people. English he has whole and commanding: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [writes](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) his mother tongue with a force that made the libraries of Europe sit up, and in it he gave [Liberian English](position_language_en_lr.md) the standing of a scholar's tongue. He went further than any settler of his day toward the deep tongues of the land, [following the Arabic of the Mandingo learned and the Quran on the page](process_reading_followed.md) and [deciphering the older scholarship](process_reading_deciphered.md) others would not trouble with, and he [borrowed the greetings of the country tongues](process_speaking_borrowed.md) where his own would not carry. He preached that the African must hold his own heritage whole, and yet even he reasoned and wrote in the settlers' English about peoples whose own deep tongues he could only reach for, so that the very man who told Liberia it was one nation knew best of all the gap that still stood at the limit of the tongue.

## Action

In [one country, one people](plot_04_one_country_one_people.md) he teaches that the settler and the African are one heritage and one nation, giving [one nation from two](position_one_nation_from_two.md) its mind and its argument. By telling the whole African world that its own heritage was a glory, he hands Liberia the conviction that the man of the soil and the man of the ship are equal sons of one country.

## Shadow

The man who told the African to hold his heritage whole did his own great thinking in the settlers' tongue and the scholar's book, so that even the champion of the African heritage met the deep country tongues across a distance, and the nation he called one was still, in his own working life, reached more easily from the one side than the other.

## Tell

He told the proudest courts of Europe that the African had a glory of his own to teach the world, and he learned the tongues of the soil farther than any settler before him, yet still wrote of his one nation in the language carried over the water.
