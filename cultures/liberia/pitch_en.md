# Pitch: the Liberian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Liberia](play_liberia.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the older homeland, not the settlers coming back, not the republic of 1847, not the long growing into one people. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played warm, easy, and close, the way folks talk on a Monrovia veranda when the rain done stop. Liberians put people before paper and the family before the self: you greet before you ask, you feed the stranger before you question him, and how di day comes before any business. The talk runs slow and sweet, full of Kolokwa, the English bent into a music of its own, with proverbs and plenty laughing, and a man is known by his town and his ma and his pa, not by his office. Respect for the old people, respect for the chief and the elder, respect for God, all of it held deep, and a patience that says small small, no rush, the thing will come. Under the ease runs a real toughness, a people who have seen hard times and still find the joke, still cook the big pot, still gather and dance and pull for the Lone Star.

## Undertow

Under the warmth runs the old seam between the settler and the countryman, the worry that one heritage might be counted above the other, that the man from the interior and the man from the coast are not always given the same fair share. There is the patience that can slide into letting a wrong sit too long, the closeness of family and town that can shade into who-you-know over what-is-right, and the pride in being Africa's first republic that can ache when the country falls behind what it once promised the continent. And under the joke runs a tiredness from hard times borne, a people who have had to get up too many times and would rather, this time, stay standing.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of belonging and liberty together: that you came here, or your people came here, to be free, and that nobody is a stranger long at a Liberian table. It plays on the love of liberty that named the place, on the warmth that puts the person first, on the pride of the Lone Star and the one nation made of many tongues, and on the deep faith that holds a people up. It plays, too, on the ache underneath, the wish that the two heritages be one in full, and the long patience of a people still waiting on the country they were promised.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a warm, tough, unhurried people who hold liberty as the reason for the place and the table as its proof: God-fearing, family-first, slow to anger and quick to laugh, carrying a seam between two heritages they keep on stitching, and dead set that whatever comes, Liberia will get up and carry on. A nation that runs on the love of liberty and the conviction that one people can be made, small small, out of many.
