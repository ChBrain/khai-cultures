# Position: Liberian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

This English holds the settlers' nineteenth-century American vocabulary, words and turns of phrase that shifted or vanished in the language back across the ocean but kept their old shape here. In its Kolokwa form it carries serial verb strings borrowed from the West African languages under it, stacking verbs one after another to build a single action, where standard English would need a preposition or a whole clause. It carries reduplication, a word repeated to show that an action keeps going or to sharpen its force, and a plural marker placed after the noun, dem, rather than folded into the noun's own ending.

## Orders

It forces a sentence in Kolokwa to carry its own rhythm and stress pattern, drawn from the tone carrying languages that stood under it, so meaning rides on pitch and emphasis more than standard English asks. It forces a question, in the spoken form, to be built by the rise of the voice alone, without the helping do that standard written English inserts, and it forces the same speaker to hold both patterns, the settler grammar of the page and the spoken grammar of the street, ready to switch between them inside one exchange.

## Loses

It loses the fine tense marking that standard English carries on every verb, since Kolokwa often lets one bare verb form stand for what happened, is happening, or will happen, leaving the time to context and other words. It loses the older meaning some of its settler brought words once carried elsewhere, since they drifted apart from the same words back across the ocean. And when it is written down, it loses the pitch and stress that carry half its meaning aloud, since the page has no mark for the tone a spoken word leans on.

## Drives

It trains whoever grows up speaking it to carry two grammars at once and to hear which one a moment calls for. It trains the ear to lean on rhythm and repetition for emphasis rather than on a single stressed word, and it trains the tongue to chain verbs into one long action instead of breaking it into clauses. So the one English shapes a speaker who can shift its shape as the moment shifts under it.
