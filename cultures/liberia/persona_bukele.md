# Persona: Momolu Duwalu Bukele

## Taxonomy

[the societies](position_the_societies.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The Vai man of Cape Mount who, in the early eighteen hundreds, gave his people a writing all their own, a syllabary the Vai could read and write without any borrowed alphabet, one of the few scripts Africa ever made for itself from the ground up. The story is that the signs came to him in a dream and he and his kin worked them out into a full writing, town by town, so that letters and ledgers and the deep knowledge of the people could be set down in Vai by Vai hands, ya hear. A man of the soil, of the societies and the elders, he made his people the keepers of a literacy that owed nobody. Vai he has from the cradle: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue, and he did the thing few in the world have done, he [wrote his own mother tongue](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) into a script born for it, where his people had carried their wisdom only by mouth before. The Arabic of the Quran that came along the trade roads he [followed on the page](process_reading_followed.md), and it showed him that a tongue could be written, but the writing he made was Vai through and through. He closed for his own people the very gap that swallows so many tongues, the gap at the limit of the tongue where a deep language has no letters to hold it, and into that gap he set the Vai script and made his mother tongue a written one.

## Action

In [the older homeland](plot_01_the_older_homeland.md) he gives the Vai a script of their own, setting [the societies](position_the_societies.md) and the deep knowledge of the country side into a writing the people own outright. By making his mother tongue a written tongue, he hands Liberia and the whole continent the proof that an African people could devise its own letters and keep its own record.

## Shadow

A script born of one people in one corner of the land could not, by itself, carry the many tongues of the whole country, and the writing that came over the water in the settlers' English would spread wider than the Vai signs; so even this great gift met the long trouble of a land of many tongues, where what one people sets down another still carries only by mouth.

## Tell

He turned a dream into an alphabet and gave the Vai a way to write their own deep tongue with nobody's borrowed letters, the country side answering, with its own hand, the question of how a people keeps its word.
