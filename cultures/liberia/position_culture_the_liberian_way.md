# Position: the Liberian way

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The bond of being Liberian, carried by two heritages folded slowly into one people: the settlers who sailed back across the water for [the love of liberty](position_love_of_liberty.md) that named the place, and the older nations, the Kpelle, Bassa, Kru, Vai, Grebo, Gio, Mano and the rest, who held the land long before under [the societies](position_the_societies.md) that raise a child right. It carries the warmth of the Monrovia veranda, the ease of Kolokwa talk in [Liberian English](position_language_en_lr.md), the family before the self, the stranger fed before he is asked his business, and a God-fearing patience that says small small, no rush. It holds the Lone Star flying alone over Africa's first republic, and the slow stitching that makes [one nation from two](position_one_nation_from_two.md).

## Orders

It orders that you greet before you ask and feed the stranger before you question him; that a man is known by his town and his ma and his pa, not by his office; that the old people, the chief, and the elder are given their due respect, and God thanked before any plan is laid; and that whatever quarrel sits between settler and countryman is worked slow, small small, at the table, rather than forced.

## Loses

It loses ground where the old seam between the settler and the countryman is left to fester rather than stitched, where one heritage counts itself above the other, and where the motto on the seal is recited at the ceremony but not lived in the daily fact of the place. It loses, too, when the hard times, the wars and the want, wear a people so thin they cannot find the joke or cook the big pot, and when the brightest sons and daughters carry the name of Liberia high abroad but do not carry it home again.

## Drives

It drives a warm, tough, unhurried people who put the person before the paper and hold liberty as the reason for the place. It lives in the scholar who taught that the African heritage was a glory to be claimed, in the inventor who gave his people a writing of their own, in the market woman who feeds the whole city out of her mother tongue and her selling tongue both, and in the son of the soil whose feet carried the name of Liberia before the world. It drives Liberia to get up after every hard time, dust off, and keep weaving the settler heritage and the indigenous heritage, small small, into one nation, ya hear.
