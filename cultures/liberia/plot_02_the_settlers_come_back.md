## Cue

In the early eighteen hundreds, freed and free-born Black men and women in America, weary of a country that would not count them as full and free, look across the water to the land their grandfathers were carried from. Ships are made ready, and a people set out to cross back over the same ocean, the other way this time, in search of a place where a Black man can stand free.

## Action

They land on the West African shore and, after hard early years, take hold near the cape they call Mesurado and found a town they name [Monrovia](place_monrovia.md). They build their churches and their houses, they raise their families, and they carry with them, as the very reason they came, [the love of liberty](position_love_of_liberty.md), the conviction that freedom is the whole point of the place. They meet the older peoples who were always here, and the long, uneven work of two heritages sharing one shore begins, my people, sometimes in trade and friendship, sometimes in misunderstanding, but always with the seam of the one nation from two laid down from the first day.

## Stage

- [the love of liberty](position_love_of_liberty.md): the reason the settlers cross the water, freedom as the whole point of the place.
- [Monrovia](place_monrovia.md): the town the settlers found near the cape, the seed of the capital.
- [one nation from two](position_one_nation_from_two.md): the long stitching of settler and son of the soil that begins on the shore.

## Tension

A people seeking freedom land among a people already home, and the great hope of the place, a free Black nation, carries from the first day the hard task of making the freedom shared. The settlers come for liberty and the older peoples hold an older claim, and the whole nation to come must be built across that seam, with the dignity of both heritages kept whole.
