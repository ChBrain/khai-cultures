## Owner

## Shown

The capital on the Atlantic, founded near the cape the settlers called Mesurado, a city of zinc roofs and old churches climbing the hills above the surf, the markets loud with greens and pepper and dry fish, the football raised on every dusty lot. The seat of the first republic Africa ever knew, where the Lone Star flies over the government and the whole nation, settler and son of the soil, meets and mixes in the warm Kolokwa talk of the waterside.

## Offers

A place where the whole nation gathers and mixes, where the country side comes down to the coast in the market and the kitchen, where the freedom the settlers crossed the water for took the shape of a standing republic, and where the football and the talk and the table bind the two heritages, small small, into one people.

## Withheld

The ease of a place that has never known hard times. Monrovia carries the long seam between the settler quarter and the son of the soil, the gap between the love of liberty on the seal and the fair share in the street, and the memory of a city that has had to get up more than once and carry on. It holds the pride of Africa's first capital and the honest weight of the work still unfinished.
