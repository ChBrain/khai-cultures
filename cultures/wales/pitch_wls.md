# Pitch: the Welsh key

## Taxonomy

The key in which the production [Wales](play_wales.md) is played. It changes nothing in what happens: not the fall of Llywelyn, not the rising of Glyndwr, not the Bible of 1588, not the coal and the chapels of the south. It sets only the tone the whole thing is struck in.

## Tenor

Belonging runs deep and close: to the square mile, to the tongue, to the people who sing the same hymns. Speech has a lilt and a rise to it, quick to warmth and to wit, and feeling is not hidden but lifted up - in the hymn, the recitation, the roar on the terraces. Rank counts for little; the chapel and the lodge taught that one man is no better than another and that the gifted poor stand level with the great. It is played in a warm, singing, underdog key, where pride and longing sit close together, and where a small people that was never meant to last takes its stubborn joy in lasting.

## Undertow

Beneath the song lies hiraeth, a deep and wordless longing for a land, a tongue, and a belonging always half-lost: princes gone, a language pressed to the margins, pits closed and chapels emptied. Out of that grew a settled conviction - that the language and the community must be carried, by hand if need be, against a louder world to the east. Under it runs the Nonconformist grain, egalitarian and self-improving, distrustful of show, and a grievance at being overlooked or spoken for that can curdle into self-pity, kept in check only by humour and by the pride of having endured. It keeps the key from turning merely sentimental; behind the warmth is a hard insistence that the people will not be silenced.

## Nerve

It strikes the catch in the throat when the whole crowd sings as one, the swell of hwyl in a sermon or a song, and the fierce, tender attachment to a hard and beautiful land. Beside it the quiet pride of the self-taught and the chapel-schooled, the solidarity of those who went down together and came up together, and the underdog's joy in standing level with the great. It plays for the conviction that to keep one's own tongue and one's own song is a freedom no conquest can take.

## Echo

What is left is the trust that a people can lose its rule and its riches and still keep itself, so long as it keeps its language, its song, and its care for one another. The aftertaste is warm and aching, like a hymn sung in a half-empty chapel: longing on the surface, defiance beneath, and sure that a small nation which held its tongue against the centuries has the better of the bargain.
