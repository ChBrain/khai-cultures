# Position: Welsh English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The English spoken across Wales carries a lilt no other English does, a rising and falling music borrowed from the pitch pattern of the older tongue that sits underneath it, so valley speech sings even where the Welsh words themselves have gone. It keeps a fuller value in unstressed vowels than most English, and a habit, carried over from Welsh, of putting the important word first, there's lovely, isn't it, tidy, that. It holds loanwords the standard tongue has no equivalent for, cwtch for a hold that is more than a hug, bach for a small dear thing, words that name a warmth English elsewhere has to explain in a full sentence.

## Orders

It puts the tag isn't it or is it at the end of a statement almost as a reflex, turning a plain sentence into a small check for agreement every time it is said. It fronts the adjective or the comment before the subject, there's cold, lovely that is, where standard English would hold the comment till last, so judgment is spoken before the thing judged is even named. And it keeps a rolled or tapped r in many valley mouths where the standard tongue has long since let the sound go soft, a trace of the older language's own r still audible in the new one.

## Loses

It loses its lilt wherever a flatter, metropolitan English is heard as more correct, so the very tune that marks the speech as Welsh gets smoothed toward a standard that has no tune at all. It loses too the Welsh it stands in the place of, for the fronted comment and the isn't it tag are themselves the last shape of a grammar the older tongue once carried whole, so what is heard as charming color is also what is left of a language a generation gave up. And it thins where the loanwords go, for a child who no longer hears bach or cwtch at home loses the one word that named that feeling exactly.

## Drives

It teaches the ear to hear a statement as unfinished until the tag lands, isn't it, so a Welsh English speaker checks for agreement in nearly every sentence rather than asserting flatly and moving on. It raises a way of speaking that puts the feeling or the judgment first and the fact after, lovely, that is, shaping a mind that leads with reaction before it leads with report. And it carries, in every rolled r and every bach, the sound of a people speaking English with an older tongue's rhythm still running underneath, so even in English the valleys keep something the standard tongue cannot hear in itself.
