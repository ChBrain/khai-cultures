# Position: Cymreictod

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a small nation that lost its native princes to the conqueror and its rule to the castle ring, and kept, against every attempt to erase it, [yr iaith](position_language_cy.md), the oldest living tongue in these islands. It carries the discipline of [y capel](position_y_capel.md), the chapel that made a literate, singing, levelling people out of a poor one, and it carries [solidariaeth](position_solidariaeth.md), the fierce loyalty the pit and the lodge forged in the southern coalfield. What it hands to whoever is raised in it, whether they grow up Welsh-speaking in the north and west or English-speaking in the Valleys, is hiraeth, the deep, wordless longing for a land and a belonging half-lost, and a stubborn pride in the square mile that refuses to be spoken for by anyone else.

## Orders

That the community gather, sing, and govern itself before it looks to rank or to London; that a Welshman stand by his chapel or his club, his lodge or his valley, the way a miner once stood by the man beside him underground; that the eisteddfod and the choir and the eleven of a rugby side matter as much as any office, because they are where a small nation proves to itself that it can still stand as one; and that the tongue, wherever it survives, be carried to the next generation as the treasure that no conqueror, no coalfield, and no exodus to the city ever quite managed to take.

## Loses

Ground along its own fault-line, the Welsh-speaking heartland of the north and west set against the largely English-speaking Valleys of the south, so that one Wales sings in [yr iaith](position_language_cy.md) and another sings the same hymns in [Welsh English](position_language_en_gb_wls.md) without the words that carried them. It loses ground as the chapels empty and the pits close, the egalitarian discipline of [y capel](position_y_capel.md) and the underground loyalty of [solidariaeth](position_solidariaeth.md) left without the house and the industry that once gave them their daily practice. And it loses ground to hiraeth turned inward, a longing for princes and a golden age that can curdle into looking backward for a saviour instead of building forward, and a wry fatalism in the terraces that has watched its best days recede.

## Drives

It drives the Welsh to measure a small nation's worth not by armies or by rule but by what could not be taken from it: a tongue kept alive in the hearth and the school, a hymn that can still lift a rugby crowd to its feet, a habit of standing together that outlasted the industry that first demanded it. It carries a rebel prince's memory into the mountains as proof that the nation could once rise as one, and it carries a collier's son onto the national stage as proof that the solidarity of the coalfield could speak for the whole country. It drives every Welsh man and woman, whichever half of the country raised them, to hold the square mile, the chapel or the terrace, the tongue or its echo, against a larger neighbour that has never quite managed to make Wales speak only in its voice.
