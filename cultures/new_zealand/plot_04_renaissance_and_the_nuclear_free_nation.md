# Plot: the Renaissance and the Nuclear-Free Nation

## Taxonomy

[play_new_zealand](play_new_zealand.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

By the later twentieth century the breaches of the founding compact can no longer be left unspoken. Te reo Māori has come near to being lost, driven from the schools and the streets within living memory; the land taken in the wars and the confiscations is unanswered; and a new generation of Māori, heirs to the work of leaders like [Sir Apirana Ngata](persona_ngata.md), rises to demand that the partnership of te Tiriti be honoured at last.

## Action

Two returns come together in these decades. The Māori renaissance revives what had nearly been silenced: te reo Māori is brought back through the kōhanga reo and the kura, made an official language, woven again into the common voice; the Waitangi Tribunal is established to hear the breaches of the Treaty, and the long process of settlements begins to answer the historical wrongs. And in 1987 the country declares itself nuclear-free, turning away even its great allies' warships and setting, against considerable pressure, its own independent course in the world. A small distant nation, with [Sir Edmund Hillary](persona_hillary.md) its modest face abroad, claims at once its bicultural identity and its own voice.

## Stage

- [Sir Apirana Ngata](persona_ngata.md): the leader whose life's work for te reo and the Treaty flowers in the later renaissance.
- [Sir Edmund Hillary](persona_hillary.md): the modest, independent face of the small nation to the world.
- [te Tiriti o Waitangi](position_the_treaty.md): the founding compact at last made a living claim through the Tribunal and the settlements.
- [Waitangi](place_waitangi.md): the ground of the founding compact and the stage of its annual reckoning.

## Tension

The nation turns to face what it had let lapse, reviving the tongue it nearly lost, answering the breaches of the Treaty it had signed, and finding an independent voice that owes nothing to the great powers. And yet the work is unfinished and the easygoing surface tempts the country to call it done: the settlements continue, the partnership is still being read true, te reo is revived but not yet whole, and the open question of every Waitangi Day is whether the two peoples can truly be held as one, or whether the reckoning, once begun, will be allowed to drift.
