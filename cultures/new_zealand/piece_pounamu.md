# Piece: pounamu

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

Around the neck and in the hand: the green stone, the jade and nephrite found in the rivers and the wild west of the South Island, carved into the hei-tiki, the toki, the koru, and worn against the skin or passed hand to hand. A taonga, a treasure, it is given rather than bought for oneself, gifted at a birth or a parting or a homecoming, and worn until it is given on again, gathering the warmth and the story of each who wore it.

## Load Bearing

It carries mana and ancestry. Pounamu is a treasure of Te Waipounamu, the South Island that takes its name from the stone, so prized by Māori that it was traded the length of the country and fought over and held as wealth and as mana. Carved into a pendant it carries the line of those who have worn it, growing in mana as it passes down the generations; given as a gift it carries the bond between giver and receiver. It is the land made into a treasure that holds memory and kinship, the whenua worn as whakapapa, and in the giving of it the values of the older world are kept alive in the new.

## Apparent

A piece of green stone, hard and cool and smooth, polished and carved and threaded on a cord. Geologically it is nephrite and bowenite, a mineral pulled from a riverbed. Its weight is entirely in what the people have poured into it - the giving, the wearing, the line of names it carries - so that the same stone a stranger sees as a green ornament a family holds as an ancestor kept close.

## Yearbook

- Prized by Māori as a taonga and traded the length of Aotearoa from its source in the South Island, Te Waipounamu, the waters of greenstone.
- Carved into the hei-tiki, the toki, and other forms, and given at births, partings, and homecomings rather than bought for oneself.
- Held in many Treaty settlements as the ownership of the stone was returned to Ngāi Tahu, the stone of the south restored to the iwi of the south, a tie to [te Tiriti o Waitangi](position_the_treaty.md).
