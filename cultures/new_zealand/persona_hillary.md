# Persona: Sir Edmund Hillary

## Taxonomy

[number 8 wire](position_number_8_wire.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The Auckland beekeeper who, with Tenzing Norgay, became the first to reach the summit of Everest, in 1953, and then spent the rest of his life building schools and hospitals for the Sherpa people who had carried him there: the modest hero who did the impossible thing and refused to be grand about it. Practical, understated, generous, and dry to the bone, he was number 8 wire made flesh, the amateur from the bottom of the world who climbed the highest mountain on it and came down to say only that they had knocked the bastard off. His mother tongue is English, whole and plain: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [writes](process_writing_drafted.md) in the level, unshowy register of [New Zealand English](position_language_en_nz.md), the deadpan that takes the summit of the world and underplays it into a wry aside, the country's whole modesty compressed into a single offhand line. He had a little of the Sherpa and [Nepali](../nepal/position_language_ne.md) tongues he [borrowed](process_speaking_borrowed.md) in his long years among the mountain people he came to serve, enough to work and to thank, but never the whole of them. And there is the gap at the limit of his tongue: that the greatest moment of his life resists the very language he reaches for, the standing-on-the-roof-of-the-world that no words quite hold, so he reaches instead for the smallest, driest line he can find, the New Zealand instinct to underclaim the unspeakable rather than strain after a grandeur the tongue cannot carry. He belongs to [New Zealand culture](position_culture_new_zealand_culture.md) as the modest hero who proved the country's practical ingenuity could reach the summit of the world and still come home unboastful.

## Action

In [the Renaissance and the Nuclear-Free Nation](plot_04_renaissance_and_the_nuclear_free_nation.md) he stands as the country's modern face to the world, the small nation's proof that its ordinary, practical people could do the most extraordinary things and stay decent and unboastful doing them, the living emblem of the [number 8 wire](position_number_8_wire.md) creed. By climbing the mountain and then giving his life back to the people of it, he showed the country a heroism measured in service, not in glory.

## Shadow

The hero who underplays everything is the nation's own shadow turned kindly: the modesty that is a grace can also be a refusal, the deadpan that will not boast sometimes the same instinct that will not aim high or take the credit or admit the want - the dry line that protects the self by never quite saying what it felt.

## Tell

He stood higher than any human had ever stood and described it as knocking the bastard off, then came down and built schools for the people who got him there - the whole country's modesty and decency in one man.
