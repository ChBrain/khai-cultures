# Position: te Tiriti o Waitangi

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The founding compact of the nation: the agreement signed in 1840 between the Crown and the Māori rangatira, the partnership of two peoples bound into one country. It is the only nation founded on a treaty between the indigenous people and the newcomers, and it is a treaty written twice - once in English, once in te reo Māori - whose two texts do not say the same thing, the English ceding sovereignty where the Māori reserved tino rangatiratanga, the full chieftainship over their own. From that gap between the two texts the whole long dispute flows. The compact promised partnership, protection, and participation; it was broken almost at once by war and by the confiscation of land. And it never went away: it remained the standing claim, the agreement the nation is held to, the partnership still owed.

## Orders

That the country is two peoples in one nation, Māori and the Crown bound to each other as partners, not one absorbed into the other; that the Treaty is a living document and a standing standard, against which the nation can be measured and found wanting and held; and that the work of the country is the long task of honouring what was signed, answering the breaches, and making the partnership real in fact and not only in name.

## Loses

Ground when the partnership is treated as settled history rather than a living obligation - when the Treaty is recited as a founding ornament instead of honoured as a debt; and ground, too, to the resentment that reads the partnership as division and would dissolve the two peoples into a single undifferentiated whole, forgetting that the same compact that the Crown breached is also the one that arms every claim to set the breach right.

## Drives

Lives in the statesman who mastered the colonial tongue and the colonial law to hold the Crown to its own signature and to carry his people's mana into the nation's institutions, and reaches back to the navigator, the first to name the land that the rangatira would later refuse to surrender. It drives Aotearoa New Zealand to keep returning to what was signed at Waitangi and to keep honouring the partnership down.
