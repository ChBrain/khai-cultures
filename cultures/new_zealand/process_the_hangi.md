# Process: the hangi

## Taxonomy

Parent group: processes

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Initiated by

Whānau and community gathering for an occasion worth the work: a marae welcoming visitors, a family marking a tangi or a birthday, a town laying on a feast. It takes a pit dug in the earth, stones heated red in a fire, baskets of meat and kūmara and vegetables, and many hands to dig, to set, and to wait the long hours while the ground does the cooking.

## Direction

Toward the gathered people and the shared table: the hangi is Māori in origin, the earth-oven feast carried down the centuries, and now shared across the whole country, a thing both peoples sit down to. It is not fast food and not for one; it is slow, communal, and generous by design, the manaakitanga that honours the guest and binds the gathering, food drawn from the ground for everyone at once.

## Lever

It is driven by the deep value of manaakitanga, the duty and the joy of feeding and honouring those who come, and by the patience of a cooking that cannot be hurried. It holds against the rush of the modern table and the meal eaten alone; against it stand only the labour it asks and the hours it takes, which are the whole point, the work and the waiting the price and the proof of the welcome.

## Echo

The hangi makes audible what [te Tiriti o Waitangi](position_the_treaty.md) asks at its best: the shared table of two peoples, a Māori practice become the whole country's feast, the partnership lived not in law but in the breaking of bread drawn from the same ground. Around such a fire [the Kiwi](persona_the_kiwi.md) waits the long hours with whānau and strangers alike, the welcome cooked into the earth.
