# Persona: Sir Apirana Ngata

## Taxonomy

[te Tiriti o Waitangi](position_the_treaty.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The Ngāti Porou leader and scholar-politician who carried his people's mana into the nation's institutions, the architect of the Māori cultural renaissance and one of the great minds of Treaty thinking: the man who used the colonial law and the colonial tongue to save what the colony had set out to dissolve. One of the first Māori to take a university degree and a long-serving member of parliament, he worked to revive the haka and the carving and the song, to consolidate Māori land, and to hold the Crown to the partnership it had signed. His mother tongue is te reo Māori, whole and deep: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) the world in the language of his people, and worked all his life to keep it from being lost. The colonizer's English he carried and mastered as a second tongue: he [reads it without effort](process_reading_followed.md) and [wrote and legislated in it](process_writing_drafted.md), turning the very language of the empire into the instrument by which he defended his own people - drafting in English the laws that would protect Māori land and Māori culture. He fought for te reo in the tongue that was crowding it out, and there lies the gap at the limit of his tongue: that to save his mother language he had to do his nation's business in the language that was killing it, his own te reo near-silenced in the courts and the schools even as he used English to win it room to live.

## Action

In [the Renaissance and the Nuclear-Free Nation](plot_04_renaissance_and_the_nuclear_free_nation.md) his life's work flowers, the revival of te reo and the Māori cultural return for which he laid the ground bearing fruit in the later renaissance, and the partnership of [te Tiriti o Waitangi](position_the_treaty.md) he insisted upon becoming the standing claim the nation at last began to honour. By carrying his people's mana into the nation's law and learning, he made the partnership a living thing the country could be held to.

## Shadow

The leader who must master the colonizer's tongue and law to defend his own is the nation's own shadow: the painful necessity that the partnership is argued, even now, largely on the Crown's terms and in the Crown's language, and the danger that in winning a seat at the table one is slowly remade in the image of the table - the cost of using the very system that did the harm to set the harm right.

## Tell

He rose in parliament to defend Māori land in flawless English and went home to compose in te reo the songs that would keep his people singing - holding two tongues and two worlds in one life, and refusing to let go of either.
