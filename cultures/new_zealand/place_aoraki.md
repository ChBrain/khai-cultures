# Place: Aoraki

## Taxonomy

Parent group: places

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Shown

The highest mountain in the country, a great pyramid of rock and ice rising above the Southern Alps of the South Island, carrying its doubled name as the nation does: Aoraki to Ngāi Tahu, the iwi of the south, and Mount Cook to the maps. To Ngāi Tahu it is no mere peak but an ancestor, the eldest of the sky-father's sons turned to stone, the most sacred of mountains; to the climbers it is the supreme test, the training ground where the country's mountaineers, Hillary among them, learned the craft. The land made kin and the land made challenge, in one summit.

## Holds

## Offers

A place where the land itself is held as family and as trust, where the deep Māori tie to whenua is made plain in a mountain that is an ancestor, not a possession; a stage of kaitiakitanga, the guardianship that holds the land for those still to come, and of the ingenuity and self-reliance the high country has always demanded of those who would cross it.

## Withheld

The mountain as mere scenery or mere conquest. Aoraki refuses to be only a postcard or only a summit to be bagged: it carries the claim of [number 8 wire](position_number_8_wire.md), the practical reckoning with a hard land, and the deeper claim of an ancestor owed respect, reminding the nation that the land is kin and a trust before it is ever a view or a prize.
