# Position: New Zealand English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The authority of the shared public tongue of Aotearoa New Zealand, the settler English that grew into a register all its own: level, dry, deadpan, and understated, the voice that takes the largest thing and underclaims it into a wry aside. It is the plain speech of the pamphlet and the parliament, the newsroom and the shed, and it is increasingly braided through with te reo Māori, the loanwords once suppressed and now carried into the common voice - kia ora, whanau, mana, kai, aroha - so that the everyday tongue of the country speaks its biculturalism at the surface of the sentence.

## Orders

What counts as the plain public register of the land, the measured, unshowy speech in which the country does its shared business, argues its case, and reports itself to the world. It orders the deadpan that will not boast and the level line that will not skite, the instinct to underclaim rather than strain after a grandeur the tongue will not carry, and it sets the bicultural cadence in which te reo greeting and farewell now open and close the common English speech.

## Loses

Ground to te reo Maori, the older tongue it once crowded near to silence and that is now returning, for the deepest naming of the land and the whole of the partnership live in a language that the everyday English holds only at the surface, the greeting kept while the full fluency stands just out of reach. It loses too at its own limit, where the modesty that is a grace becomes a refusal, the deadpan that will not boast the same instinct that will not aim high or admit the want, so that the plainest and justest thing must be said and said again before the words are heard.

## Drives

It drives New Zealand to speak plainly and underclaim the large thing. It is the whole mother tongue of [Sir Edmund Hillary](persona_hillary.md), who took the summit of the world and knocked the bastard off; of [Kate Sheppard](persona_sheppard.md), who wrote her way to the vote in the pamphleteer's measured force; and of [the Kiwi](persona_the_kiwi.md), dry and deadpan and now braided with the borrowed te reo of the common voice. It was carried and mastered as a second tongue by [Sir Apirana Ngata](persona_ngata.md), who turned the very language of the empire into the instrument that defended his own people and their te reo. So the shared register shows who owns it by birth and who took it up to be heard.
