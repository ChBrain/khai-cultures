# Place: Waitangi

## Shown

The Treaty grounds above the Bay of Islands in the far north, the green sweep of lawn running down to the water where, on 6 February 1840, the Crown and the Māori rangatira signed te Tiriti o Waitangi. The Treaty House stands there, and beside it the great carved wharenui and the largest of waka, the war canoe launched each year on the anniversary. It is the birthplace of the nation, the one ground where the founding compact was made flesh, and each Waitangi Day it becomes the stage of the country's annual reckoning with the partnership it began there.

## Offers

A place where the nation looks its own founding in the face, where the two peoples gather on the ground of their agreement to mark it, debate it, and hold each other to it; a stage at once of ceremony and of protest, where the partnership is celebrated and contested in the same breath, and where the country renews, or fails to renew, the compact it was born of.

## Withheld

The comfort of a settled founding. Waitangi carries the gap between the two texts signed upon it - the partnership promised and the partnership breached, the protest as much a part of the day as the powhiri - and refuses the nation the easy story, reminding it each February that the treaty is a living debt and that the doubled name is not yet wholly true.
