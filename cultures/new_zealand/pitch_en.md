# Pitch: the New Zealand tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Aotearoa New Zealand](play_new_zealand.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens - not the Great Migration, not the Treaty, not the social laboratory, not the renaissance and the nuclear-free turn. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which all of it is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played low, dry, and level: a people who would rather underclaim than overclaim, who deflect a compliment, who find the show-off and the big-noter faintly embarrassing and the quiet achiever the real thing. Modest, understated, self-deprecating, practical and unflashy, warm but reserved, they prize the fair go and the level field, back the underdog as a matter of instinct, and trust the person who gets on with it over the one who talks about it. Humour runs dry and deadpan, the joke often at one's own expense. And through the everyday English runs te reo Māori, woven in without ceremony - kia ora, whānau, mana, aroha - the bicultural cadence that has become the country's own voice.

## Undertow

Under the modesty runs the tall poppy cut down: the same instinct that distrusts the skite can also shrink ambition, mistrust excellence, and pull the one who rises back into the row. The smallness and the distance breed ingenuity and self-reliance, but breed too an insecurity, a cringe, an eye forever turned to how the country looks from the larger world. And under the easygoing "she'll be right" surface, with its faith that things will sort themselves out, lies the unfinished work of the Treaty, the partnership still owed, which the relaxed manner can let slide rather than face.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of the fair go and the quiet achiever: the conviction that no one is your better, that the field should be level, that the modest doer is worth more than the loud claimer, and that ingenuity and decency matter more than show. It plays on the pride of being small and far and self-sufficient, of fixing it yourself, of backing the underdog and standing one's own ground against the big and the powerful. And it plays on the warmth under the reserve, the manaakitanga that opens the door and shares the table once the show is set aside.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people understated and self-sufficient: dry, modest, practical, suspicious of pretension and proud of being small, who would sooner do the thing than boast of it. A nation that runs on the fair go and the level field, that carries two tongues and two peoples in one easygoing voice, and that, for all the work the Treaty still asks of it, believes the partnership and the fairness are worth the keeping.
