# Piece: the silver fern

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On the black jersey of the All Blacks and the Silver Ferns, on the coins and the war graves, on the exporter's box and the traveller's backpack: the frond of the ponga, the native tree fern that shows silver-white on its underside, and the koru, the unfurling spiral of the new frond. It is the national emblem worn and shown wherever the country wants to mark itself, the quiet sign by which New Zealanders abroad know each other.

## Load Bearing

It carries the nation's shared face. A small country without a long heraldry took a plant from its own forest floor and made it the badge of who it is, a symbol drawn from the land itself rather than from crown or conquest. On the black jersey it stands for the country's sporting pride and its underdog ferocity; as the koru it carries the Māori meaning of new life, growth, and renewal, the spiral that unfurls. It is the mark the modest nation allows itself, the one emblem the understated people will wear openly, and the sign that gathers Māori and Pākehā under a single image grown from their common ground.

## Apparent

A leaf, in the end: the frond of a fern that grows wild through the bush, silver beneath and green above, or its young shoot curled into a tight spiral. Printed white on black or stamped in metal, it is only the shape of a plant. Its weight is entirely in what the country has poured into it, and in the jerseys, the graves, and the badges that carry it out into the world.

## Yearbook

- Worn on the jersey of New Zealand's national teams since the nineteenth century, the silver fern on black becoming known across the world of sport.
- Carried on the headstones and memorials of New Zealand's war dead, the emblem of the country far from home.
- Taken up as the koru, the unfurling spiral of new life, in Māori art and in the nation's design, a symbol of growth and renewal drawn from the same plant.
