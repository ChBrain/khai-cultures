# Process: the haka

## Taxonomy

Parent group: processes

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Initiated by

The All Blacks, the national rugby team in their black jersey and silver fern, taking the field before a test match, and the whole country watching with them. Rugby is the national game, the sport a small nation plays as well as anyone on earth, and before the whistle the team lays down the haka, the Māori posture dance of challenge, performed by all the players whatever their whakapapa, with the nation roaring behind them.

## Direction

Toward the contest and the shared identity that opens it: the haka is a Māori challenge, a wero laid down before the rivals, and in rugby it has become the ritual by which the whole nation, Māori and Pākehā together, declares itself before the world. It channels the small country's fierce pride and its underdog defiance into a single stamping, chanting wall, the modest people for once openly, unmistakably announcing themselves.

## Lever

It is driven by the love of the game that binds the country and by the power of a Māori ritual taken up as the whole nation's own. It holds against the cringe and the smallness, giving the understated people one moment of unembarrassed ferocity; against it stands only the risk of the haka worn thin by repetition or performed without the respect it is owed, the challenge that must be meant to mean anything.

## Echo

The haka makes audible what [te Tiriti o Waitangi](position_the_treaty.md) promises: a Māori thing carried by the whole country as its own, two peoples speaking with one voice before the world, the partnership performed where everyone can see it. In that stamping line [the Kiwi](persona_the_kiwi.md) sheds the modesty for ninety seconds and roars, then goes back to playing, and to being, the quiet achiever.
