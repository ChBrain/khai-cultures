# Position: Micronesian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Micronesian English carries the plain resources of English itself: a fixed clause that needs no noun class or possession category to parse, and a paperwork register practiced enough to let a Yapese speaker and a Kosraean speaker read the identical clause without either crossing into the other's mother tongue. Its borrowed nouns, "compact," "constitution," "grant," slot in unmarked by gender or class, letting one written page serve four otherwise unlike grammars at once.

## Orders

Like all English, it forces an overt subject and a fixed subject-verb-object order in place of the case endings the local languages do without, a third-person -s stitched onto the present-tense verb, and a single possessive marker, an apostrophe-s or "of," to cover ownership of anything at all, whether food, land, a canoe, or a title, categories the islands' own tongues keep grammatically apart.

## Loses

It cannot easily carry what the mother tongues grammaticalize as a matter of course: Pohnpeian's separate possessive classifiers, which mark whether a thing owned is drunk, eaten, planted, or ridden, its distinct chiefly vocabulary reserved for address to a title-holder, or the Chuukese and Pohnpeian kin terms that split a mother's side of the family from a father's side where English flattens both to "aunt" and "uncle." Set down in English, a rank or a bond that the home tongue marks in the grammar itself survives only if a speaker adds the words by hand.

## Drives

It drives whoever must hold both systems to translate rank, kinship, and ownership across two grammars before a sentence can leave the mouth, carrying the local classifier or honorific silently in mind while the flat English clause carries none of it aloud. So the shared tongue trains a doubled attention, one grammar that marks these things by rule and one that must be told them each time.
