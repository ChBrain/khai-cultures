# Persona: Samuel Ajayi Crowther

## Taxonomy

[the Learning](position_the_learning.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

A Yoruba boy named Ajayi, seized and carried off as a child, set free from a slave ship and landed at Freetown, who there was schooled and grew to become the first African bishop and a maker of the written word in his own people's tongue. Diligent, devout and exact, he is the very fruit of the town of the free and of Fourah Bay: the carried-away child remade by the Learning into a scholar who gave learning back to the coast. He was schooled into [English](position_language_en_sl.md) and Latin and Greek, which he came to hold whole - he [read deeply and followed all he read](process_reading_followed.md) and [wrote in the schooled tongue](process_writing_drafted.md) with care. But his deepest labour was to turn back to his own Yoruba: he [thought it](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) as his first tongue and [set it down on the page](process_writing_mother_tongue.md), building its grammar and its dictionary and putting scripture into the mother tongue, so that what had only been spoken could now be read. He carried a child's lost language back across the gap and gave it letters, and in that crossing - from the spoken to the written, from the tongue of his capture to the tongue of his cradle - he closed by his own hand the gap at the limit of the tongue that had once left him speechless and sold. He belongs to [Salone](position_culture_salone.md), the boy the Learning raised into a bishop, and carries too the [Naija](../nigeria/position_culture_naija.md) of the Yoruba birth-tongue he never let go.

## Action

In [the Athens of West Africa](plot_03_athens_of_west_africa.md) he is the first fruit of the Learning: the boy freed from the ship and schooled at Fourah Bay who rose to be the first African bishop, the living proof of what the town of the free could make of a carried-away child. He carries [the Learning](position_the_learning.md) out from Freetown across the coast, repaying the world that took him by teaching it and by giving his mother tongue its letters.

## Shadow

The scholar lifted up by the Learning can be set so high above his beginnings that the lamp becomes a wall: the schooled coast, with men like him for its pride, can come to look down on the people of the land and their societies, and the gown that was a ladder up can be turned into a fence to keep others below. The making of one bishop must not become the unmaking of respect for the unlettered elder.

## Tell

He spends his greatest learning not on the schooled tongue that raised him but on giving letters to the unwritten mother tongue of his childhood - holding that the highest use of the book is to hand the book to those who have none.
