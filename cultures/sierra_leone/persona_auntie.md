# Persona: Auntie Yeama

## Taxonomy

[the Societies](position_the_societies.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The auntie who holds the household and the whole street together: the woman in the board house with the big cooking pot, who feeds everybody who passes, settles the quarrels, keeps the rites, and raises children who are not all her own. "Kushe, mi pikin, how di body?" she calls before you reach the gate, and the plate is on the table before you have said why you came. Warm, sharp-tongued and tireless, generous to a fault and quick with a proverb, she is the welcome of Salone in one woman - the keeper of the Awujoh table and the elder who answers for the young. Krio is her tongue, the lingua franca she works the whole street in: she [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) the gossip, the blessing and the scolding in it, and she carries the songs and prayers of the old rites in the tongues of the people who raised her. The [English](position_language_en_sl.md) of the schooled grandchildren who answer her in the town speech she [catches at the edge](process_reading_caught.md), proud of their books even as it carries them a little away from her table. When a grandchild answers in fine English what she asked in warm Krio, she feels the gap at the limit of the tongue open right across her own kitchen - "dem book dem sweet, but den nor de ansa mi na we yon mot." She belongs to [Salone](position_culture_salone.md) and keeps its welcome set at her own table for every soul that passes.

## Action

In [the Nation Comes to Itself](plot_04_the_nation_comes.md) she is the keeper of the welcome that makes one Salone of many: the Awujoh table where the living feed the family living and gone, the open door that takes in every passing soul, the elder who raises the young into people. She carries [the Societies](position_the_societies.md) into the everyday, the rites and the honour for elders kept alive in the household, the deep way that a person is made by the people around them.

## Shadow

The auntie who feeds and raises everyone can hold the household so tight that the young must choose between her warm table and the wide world the books open; the welcome that gathers can also bind, and the elder who answers for the young can also rule them. And the home tongue she keeps so warm can thin in the mouths of grandchildren schooled into other speech, the rites she guards going unlearned by those who go to the town and never carry the lore home.

## Tell

She sets a plate for the visitor before she asks his name and a plate for the family gone before she feeds the living - holding that nobody, here or hereafter, sits down hungry at her table.
