# Process: the Awujoh feast

## Taxonomy

Parent group: processes

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Initiated by

A family, most often a Krio family, gathering the kin and the neighbours for the Awujoh: a feast to give thanks, to mark a passing, to bless a child or a new house, or to keep faith with those gone before. The big pots come out - the akara and the rice, the foofoo and the plasas, the ginger beer - and a plate is set aside for the ancestors. "Una kam, una kam, di chop don redy," the auntie calls down the street, and no one who comes is turned away.

## Direction

Toward gratitude, blessing, and the binding of the family living and the family gone into one table. The Awujoh is the welcome made into a rite: the door thrown open, the food shared until it runs out, the prayers said for those who passed and for those still to come. It is the day a household counts its blessings, settles its debts to the dead, and proves once more that "stranger no de tan stranger" - that the table is always set for one more.

## Lever

It is driven by the deep Salone faith that nobody is alone and nobody, living or gone, sits down hungry, and by the auntie's pot that makes a feast out of whatever the house can give. It holds against want and against forgetting, gathering the scattered kin and keeping faith with the ancestors; against it stand the strain of a household giving more than it has, and the slow pull of the schooled young away from the old rites toward the wide world.

## Echo

The Awujoh makes audible what the whole country runs on: that we own people are bound to one another across every distance, even the distance of death, and that the surest welcome is a shared plate. Around this table the family living feeds the family gone, and the many of Salone are made one for a day - "if yu wan go far, go togeda."
