# Pitch: the Salone tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Sierra Leone](play_sierra_leone.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens - not the Lion Mountains, not the founding of Freetown, not the Athens of West Africa, not the nation coming to itself. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played warm, easy and full of welcome, with plenty time for greeting. Here you do not rush past a person; you stop, you ask "kushe, how di body?", you ask after the family before you ask after the matter. People hold tight to one another - una own people, the kin, the town, the church or the mosque, the secret society that raised you - and a person stands or falls with the group, never just alone. Age is honoured and the elders are heard; the chief and the family head carry weight, and you bend the knee to those who came before. The tongue is Krio, soft and proverbed, made to carry a joke and a hard truth in the same breath. The visitor is fed before the matter is touched, for "stranger no de tan stranger", and tenki, padi, una and we own people thread through every exchange, marking that nobody here is meant to stand by himself.

## Undertow

Under the welcome runs the weight of memory - a people who know in the marrow what it is to be carried off and to be brought home, so the open door is also a wound remembered with dignity. Under the warmth runs the pull between the two stories, the people of the land and the returned, each fearing to be made the smaller; under the honour given to elders and chiefs runs the patience of those who must wait their turn; and under the blessing of the diamonds and the green hills runs the old fear that what comes up out of the ground may divide as much as it provides, and that the rope held together by trust can fray when each pulls his own end.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of homecoming and belonging: the conviction that nobody is meant to be alone, that a guest soon becomes one of us, and that a person is made a person only by the people and the society around them. It plays on the pride of being the home for those the world sold away, on the sweetness of the Awujoh table where the living feed the family living and gone, on the lilt of the Krio proverb and the bend of the maringa, and on the trust that "if yu wan go far, go togeda." It plays, too, on the ache underneath - the long memory of the trade, and the prayer that the land's riches bless the whole house.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people who carry their welcome like a lamp: warm, unhurried, bound close to kin and society, schooled and proverbed, holding two stories in one Salone. A country that runs on the faith that no stranger stays a stranger, that the table is always set for one more, and that for all the weight of memory and the pull of the rope, we own people will see one another through.
