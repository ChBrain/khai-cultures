# Position: Sierra Leonean English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The grammatical machinery of English, an array of modal verbs, would, could, might, must, that lets a speaker grade a claim from bare possibility to plain obligation without adding a single new word to the sentence, and a vast, precise vocabulary built for the school, the ledger and the law. It is a stress-timed tongue, its rhythm carried by the beat of the stressed syllable rather than by rising and falling pitch.

## Orders

It forces a tense onto every finite verb and an article, a or the, onto nearly every countable noun, marking definiteness every time a thing is named. It forces do-support to ask a question or deny a thing, so that no statement can be turned round or denied without calling in a second, empty verb to carry the work.

## Loses

Carrying no tone of its own, it cannot hold the pitch-based distinctions that Mende, Temne and Limba build into the very same syllables, where a change of pitch alone can change one word into another; whatever meaning those tongues carry on pitch, English must instead spell out in extra words. It also loses the proverb and the praise line that live whole only in Krio and the home tongues, and thins toward silence wherever a schooled answer comes back flat and correct where a warmer tongue once carried the same thought.

## Drives

It drives whoever holds it to lean on word order and auxiliary verbs rather than tone or ending to carry meaning, subject before verb before object, every time. It keeps a cooler, schooled register apart in the mind from the tonal warmth of Krio and the home tongue, so that the same person often holds two ways of meaning the same thing, one flat and precise, one carried on the rise and fall of pitch.
