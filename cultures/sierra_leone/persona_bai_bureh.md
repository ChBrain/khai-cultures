# Persona: Bai Bureh

## Taxonomy

[the Societies](position_the_societies.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

A ruler and warrior chief of the northern country, a Temne and Loko leader famed for his command and his nerve, who stood for the law and the authority of the people of the land. Shrewd, disciplined and respected far beyond his own town, he is the figure of the rooted Salone - the elder whose word carries weight, the chief who answers for his people, the man made by the society that raised him and who in turn holds the society together. His tongue is the speech of the northern country, Temne, and he holds it whole: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) it, [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) it, and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) the law and the lore of his people in it, the tongue of the elders and the secret societies. The strangers' [English](position_language_en_sl.md) on the coast he [caught only at the edge of the ear](process_reading_caught.md) and never took for his own, dealing with that world through the Krio that ties the country together and through those who could carry his words. He commanded in the tongue of his own land, and what passed between his people and the coastal power often had to cross from Temne through other mouths, so that at the limit of the tongue stood the old distance between the rooted and the schooled - "wetin di kantri pipul sabi, na for dem yon mot e de tok."

## Action

In [the Lion Mountains](plot_01_the_lion_mountains.md) he stands for the deep authority of the people of the land, the chief and the society older than any settlement on the coast, holding the law and the honour of the rooted Salone. He carries [the Societies](position_the_societies.md) into the country's story as the proof that a person is made by the community, the elders are heard, and the land had its own order long before any ship came.

## Shadow

The strength of the rooted leader can harden into a wall: the authority of the chief and the society, held too tight against the wider Salone, can keep the two stories of the country apart, the land and the settlement looking sideways at each other. The honour owed to the elders can curdle into a refusal of the new, just as the schooled coast can curdle into a contempt for the old - and the rope frays from both ends at once.

## Tell

He answers for his whole people and bends to no man above his own law, holding that a chief is the servant of the society that made him, not its master - the elder who carries the town on his own back.
