# Play: Saint Kitts and Nevis

## Estate

## Name

The Federation of Saint Kitts and Nevis, two volcanic isles in the Leeward chain of the eastern Caribbean, the smallest sovereign state in the Americas by both land and people. ISO KN, language en, the King's English the official tongue and a warm Kittitian-Nevisian Creole the tongue of the yard. St. Kitts the larger isle with the great green cone of Mount Liamuiga at its heart, Nevis its sister across a two-mile strait beneath her cloud-ringed peak. A federation of two, bound by law and by a narrow channel, and not always of one mind.

## Arc

Long before the sail, the Kalinago held these isles and called the larger Liamuiga, the fertile land. The English came in 1624 and made St. Kitts their first colony in the whole Caribbean, the seed-bed from which they settled Antigua, Montserrat, Barbuda and more, so that the island earned its name as the Mother Colony of the West Indies. The French shared and fought over it, and high above the cane the great fortress rose on Brimstone Hill, the Gibraltar of the West Indies, its black stone walls climbing the volcanic rock. The land turned almost wholly to one crop: sugar, cane to the waterline, worked under a slavery as intense as any in the islands, a near-total monoculture that made a few rich and held the many in bondage. Emancipation came in 1834, but the cane stayed king and the worker stayed in the field, free in law and bound to the estate still. Out of that field, in time, rose a labour movement, and out of the labour movement rose a nation. Across the strait, Nevis, the sister isle, had given the world Alexander Hamilton, born under her peak; she would give the federation its hardest question, very nearly seceding to go alone. Independence came in 1983, two islands made one country, the smallest in the hemisphere. The sugar ran on until 2005, the last working sugar industry in the Caribbean, and then the mills fell silent and the cane gave way to the visitor and the sea. Through it all runs Sugar Mas at the turning of the year, the masquerade dancing in the streets of a people who made a festival out of the very word for the harvest that once held them.

## Stakes

Whether the smallest sovereign nation of the Americas can hold its two-island federation as one across the narrow strait that divides it, honoring Nevis's voice without losing the union; whether it can find a future beyond the sugar that made and bound it, now that the cane is cut for the last time and the sea is all that is left to sell; and whether it can carry the weight of being the cradle of the English sugar empire, reckoning soberly with the slavery at its foundation while building something free of it, or whether smallness, division, and the long shadow of the cane finally outrun what so small a country can hold.
