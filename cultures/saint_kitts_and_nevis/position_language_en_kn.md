# Position: the Kittitian-Nevisian tongue

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

English as spoken across St Kitts and Nevis carries, at its creole end, features no formal English grammar owns: preverbal tense-aspect markers standing in front of the verb instead of endings tacked onto it (done, does, going or gon for completed, habitual, and future action), serial verb constructions that string two verbs together for a single event (carry go, take come), and a zero copula, where "is" or "are" can simply be left out between subject and description. It keeps reduplication as a live device for intensity (nice-nice, hot-hot), a resource formal English grammar does not offer at all.

## Orders

It forces a choice at every verb of which marker goes in front of it rather than which ending follows it, since the creole end of the range carries tense and aspect ahead of the verb, not inside it. It forces the speaker to mark habitual action with "does" where formal English would simply inflect the verb, and it forces a plain, uninflected noun to stand for both singular and plural, leaving number to be read from context rather than from a suffix.

## Loses

What inflected marking would show at a glance, the creole register leaves to be inferred: past tense and plurality can go unmarked on the word itself, recoverable only from a preceding marker or from the sentence around it, so a form doing without "-ed" or "-s" is not incomplete, only carried differently. And behind it, the deeper loss: the West African languages the foreparents were carried across the water speaking, thinned by the plantation into the preverbal markers and serial verbs of the creole itself, surviving now as grammar rather than as words.

## Drives

It drives whoever speaks it to hold two systems of marking tense at once, the suffix of formal English and the preverbal particle of the creole, choosing between them by ear for who is listening. It trains the speaker to hear serial verbs and a missing copula as complete, ordinary sentences rather than as fragments, and it makes switching between the schooled register and the yard register a daily act of judgment rather than a fixed rule.
