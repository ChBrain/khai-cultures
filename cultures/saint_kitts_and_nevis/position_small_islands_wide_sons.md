# Position: small islands, wide sons

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The standing of two of the smallest inhabited specks in the Atlantic as places that sent figures out to the whole wide world far beyond their size. From the strait between them came Alexander Hamilton, born on Nevis, who crossed the sea as a boy and helped found the United States and shape its money and its arguments. From St. Kitts came Caryl Phillips, carried to England as an infant, who grew to write the Caribbean and its scattering into the literature of the English language. It is the proposition that smallness is no measure of reach, that a country no bigger than a town can be the cradle of a nation's founder and of a literature, and that the islands' truest export, after the sugar, has been their people, sent out across the water to make their mark and to carry the islands' name.

## Orders

That the islands claim their wide sons with pride and count them part of the national story, however far they went; that the scattering of the people across Britain, America, and the world is not only a loss but a reach, the small islands present in great cities; and that being small is never to be small in spirit, the speck in the sea answering as an equal to the wide world.

## Loses

Ground when the wide sons are claimed without being kept - celebrated abroad and forgotten at home, the islands proud of Hamilton's portrait and Phillips's prizes while the diaspora that produced them is treated as mere departure; and ground, too, when smallness is worn as smallness of ambition, the islands shrinking to fit the sea rather than reaching past it.

## Drives

Lives in [Alexander Hamilton](persona_alexander_hamilton.md), the Nevis-born founder of another nation, and in [Caryl Phillips](persona_caryl_phillips.md), the St. Kitts-born novelist who wrote the islands into the world's letters. It drives Saint Kitts and Nevis to claim its wide sons and its scattered people, and to answer the wide world as an equal for all that it is small.
