# Saint Kitts and Nevis: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md) is written from a Kittitian-Nevisian cultural
profile on Geert Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and
carries no number. Saint Kitts and Nevis is not separately scored in the published
Hofstede data; the profile is read from the regional West Indian and small-island
Caribbean pattern (a relatively close, hierarchical, warm and indulgent culture
with strong in-group ties), recorded here so the basis is on the record without
entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Caribbean / West Indian pattern (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | high                                      |
| Individualism                 | low (collective, strong in-group)         |
| Motivation toward Achievement | moderate                                  |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | moderate                                  |
| Long Term Orientation         | low (short-term, tradition-honoring)      |
| Indulgence                    | high                                      |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the six-dimensions model of national
culture and the West Africa / Caribbean regional profiles). Saint Kitts and Nevis
has no separate published country score; the values above are the approximate
regional pattern and are read soberly, never claimed as a measured national score.

The single defining archetype, [the sugar worker](persona_the_sugar_worker.md), is
not a historical individual but a composite: the enslaved and then freed hand of
the cane who carries the whole monoculture, the gap between freedom in law and
freedom in the field, and the ancestral African tongues carried across the water
from slavery and lost in the generations of the cane.
