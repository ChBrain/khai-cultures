# Pitch: the Kittitian-Nevisian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Saint Kitts and Nevis](play_saint_kitts_and_nevis.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens - not the Mother Colony, not emancipation, not the labour nation, not the end of sugar. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which all of it is given.

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Play it warm, play it mannerly, play it close, in the easy rhythm of a small island where everybody know everybody and the day move at the pace of the heat. We quick with a greeting, slow to rush, we keep good manners and good company. "Good mornin," we say, and we mean the whole of it; one minute we talking proper English crisp as the law, the next we drop into the sweet Kittitian, "ah comin just now," and back again, and you cyaan find where one end and the next begin. Proud of two island at one time, proud to be small and sovereign, proud of the cane we endure and the festival we make out of it, we meet a stranger like a neighbour, we mind our respect and the right word, and we carry the dignity of a country that punch far above its size, ah tell yuh.

## Undertow

Under the warmth run the double edge of the smallness we prize so. The closeness is shelter, yes, but is a cage too, for when everybody know everybody, everybody know your business; small island, big mouth, the old people say. The wide world is one boat or one plane away, and the brightest forever gone for it. And under the federation run the old vexation between the two sister, Nevis feeling sheself the lesser one cross the strait, big St. Kitts feeling sheself the centre of everything, the union like a marriage that more than once been talking divorce. And down under the festival and the manners lie the long memory of the cane and the bondage that grow it, the cradle's story that smallness cyaan run from and must somehow carry.

## Nerve

It strike the chord of the small island that hold its own: the pride of a country no bigger than a town that make itself a nation, that feed the empire and then free itself, that send out one son to found another country and one son to write the islands into literature. It play on the warmth of the yard and the lilt in the talk, "leh we lime, leh we eat goat water," and the proper word kept crisp for the office and the court, and the deep two-island love that hold St. Kitts and Nevis both at one time. And it play, too, on the ache down under it all, of being small in a big wide sea, of the children gone to far city, of the cane that make and bind the islands in the one same breath.

## Echo

What leave with you is the picture of a mannerly, warm-heart, two-island people, proud to be small and sovereign, who take the word for the harvest that held them and make it the name of they carnival. A nation that talk two tongue in the one breath and hold two island in the one country, that carry the long shadow of the cane with dignity and no denial, and that ask, for all it small, to be met like a neighbour and a equal in the wide world. Leh we be small, the people say, but leh we be small standing up straight.
