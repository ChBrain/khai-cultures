# Position: the Mother Colony and the sugar cradle

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The standing of St. Kitts as the first English colony in the whole Caribbean, planted in 1624 and made the seed-bed from which the English settled Antigua, Montserrat, Barbuda and beyond, so the island earned its name as the Mother Colony of the West Indies. Above the cane rose the great fortress on Brimstone Hill, the Gibraltar of the West Indies, guarding the prize below. And the prize was sugar: a near-total monoculture, cane planted to the very waterline, worked under a slavery as intense as any in the islands. It is a position founded in contradiction, the cradle of a Caribbean built by the English and built on bondage, the mother of colonies and the mother of an unfree labour, the gap between the wealth it made and the cost it exacted present from the first furrow.

## Orders

That these small islands are not a footnote but a foundation, the place where the English Caribbean began and from which it spread; that the great fort and the ruined mills are a heritage to be kept and not erased; and that the cane and the bondage that grew it are to be carried soberly, the cradle owning its history rather than hiding it, holding the wealth and the wound in the same hand.

## Loses

Ground when the cradle is told as glory alone - the proud first colony, the mighty fort, the rich estates - with the slavery that made it all unspoken; and ground, too, when the islands are dismissed as merely small, a speck in the sea, forgetting that this speck was the mother of a whole colonial Caribbean and bore the weight of its founding.

## Drives

Lives in [the sugar worker](persona_the_sugar_worker.md), the enslaved and then freed hand of the cane in whom the whole monoculture is embodied, the one who bore the cradle's history in the flesh and outlasted it. It drives Saint Kitts and Nevis to keep the memory of the Mother Colony whole - the first colony, the great fort, and the bondage in the field - and to honor the worker who carried it.
