# Mauritania: a khai culture

Mauritania staged as a full khai play, ISO MR, language ar. The desert bridge of
the caravan and the manuscript, of tea and the tent, where the Maghreb meets the
river south, as a theatre: its history as the plot line, its people as the cast,
the roles its languages and learning encode, its places, its practices, and its
defining objects, all tuned by a single pitch.

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the house that holds
this production and answers for it. This culture logs the house as its Estate; a
culture with no Estate is not yet a production.

## The production

- **Anchor:** [play_mauritania.md](play_mauritania.md), the culture itself.
- **Pitch:** [pitch_ar.md](pitch_ar.md), the tone the whole play is performed in,
  written from Mauritania's own bearing (an unhurried patience and gravity, a
  quiet desert dignity that does not abase itself, a wealth measured in
  hospitality and learning rather than money, and a steady care for memory against
  the encroaching sand) and naming nothing it does not change.
- **Plots (history):** the Almoravids (al-Murabitun) who carried the Book out of
  the desert in the eleventh century, the caravans and the libraries that raised
  Chinguetti and Ouadane into towns of trade and learning, the coming of the Banu
  Hassan and the settling of Hassaniya Arabic, and the rise of the modern state in
  1960 - each casting the elements it needs, and each staging the plurality of the
  land soberly and with dignity.
- **Personas:** Cheikh Sidiyya al-Kabir, the nineteenth-century scholar of the
  Trarza who taught and built a manuscript library; Mohamed El Mokhtar Ould Bah,
  the modern scholar who carried the heritage of the Shanaqita from the wooden
  tablet to the printed book; Dimi Mint Abba, the great voice of Hassani song
  known as the desert's diva; Ousmane Moussa Diagana, the Soninke poet and
  linguist of the river south who gathered his mother tongue and wrote in French;
  and the Tea-Pourer, the archetype of the tent who boils the three rounds of tea
  for whoever alights. Each is wired to the language engine by the
  language-crossing it carries.
- **Positions:** the badia (the nomad inheritance of the Bidhan, the tent, the
  camel, and the Hassani verse, shared too by the people of the river south), the
  mahadra (the desert school without walls, where the Book is kept in the chest
  before the page), and hospitality (the open tent and the tea gathering, one
  dignity for desert and river alike).
- **Places:** Chinguetti and Ouadane (the old caravan towns of stone, their
  libraries of manuscript, Chinguetti called the seventh city of Islam), and the
  Senegal River (the green line of the south, home to the Halpulaar, Soninke, and
  Wolof, the land's other face).
- **Processes:** the three rounds of tea (atay, the ritual of time and talk poured
  with foam), and the date harvest (the guetna, the summer gathering under the
  palms that draws the scattered kin back together).
- **Pieces:** the khaima (the tent of hair and wool that makes a home of any
  ground), and the ardin and the tidinit (the two instruments of Hassani music
  that carry the poetry as sound).
- **Plan:** Keeping the Memory, the standing pledge to guard the manuscripts, the
  Hassaniya tongue and its verse, the languages of the river people, and a single
  shared dignity for all the land's peoples.

## Provenance

The historical figures, the events the plots stage, the places, and the pieces
are drawn from the public record and recorded in [REFERENCES.md](REFERENCES.md).
The Arab-Berber (Bidhan), the Haratin, and the Afro-Mauritanian peoples - the
Halpulaar, the Soninke, and the Wolof - are each given their dignity; the question
of one equal dignity for all the land's peoples is held open soberly and without
taking a side. Sources in the public domain are credited, never claimed; the
staging and the architecture are original work.
