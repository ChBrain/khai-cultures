# Plot: the Archipelago Nation

## Taxonomy

[play_bahamas](play_bahamas.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

A self-governing nation of seven hundred islands with little farmland and few minerals must make its living from what it has: the warm sea, the white sand, the turquoise water, and the sheltered harbours. The world that once sailed past now wants to come ashore.

## Action

The modern Bahamas builds a prosperity on tourism and offshore banking, turning the sea and the sand the brochure sells into the engine of the economy, the cruise ships and the resorts crowding Nassau and the cays. The country wins a place among the better-off of the region, yet the wealth gathers at the capital while the Out Islands thin and the young sail away for work. Through it all the islands keep their own voice: Junkanoo rushes down Bay Street before dawn, the Family Island Regatta races the wooden sloops, and Joseph Spence plays the old rake-and-scrape that owes nothing to the brochure. The nation lives the open question of whether the wealth of the sand can reach every island and the culture of the settlements can outlast the show staged for the visitor.

## Stage

- [Joseph Spence](persona_joseph_spence.md): the folk guitarist whose rake-and-scrape carries the islands' own voice through the age of tourism.
- [the Out Islands](place_the_out_islands.md): the Family Islands of the sea life and the old music, and the test of whether the capital's wealth reaches them.
- [Junkanoo and rake-and-scrape](position_junkanoo_and_rake_and_scrape.md): the African-rooted music and masquerade that keeps the nation's own voice sounding.

## Tension

The sea and sand that make the nation rich are also what the brochure sells, and the same easy paradise the visitor flies to is a working country of real inequality where the wealth pools at Nassau and the Out Island young must leave to find work. The archipelago must hold its scattered islands together and keep its own voice - the Junkanoo, the Regatta, the rake-and-scrape - from thinning into a show for the cruise ship, steering its own course as a small nation that has made the whole world its sea of strangers and its livelihood at once.
