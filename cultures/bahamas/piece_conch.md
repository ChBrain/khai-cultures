# Piece: conch

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On every Bahamian table and along every shore: the great pink-mouthed sea snail of the shallow flats, cracked from its spiralled shell and eaten cracked, scorched, in fritters, in salad, and in chowder, the national staple food of the islands. The shells heaped in pink mounds at the fish fry and the settlement, blown as a horn, and built into the very walls of the old conch houses. The creature, the meal, and the emblem of the sea-people all in one.

## Load Bearing

It stands for [the sea and the isles](position_the_sea_and_the_isles.md) made food and identity: the harvest of the flats that fed the islands when there was little else, the proof that the sea is the larder and the Bahamian a sea-person to the bone. So central is it that the people themselves are called Conchs, the shell raised over a settlement marking a Bahamian home, and a meal without it scarcely a Bahamian meal. The diving for it, the cracking of it, the eating of it raw with lime and pepper on the dock - this is the islands tasting of themselves. Without the conch the sea-nation would have no dish to call its body.

## Apparent

A large marine snail in a heavy pink-lipped spiral shell, the muscular foot inside cut out and cleaned, white and firm, eaten raw or cooked; the emptied shell a pink trumpet and a doorstep ornament. In itself only a sea-snail of the warm flats; its weight is in the centuries it fed the islands and the people who took its name for their own.

## Yearbook

- Harvested from the shallow flats by free-diving since the Lucayans, the oldest food of these islands.
- Made the national staple, eaten cracked, scorched, and in chowder, and the reason Bahamians are called Conchs.
- Now fished so hard that the beds thin, the staple of the sea-nation pressed against the limits of the sea that gives it.
