# Pitch: the Bahamian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [the Bahamas](play_bahamas.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens - not the First Landfall, not the pirates and the Loyalists, not the road to majority rule, not the archipelago nation. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which all of it is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played warm, easygoing, and unhurried, in the long rhythm of the sea: a people who take their time, greet a stranger like a cousin, and would rather talk story on the dock than rush. Sociable and quick to laugh, fond of a name turned into a nickname and a hard truth softened into a joke, they carry the lilt of the Bahamian dialect, the dropped h and the sweet "eh" at the end of a question, and they hold the family, the church, and the settlement close. Time runs on island time, "soon come" meaning when it comes; the visitor is welcomed, the conversation savoured, and the day measured by the tide and the heat rather than the clock. Under the ease runs a deep pride in the islands and a sailor's patience with whatever the sea brings.

## Undertow

Under the easy warmth runs the gap the brochure papers over: the tourist's paradise of white sand and turquoise water is also a working country of real inequality, and the smile turned to the visitor can tire of being the welcome a living depends on. There is the long distance between Nassau, where the money and the people gather, and the Out Islands left quiet and thinning as the young sail away; the dependence on a sea of strangers for the bread; and the ease that, pressed, can shade into a fatalism, a "what to do" that lets the hard thing slide rather than face it down.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of the welcome and the sea: the conviction that there is always time, that a guest is a blessing, that trouble passes like weather and the island abides. It plays on the pride of a people who turned a poor scatter of cays into a nation the world sails to, on the sweetness of the home settlement and the home tongue, and on the deep Bahamian faith that the sea provides. It plays, too, on the ache underneath - the cost of being everyone's paradise, and the quiet of the Family Island the tide of the world has passed by.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a sea-people forever between the welcome and the wave: warm, unhurried, sociable, and proud, holding the family and the settlement close while the world washes through. A nation that runs on the rhythm of the tide and the open hand, and that, for all the distance between the brochure and the cay, believes the sea will provide and the islands will hold.
