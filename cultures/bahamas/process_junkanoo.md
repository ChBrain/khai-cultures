# Process: Junkanoo

## Taxonomy

Parent group: processes

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Initiated by

The groups and the settlements in the dark before dawn on Boxing Day and again on New Year's, the costumes built in secret for months out of cardboard and crepe paper, the goombay drums tuned over a fire, the cowbells and the brass and the whistles gathered. It takes a whole community sewing and pasting through the year and then pouring into Bay Street in the cold dark, and the rush begins as the city sleeps and breaks into thunder at first light.

## Direction

Toward joy, identity, and release: the masquerade rush that turns the few days of liberty the enslaved were once given at Christmas into the nation showing itself to itself in colour and noise. It is the Bahamas at its loudest and most its own, the African root of the people pounding down the colonial street, a competition of groups and a communal ecstasy at once, the year's burdens danced off in a roar of bells and drums as the sun comes up over Nassau.

## Lever

It is driven by the deep need of a people descended from the enslaved to claim a voice and an art entirely their own, made from nothing but cardboard, paper, and rhythm. It holds against the long year of secret labour and cost, and against the pull to perform it for the cruise ship rather than live it; against it stand the temptation to sell the rush as a poolside show and the strain of keeping the settlements' groups alive as the young drift to the imported sound.

## Echo

Junkanoo makes audible what [Junkanoo and rake-and-scrape](position_junkanoo_and_rake_and_scrape.md) holds: that the islands have a voice all their own, made by the descendants of the enslaved out of African roots and island scraps. Into this rush [Joseph Spence](persona_joseph_spence.md) and the settlements pour the same home music, the nation speaking in its own tongue before the sun is up.
