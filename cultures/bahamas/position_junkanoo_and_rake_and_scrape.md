# Position: Junkanoo and rake-and-scrape

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The proposition that the soul of the Bahamas is its African-rooted music and masquerade: the goombay drum and the cowbell, the brass and the whistle of Junkanoo rushing down Bay Street before dawn, and the carpenter's saw scraped under a guitar and a goatskin drum that makes rake-and-scrape. Carried over in the memory and the bodies of the enslaved and kept alive in the settlements, it is the art the islands made out of what they were given the least of: a few days of liberty at Christmas turned into a roaring parade of crepe-paper costumes, and the everyday tools of work turned into instruments. It is the Bahamas speaking in its own voice, the part of the culture that owes nothing to the brochure and everything to the people.

## Orders

That the islands have a music and a masquerade entirely their own, made by the descendants of the enslaved out of African roots and island materials; that the rush of Junkanoo and the scrape of the saw are the nation showing itself to itself, not a show staged for the visitor; and that this art is to be carried on by the young and the settlements, costume sewn and drum tuned each year, lest the voice thin into a souvenir.

## Loses

Ground when the music is sold rather than lived - when Junkanoo becomes a poolside performance for the cruise ship and rake-and-scrape a quaint act for the hotel, the rush emptied of its meaning and the saw silenced in the settlements where it was born; and ground when the young trade the home music for the imported sound and let the old players take the tunes with them when they go.

## Drives

Lives in the legendary folk guitarist whose rake-and-scrape and ragged hymns carried the island music to the world without ever leaving the islands behind. It drives the Bahamas to keep its own voice sounding, in the costume and the cowbell and the scraped saw, against the pull of the borrowed tune.
