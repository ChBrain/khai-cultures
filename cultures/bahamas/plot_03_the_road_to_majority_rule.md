# Plot: the Road to Majority Rule

## Taxonomy

[play_bahamas](play_bahamas.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

After emancipation the Afro-Bahamian majority works the sea and the reef, but the colony's money and power stay in the hands of a small white merchant class on Bay Street, and the franchise is kept narrow. By the middle of the twentieth century the demand for a voice and a vote can no longer be held back.

## Action

The road from colony to nation is climbed by the ballot and the union hall, not the gun. Milo Butler shouts the working Bahamian's case for the franchise and dignity year after year on the floor of the assembly; Doris Johnson leads the women's suffrage movement and wins Bahamian women the vote, making the majority whole. Lynden Pindling gathers the Afro-Bahamian majority into a political force and leads it to majority rule in 1967, ending the long dominance of the merchant class, and on to independence in 1973, when the Bahamas takes its own flag and governs itself for the first time. A slaveholding crown colony becomes a self-ruling nation, led by the descendants of the enslaved.

## Stage

- [Lynden Pindling](persona_lynden_pindling.md): the father of the nation who leads the majority to majority rule and independence.
- [Milo Butler](persona_milo_butler.md): the labour and rights pioneer who fights for the working man's vote and becomes the first Bahamian Governor-General.
- [Doris Johnson](persona_doris_johnson.md): the leader of the women's suffrage movement who wins the vote for half the nation.
- [Nassau](place_nassau.md): Bay Street and the assembly where the franchise is fought for and won.

## Tension

The freedom the majority climbs to win is real and lawfully gained, yet the harder work begins where the celebration ends: independence hands a scattered nation to its own people, but the wealth that pooled where the old merchant money pooled does not redistribute by itself, and the new government at the capital can forget the far settlements that voted it in. The road from colony to nation closes one great gap and opens another, between the self-rule won and the fair share of it that must still reach every island.
