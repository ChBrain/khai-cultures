# Persona: Doris Johnson

## Taxonomy

[colony to nation](position_colony_to_nation.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Dame Doris Johnson, the teacher and scholar who led the Bahamian women's suffrage movement and carried the petition for the women's vote to the colonial assembly, winning the franchise for half the nation and rising to become the first woman to sit in and to preside over the Senate. Learned, eloquent, resolute, and dignified, she made the case that majority rule was not whole while half the people could not vote. English she has whole and commanding: she [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [writes](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) in her mother tongue with a teacher's exactness and a scholar's depth, the educated English that let her stand before the assembly and the world and not be dismissed. The [formal rhetoric of the colonial assembly](../united_kingdom/position_language_en_gb.md) and the petition she [carries as her own](process_speaking_carried.md), having [read deeply](process_reading_followed.md) in the cause of the vote here and abroad, and shaped the women's demand into argument the chamber had to answer. She made the women's case in the polished English of the lectern that no rule could rule out of order, while beneath it lay the [Bahamian dialect](position_language_en_bs.md) of the island women she spoke for and, behind that, the African tongues their enslaved foremothers had carried over and lost - so the gap at the limit of the tongue is the distance between the schooled English that won the vote and the home speech of the women whose silence it ended.

## Action

In [the Road to Majority Rule](plot_03_the_road_to_majority_rule.md) she completes the road from [colony to nation](position_colony_to_nation.md), leading the suffrage movement that wins Bahamian women the vote and making the majority a whole majority. By carrying the women's petition into the chamber, she turns self-rule from the rule of half a people into the rule of all of them.

## Shadow

The reformer who wins the vote carries its shadow: the danger that the franchise once granted is counted as the victory complete, the women enfranchised on paper but the chamber and the high office still kept by the men, and the harder equality - of seat, of voice, of pay - left for the daughters to go on winning after the law is changed.

## Tell

She stands before an assembly of men and makes the unanswerable case that their majority rule is no majority at all while their mothers, wives, and daughters cannot vote - and wins, opening the ballot to half the nation by argument alone.
