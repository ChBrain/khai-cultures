# Piece: the Bahamian sloop

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

Drawn up on the sand of every settlement and racing in the Regatta: the wooden working sailboat of the islands, single-masted, shallow-drafted, and built by hand to slip across the thin water of the banks. The boat of the sponger and the fisherman and the wrecker, the craft that carried people, goods, and mail from cay to cay before the ferry and the plane, and the proud racer of the Family Island Regatta. The islands' true vessel, on the water and hauled up under the casuarinas alike.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the sea as road and livelihood, the means by which a scattered nation held itself together over the water. In an archipelago with little land and much sea, the sloop was the highway, the workplace, and the link: it took the sponger to the beds, the wrecker to the reef, the family to the next settlement, and the mail and the goods around the islands. Shallow-drafted to cross the banks where deeper boats ran aground, built by island hands from island knowledge, it is the craft that made the sea passable and the archipelago one. Without the sloop the seven hundred islands would have been seven hundred prisons.

## Apparent

A small single-masted wooden sailboat, gaff- or sloop-rigged, shallow in the keel and broad in the beam, built plank by plank in the settlements and worked under sail alone. Plain and weathered on the beach; its weight is in the centuries of sponging, wrecking, fishing, and inter-island travel it carried, and in the Regatta where the old working boat is raced for the islands' pride.
