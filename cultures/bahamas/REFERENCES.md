# the Bahamas: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md) is written from the Bahamas' regional and
cultural profile on Geert Hofstede's model of national culture and names no
dimension and carries no number. The Bahamas is not separately scored in the
core published data; the pitch is drawn from the broader Caribbean and West
African-rooted profile (low individualism and a strong family and community
orientation, a relaxed power distance, and an indulgent, present-focused ease).
The framing is recorded here so the basis is on the record without entering the
prose.

| Dimension                     | Bahamas / Caribbean profile (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | moderate to high                      |
| Individualism                 | low (family- and community-oriented)  |
| Motivation toward Achievement | moderate                              |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | low to moderate                       |
| Long Term Orientation         | low (present-focused)                 |
| Indulgence                    | high                                  |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the six-dimensions model of national
culture) and the West African and Caribbean regional profiles. The Bahamas is not
individually scored in the core published data; the values above are an
approximate regional framing, as published by Hofstede Insights / The Culture
Factor Group, used only to set the tone.

## Historical sources

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public historical record. They are credited here and never claimed; the
staging is original work. The harder parts of the story - the disappearance of the
Lucayan Taino after 1492, slavery, and the Loyalist plantations - are staged
soberly on the public record as the gap between the promise the islands offered and
the reality they lived.

The one defining persona, [the wrecker](persona_the_wrecker.md), is an archetype,
not a historical individual: a composite that carries the islands' sea-craft, the
sponger and salvager who reads the reef and lives off the water and the ships it
tears open.
