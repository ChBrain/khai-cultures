# Bhutan: a khai culture

Bhutan staged as a full khai play, ISO BT, language dzo. The Land of the Thunder
Dragon, high in the eastern Himalaya, as a theatre: its history as the plot line,
its people as the cast, the roles its faith and language encode, its places, its
practices, and its defining objects, all tuned by a single pitch.

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the Cultures house
that holds this culture and answers for it. This culture logs the house as its
Estate; a culture with no Estate is not yet a production.

## The production

- **Anchor:** [play_bhutan.md](play_bhutan.md), the culture itself.
- **Pitch:** [pitch_dzo.md](pitch_dzo.md), the Hofstede layer, written from
  Bhutan's profile (a deep respect for hierarchy and elders, a collective and
  community-bound life, modesty and care over competition, and a faith-borne ease
  with the impermanent) and naming no dimension.
- **Plots (history):** the dharma comes to the land (8th-15th century), the
  Zhabdrung unifies the land (17th century), the people choose a king (1907), and
  happiness and the constitution (1972-2008) - each casting the elements it needs,
  and each staging the move to constitutional democracy soberly and with dignity.
- **Personas:** Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava), Pema Lingpa, the Zhabdrung Ngawang
  Namgyal, and Gongsa Ugyen Wangchuck as the historic cast, plus one defining
  archetype, the monk (the young novice raised in the dzong's monastic body). Each
  is wired to the language engine by the language-crossing it holds.
- **Positions:** the Vajrayana dharma (the faith that saturates all of life),
  Gross National Happiness (the measure of a good life beyond wealth), the guarded
  land (the forests, peaks, and customs the nation keeps whole).
- **Places:** Taktsang (the Tiger's Nest above Paro, where Guru Rinpoche flew),
  the Punakha Dzong (where the nation was unified and the first king crowned).
- **Processes:** the tshechu (the masked cham dances and the festival that gathers
  the valley), archery (datse, the national sport that binds the community).
- **Pieces:** the dzong (the fortress-monastery that holds both prayer and rule),
  the prayer flag (the five-colored cloth that looses its prayers on the wind).

## Provenance

The Hofstede scores behind the pitch and the public-domain historical sources are
recorded in [REFERENCES.md](REFERENCES.md). The data never enters the prose; the
pitch is its invisible hand. Sources in the public domain are credited, never
claimed; the monarchy and the Buddhist faith are treated respectfully as the
enduring heart of the nation, and the move to constitutional democracy is staged
soberly on the public record; the staging and the architecture are original work.
