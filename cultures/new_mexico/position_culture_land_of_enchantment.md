# Position: the Land of Enchantment

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The inheritance of a high desert where the oldest continuously inhabited communities in the country sit within a day's drive of some of the newest physics ever done: [pueblo heritage](position_pueblo_heritage.md), the adobe architecture, communal land rights, and pottery craft handed down since before any Spanish or American border crossed this ground, and [desert science](position_desert_science.md), the laboratory culture that split the atom at Trinity and still runs the nuclear and aerospace research at Los Alamos. Its people speak [New Mexico English](position_language_en_us_nm.md), an English shot through with Spanish and older Pueblo place names for a landscape no single language names alone. It carries a triple layering of Pueblo, Spanish colonial, and Anglo-American history that most of the country experiences only two-deep, and a red-rock, high-desert light that outsiders reach for postcards to describe and locals simply call home.

## Orders

That the old ways, the pottery firing, the adobe upkeep, the feast day, be kept and taught rather than performed for visitors; that the laboratory's precision, kept to its telemetry logs and its safety audits, be held to the same seriousness as the potter's kiln. It orders a working coexistence of three inherited layers, Pueblo, Spanish, and Anglo, none permitted to simply overwrite the other two, and a stewardship of the Rio Grande's scarce water that mining, ranching, and pueblo alike depend on. It orders a wary pride in being first, first revolt, first bomb, worn without either boasting or apology.

## Loses

Ground where the fast, telemetry-driven tempo of the national laboratories treats water conservation and tribal land questions as regional footnotes rather than the deciding constraint they actually are. It loses ground in the still-unhealed weight of Los Alamos and Trinity, a state that gave the world its first atomic weapon and carries the fallout, literal and moral, of having done so. And it loses ground to the folklore that outside attention prefers, the alien crash story eclipsing the harder, longer history of the Pueblo Revolt and four centuries of land dispute that the state actually lives with.

## Drives

It drives New Mexicans to hold the ancient and the ultramodern as one continuous inheritance rather than a contradiction, the same red earth that fired Pueblo pottery for a thousand years now hosting solar arrays and physics labs. It drives the state to keep telling its history in the order it actually happened, the Revolt before the Test, the acequia before the accelerator. And it shapes those raised in it toward a patient, water-conscious pride, quick to defend the desert's scarce resources and quicker still to insist that enchantment is not a marketing word here but a description of a land three cultures have each, in turn, learned to survive.
