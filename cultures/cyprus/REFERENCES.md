# Cyprus: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_el.md](pitch_el.md) is written from Cyprus's profile on Geert
Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no
number. The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the
record without entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Cyprus score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 65                     |
| Individualism                 | 35                     |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 50                     |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 100                    |
| Long Term Orientation         | 45                     |
| Indulgence                    | 70                     |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published by
Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group. Published data for Cyprus is limited
and less complete than for many countries, so these figures should be read as
indicative; Cyprus's Uncertainty Avoidance is notably high in the published data.

The 1974 division is treated soberly and even-handedly on the public record: the
suffering of both communities, the displaced and the missing on both sides, is
honoured, no side is taken, and no atrocity detail is given. The enosis dream is
recorded honestly as having had its tragic part in the tragedy, and the grievances
of both peoples are acknowledged.

The three defining personas, [Androula](persona_androula.md),
[Mehmet](persona_mehmet.md), and [Andreas](persona_andreas.md), are archetypes,
not historical individuals: composites that carry the tension between the
generation displaced in 1974 (Greek-Cypriot and Turkish-Cypriot alike, the
refugees and the families of the missing) and the younger, EU-era generation that
crosses the line as a visitor. Mehmet, the Turkish-Cypriot persona, is written in
Turkish (language tr) as a full member of the cast.
