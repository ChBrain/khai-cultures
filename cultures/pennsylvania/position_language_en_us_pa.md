# Position: Pennsylvania English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Philadelphia keeps a short a system split so fine that "bad" and "bat," or "mad" and "map," can carry different vowels depending only on the sound that follows, a distinction few other American cities make so precisely, and it carries "jawn," a single word that stands in for almost any noun a speaker cannot or will not name. Pittsburgh, to the west, keeps "yinz" as a second person plural pronoun where standard English leaves "you" to do the work of both one listener and many, and it folds "down" and "house" into a single vowel, "dahn," "hahs," where most English speakers keep two sounds moving.

## Orders

Its syntax forces a Pittsburgh speaker into a construction general English does not allow, "the car needs washed," "the dog needs fed," dropping the "to be" a wider English insists on before a past participle. Its western l comes out vocalized, so "steel" edges toward "stiww," a sound shift that marks a speaker before a sentence is done, while Philadelphia's split a system forces the same speaker to choose, without thinking, which of two vowels a short a takes on in almost every word that carries one.

## Loses

The broad Pittsburgh vowels and the folded "dahntahn" thin out under the flattening pull of the national broadcast standard, which trims the "needs washed" construction from younger, more mobile speakers as a mark of uneducated speech rather than a working grammatical rule. Philadelphia's fine short a split is easy to mishear or flatten entirely outside the city, and "jawn," useful precisely because it names nothing in particular, translates into no other English at all once a speaker leaves the block that uses it.

## Drives

Growing up sorting the short a by ear, word by word, trains a Philadelphia speaker to hear a distinction that most of English does not mark at all, while dropping "to be" before a past participle lets a Pittsburgh speaker say in three words what a wider English needs four or five for. So the split state's English shapes two different ears at once: one tuned to a vowel contrast almost nowhere else in the country keeps, the other economical with a verb the rest of English insists on keeping in.
