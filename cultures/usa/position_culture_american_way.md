# Position: the American way

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The American way, what a person born or made American carries without choosing it: [the creed](position_the_creed.md) that the nation rests on an idea rather than blood or soil, [the frontier](position_the_frontier.md) that a person can light out and remake themselves on open ground, and [exceptionalism](position_exceptionalism.md), the conviction of a chosen nation with an errand to the world. It speaks [American English](position_language_en_us.md), the one tongue told to the immigrant and the native-born alike, both handed the same promise in it. It is the confidence that tomorrow beats yesterday, that anyone can make it, and that the country is still, after two hundred and fifty years, being built.

## Orders

That a person be judged by what they make of themselves and not by their birth; that the self-made stand equal to the well-born, and the newcomer equal to the native-born, so long as both answer to the same creed; that authority be questioned and earned rather than simply obeyed; and that the country be measured, again and again, against the promise it wrote down at its founding.

## Loses

Ground when the boundless promise curdles into the belief that the nation can do no wrong, or when its bootstraps myth excuses forgetting what was taken and who was left out to build it; ground, too, when the melting pot that makes a newcomer American also melts away what they brought, the mother tongue and the old country thinning out within a generation; and ground when the restless start-over becomes a refusal to reckon with debts the country has not yet paid.

## Drives

It drives the American to reinvent themselves without asking permission, to hold the country to its own written word, and to believe that the frontier, whether of land or of law, is never quite closed. It drove the planter who wrote a creed he did not live by, the rail-splitter who paid its first instalment in blood, and the preacher who called it a promissory note still due; and it drives the newcomer, arriving today as much as two centuries ago, to become American by taking up the promise as their own.
