# Persona: Abraham Lincoln

## Taxonomy

[exceptionalism](position_exceptionalism.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The rail-splitter from a frontier cabin who taught himself by firelight, pulled himself up by his bootstraps from nothing to the presidency, held the union together through its bloodiest war, freed the enslaved, and was murdered at the very hour of victory. Grave, melancholy, patient, and merciful, he saved the nation and squared up to its original sin, and gave it the words by which it knows itself. English he has whole and remade: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in a plain frontier American he sharpened on the law and the stump. The cadence of the King James Bible he [carries as his own](process_speaking_carried.md), having [read it deeply](process_reading_followed.md) by candlelight along with Shakespeare and Euclid, and from it he forged the nation's scripture-prose. A self-taught man with almost no schooling, he wove the borrowed music of an old English Bible into a plain American tongue and redefined the creed in two hundred and seventy-two words at Gettysburg - a new birth of freedom set down in the speech of a country boy, where the gap at the limit of the tongue is closed not by learning but by sorrow earned in blood.

## Action

In [the Civil War (1861-1865)](plot_03_civil_war.md) he holds the union together and turns the war into the redemption of [the creed](position_the_creed.md), making the nation, in his own words, the last best hope of earth. He takes up the unfinished promise of 1776 and pays its first great instalment in blood, binding emancipation to the very survival of [exceptionalism](position_exceptionalism.md) itself.

## Shadow

The redeemer who saves the union by war carries its shadow: the cost of the cause counted in the dead of both sides, the power a wartime president must seize, and the terrible truth that the creed was bought with blood and the wound only partly healed, leaving the rest of the tab for those who came after.

## Tell

He answers the worst slaughter the country ever knew not with triumph but with charity - "with malice toward none" - and measures the war by whether the nation can have a new birth of freedom, not by who won.
