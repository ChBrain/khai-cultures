## Place

On the miner, the cowboy, the factory worker, and then on everyone, everywhere: sturdy blue denim trousers, riveted at the seams, born as the workwear of the American West and grown into the most-worn garment on earth. From the ranch and the rail yard to the city street and the world's wardrobes.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the frontier turned into a democratic uniform. Made by Levi Strauss to outlast the hard labor of the gold rush, riveted to survive the miner and the cowboy, jeans carried the frontier's grit into the everyday, and then became the garment in which Americans of every class dressed alike - the rich and the poor, the worker and the rebel, all in the same blue cloth. They went out into the world as the country's most democratic export, the easy, classless American freedom worn on the legs of half the planet. Without this denim the frontier myth would have no garment the whole world could put on.

## Apparent

A pair of indigo-dyed cotton twill trousers, copper rivets at the pockets, double-stitched and built to wear in rather than out. Plain and rugged on the rack; their meaning is in the work they were made for and the freedom and ease they came to mean, softening and fading to fit the one who wears them.

## Yearbook

- Patented in 1873 by Levi Strauss and Jacob Davis as riveted workwear for the laboring West.
- Adopted from the ranch and the factory into the everyday dress of every American class.
- Carried abroad as the country's most-worn export, the global emblem of casual American freedom.
