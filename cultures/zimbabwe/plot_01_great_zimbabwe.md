# Plot: the houses of stone

## Cue

On the southern plateau, between roughly the eleventh and the fifteenth centuries, the ancestors of the Shona grow a kingdom rich on cattle and on the gold that moves down to the Indian Ocean coast. With wealth and with mastery, they begin to build, not in mud and pole but in stone, dressing the granite that breaks naturally into even blocks and laying it course on course.

## Action

They raise Great Zimbabwe, dzimba dzemabwe, the houses of stone: the Hill Complex above and, below it, the Great Enclosure with its great curved outer wall and its conical tower, dry-laid granite without a drop of mortar, the largest stone structure in Africa south of the Sahara. Upon its walls they set carved soapstone birds, the figures that would one day rise onto the nation's flag. The city flourishes as the seat of a kingdom and a hub of trade, and then, in its own time, as such seats do, the court moves on and the walls are left to the grass and the lichen, holding their builders' name for the centuries to come.

## Tension

The builders raise a city that will outlast their kingdom and lend its name to a country they could not have imagined, the standing answer to anyone who would doubt the land had builders of its own. Yet the city is left to the grass when its time passes, its purpose half-forgotten and its keeping handed forward to descendants who must choose to tend it, so the glory of the founding and the charge to keep it standing are laid down together in the same granite.
