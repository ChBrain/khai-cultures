# Persona: Yvonne Vera

## Taxonomy

[hunhu](position_hunhu.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The novelist of Bulawayo, a daughter of the western city in the Ndebele heartland, whose dense, lyrical prose gave the inner lives of Zimbabwean women a place in the nation's literature and carried the country's name into the literatures of the world. Fierce, tender, and uncompromising, she wrote of the things the community kept quiet, of women's silence and women's strength, and held the country to its own ideal of care, that a person is a person through other people, women among them. She wrote in [English](position_language_en_zw.md), the bridge tongue, which she [carried as her working voice](process_speaking_carried.md) and [drafted](process_writing_drafted.md) into a prose all her own. Yet she wrote from a Ndebele city of isiNdebele and a country of chiShona, [hearing both around her](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) and [following the speech of her people on the page](process_reading_followed.md), so that her English was charged with the cadences of tongues it did not use. She told a nation's women in a borrowed tongue what their own communities had not let them say aloud, and the silences in her sentences carry as much as the words, the gap at the limit of the tongue made into the very subject of her art, where what cannot be spoken is what most needs to be written.

## Action

In [the taking of the name](plot_03_taking_the_name.md) she stands for the country coming into its own voice and writing itself down, a daughter of Bulawayo making the new nation's literature and insisting that all its people, women and men, Ndebele and Shona alike, belong to the one story. She carries hunhu into letters, holding the community to the care it owes its own.

## Shadow

The hard truths she set down can be honoured as literature and dodged as counsel, the books prized and shelved while the silences they broke close over again. To celebrate the writer and not heed the writing, to award the prose and ignore the wound it names, is to keep the page and lose the reckoning it asks.

## Tell

A daughter of Bulawayo who made the unspoken her subject, she wrote the silences of her people's women into permanent words, and proved that the new nation's voice would speak from every corner of the land, the western city no less than the capital.
