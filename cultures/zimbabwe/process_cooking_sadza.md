# Process: cooking sadza

## Taxonomy

Parent group: processes

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Initiated by

Whoever holds the kitchen, most often the gogo or the mother of the household, at the day's end: the maize meal measured, the pot of water set on the fire, the wooden mugoti stirring-stick in hand. It takes only the meal, the water, the fire, and the strong arm to stir, and a household gathers, for the day is not done and the family is not fed until the sadza is on the plate.

## Direction

Toward the table and the gathering of the household: sadza is the staple of the country, the thick maize porridge stirred stiff over the fire and eaten by hand with relish, the muriwo greens, the meat or the beans, the gravy. To cook it is to feed the family and, in the feeding, to hold it together, the daily rite by which a household sits down as one, hands reaching to the common dish, no one a stranger at the plate, the meal that says you are home.

## Lever

It is driven by the place of the shared meal at the centre of the household and by the open table of hunhu, where another plate is set for whoever arrives, the visitor never sent away hungry. It holds against the hard season and the thin year, sadza the meal that stretches and sustains; against it stand the scattering of families and the long distances that empty the home pot, the one abroad longing most for sadza from a home fire.

## Echo

Cooking sadza makes daily and ordinary what holds the country dearest, the household gathered as one company at the common dish, a person made a person among other people at the simplest table. It is hunhu cooked in a pot, the warmth and the welcome of the people made into a meal, the taste that, more than any other, says to one far away the single word: home.
