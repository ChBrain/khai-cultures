# Persona: Nicholas Mukomberanwa

## Taxonomy

[the houses of stone](position_houses_of_stone.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

One of the great masters of Shona stone sculpture, a hand that drew spirit and family and the old wisdom out of the hard serpentine and springstone of the plateau, and whose work stands in galleries across the world as the country's name carved anew. Disciplined, spiritual, and deeply rooted, he took the abstract, powerful forms of the new Zimbabwean sculpture, the ancestor figures, the brooding heads, the bound families, and made of stone a language the whole world could read. His mother tongue is chiShona, in which he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and which named the spirits and the proverbs his chisel set into rock. English he [carried as the tongue](process_speaking_carried.md) in which the world's galleries spoke of his work, and the meanings he could not put into any spoken word he [wrote instead in stone](process_writing_mother_tongue.md), shaping the rock until it said what speech could not. He worked, as our forebears worked the granite at the stone city, in a tongue without words, letting the stone carry what no language quite holds, so that the silence of his finished figures is where his deepest speech lives, the gap at the limit of the tongue answered by the chisel where the voice falls short.

## Action

In [the stone and the mbira go out](plot_04_stone_and_mbira.md) he carries [the houses of stone](position_houses_of_stone.md) back into the world through his hands, making Zimbabwe a name in stone again as the ancestors made it a name in stone first. He proves anew that the country is a country of builders and carvers, the chisel taking up where the dry-laid granite left off.

## Shadow

The sculpture so prized abroad can be reduced to a curio for the airport stall, the deep spirit-figures of the masters crowded by quick copies cut for quick sale, the language of stone flattened into a souvenir. To buy the shape and miss the spirit, to praise the export and starve the carver, is to keep the stone and lose what the hand put into it.

## Tell

He looks at a raw block of springstone the way the old builders looked at granite and sees the ancestor waiting inside it, then frees it with the chisel, so that the country's oldest art, the working of its stone, lives on in a thoroughly modern hand.
