# Zimbabwe: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain cultural and historical
sources behind the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md) is written from Zimbabwe's profile on Geert
Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no number.
The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the record without
entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Zimbabwe score (approx., regional estimate) |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 65                                          |
| Individualism                 | 20                                          |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 40                                          |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 50                                          |
| Long Term Orientation         | 35                                          |
| Indulgence                    | 35                                          |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the six-dimensions model of national
culture). Zimbabwe is not always listed individually in the published data; the
figures above are approximate, drawn from the regional (East and Southern Africa)
estimates and the published model, and are used only to set the register of the
pitch. The low individualism and the respect for age and authority that mark the
region's profile tune the pitch toward communalism and hunhu.

from the public cultural and historical record. They are credited here and never
claimed; the staging is original work. The nation is staged from within its own
self-understanding, soberly and with dignity, through its heritage, its arts, and
its peoples held in equal dignity.

The one archetype persona, [Sekuru](persona_sekuru.md), is not a historical
individual but a composite: the old mbira player and grandfather who keeps the
spirit songs and passes the ancestral voice, hand to hand, to the young. Thomas
Mapfumo, named in passing as a master of chimurenga music, is credited on the
public record and never claimed.
