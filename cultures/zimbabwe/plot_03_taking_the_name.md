# Plot: the taking of the name

## Cue

By the later twentieth century the country, long known to the wider world under a borrowed name, comes into its own. As it does, it faces the most telling question a nation can answer: not only how it will be governed, but what it will call itself, by what word it will be known to itself and to the world.

## Action

In 1980 the country takes its own name, and reaches not for anything new or imported but for the oldest and deepest word the land holds: Zimbabwe, after dzimba dzemabwe, the houses of stone, naming the whole nation for the granite city its forebears built. It lifts the soapstone bird from the ancient walls onto its flag and its coat of arms, so that the emblem of the state is the emblem of the builders. By this choice the nation declares its descent in a single word, binding itself to the stone city every time it says who it is, and gathering all its peoples, Shona and Ndebele and the rest, under the one inherited name.

## Tension

The nation reaches past every borrowed word to name itself for the achievement of its ancestors, a choice of pride and of memory that roots the new country in the oldest proof of its builders. Yet to take the builders' name is to take the builders' charge: a country named for the houses of stone is measured by whether it keeps them standing and keeps faith, under that one name, with all the peoples who share the land.
