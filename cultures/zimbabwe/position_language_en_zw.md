# Position: Zimbabwean English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

English in Zimbabwe holds, for speakers crossing between chiShona and isiNdebele, a grammar without noun classes and without click consonants, in place of the roughly twenty noun classes that reorganize a chiShona sentence's prefixes and the distinct click sounds, dental, lateral, and palatal, that isiNdebele inherited from its Nguni and Khoisan contact and that no letter of the English alphabet can represent. It offers a single fixed word order and the definite article as tools for marking what is known or new, in place of the class-marking and tone that carry those distinctions in the country's own languages.

## Orders

English forces one uniform pattern of subject-verb agreement onto nouns that, in chiShona, would each carry their own class prefix reshaping the whole sentence around them. And it forces every click-bearing isiNdebele word that crosses into it to lose its click altogether; there is no way to hold a click, dental, lateral, or palatal, inside an English sentence, so the sound is dropped or crudely approximated, never carried across whole.

## Loses

The bridge tongue loses noun-class concord outright, chiShona's system of agreeing prefixes that marks, across an entire clause, what kind of thing is being spoken of. It loses tone, since neither chiShona's nor isiNdebele's pitch distinctions survive the crossing into a non-tonal language, so words that differ only in tone at home sound identical in English. And it loses the click consonant entirely, a sound isiNdebele can make and English structurally cannot, so any word carrying one is stripped of it the moment it is said in the bridge tongue.

## Drives

It drives whoever moves between the two home languages and English to feel each crossing as a reduction, tone gone, class agreement gone, and for isiNdebele speakers, the click itself gone, rather than as a neutral translation. And it drives the explicit marking of definiteness with an article, in place of the class and tone that do that work at home, a substitution speakers make consciously rather than by inherited reflex. The one bridge tongue shapes a country practiced at losing three specific things, tone, class, click, every time it crosses from home speech into the shared one.
