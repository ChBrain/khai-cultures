# Pitch: the Zimbabwean voice

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Zimbabwe](play_zimbabwe.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the houses of stone, not the gathering of the peoples, not the taking of the name, not the going out of the stone and the mbira. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played warm, patient, and held together: a people who reckon a person is a person through other people, who put the family and the dare, the extended household, above the lone self, and who carry the elders and the ancestors in the room when any matter of weight is settled. We greet properly before we get to business, ask after your people before your work, and hold our grief and our joy in common, the whole community sitting with the bereaved and dancing at the wedding. We respect age and office, defer to the chief and the sekuru and the gogo, and yet temper that respect with hunhu, the knowing that authority is held to account by how it carries others. Under the easy manner runs a deep endurance, a long view shaped by hard seasons, and a quiet pride that we are the children of the builders, the people of the stone.

## Undertow

Under the warmth runs the strain of holding so much in common: the weight of obligation to the extended family, the cost a person carries who must answer for many, and the ache of those who go far to make a living and leave the household behind. There is a patience that can shade into too long a forbearance, a respect for age and office that can sit heavy on the young, and a longing of those scattered abroad for the smell of the rains on dry ground and the taste of sadza from a home pot. And under the pride in the stone runs the sober knowledge of seasons that tried the country hard, borne with dignity and not paraded.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of hunhu and the unbroken line: the conviction that we belong to one another, living and dead, that no one stands alone, and that the ancestors walk with the living and answer when the mbira calls them. It plays on the pride of the houses of stone, the proof in granite that our forebears built greatly; on the voice of the mbira dzavadzimu that gathers the family and the spirits at the bira; and on the deep welcome of a people who set another plate without being asked. It plays, too, on the endurance underneath, the long patience of a country that has weathered much and kept its dignity and its song.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people held together by one another and by the stone: warm, patient, communal, and proud of the builders they descend from. A nation that runs on the faith that a person is a person through other people, that the ancestors are not done with us, and that whatever the season brings, what was laid stone upon stone without mortar was laid to hold.
