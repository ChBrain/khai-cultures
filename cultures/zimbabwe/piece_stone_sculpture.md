# Piece: Shona stone sculpture

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

## Place

In the galleries of the world and the sculpture gardens of the plateau, on the museum plinth and at the roadside stall: the carved figures of serpentine and springstone, the brooding ancestor-heads, the bound families, the spirit-figures drawn out of the country's own rock. Born on the plateau in the modern age and grown into one of Africa's most celebrated art movements, the stone of Zimbabwe shaped by Zimbabwean hands and standing across the earth.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the country as a country of carvers, the working of stone running unbroken from the ancient builders to the living artist. Drawn from the hard serpentine and springstone of the plateau, the sculpture takes the abstract, powerful forms of spirit and family and the old wisdom and makes of stone a language the whole world can read, carrying the country's name into the world's galleries as the soapstone birds once stood upon the ancient walls. It is the oldest art of the land, the shaping of its rock, made wholly new. Without this stone the country's name in art would rest on the ruins alone, and not on the living hands that carry the carving forward.

## Apparent

A worked block of serpentine or springstone, polished to a deep glow on the figure and left rough on the ground, a head or a family or a spirit drawn out of the rock by the chisel. Plain stone on the plinth; its whole weight is in the spirit the carver freed from it and the unbroken line it draws from the builders of the houses of stone to the artist of today.
