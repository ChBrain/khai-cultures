# Piece: the Zimbabwe Bird

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On the flag, on the coat of arms, on the banknote and the badge and the gate: the soapstone bird, hwange, carved by the builders of the stone city and set upon its walls, lifted from Great Zimbabwe to become the emblem of the whole nation, the figure a Zimbabwean meets a hundred times a day without thinking, the country's face to itself and to the world.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the descent of the nation from the builders, the single sign that links the modern state to the ancient stone city it is named for. Carved in soapstone by the hands that raised the houses of stone, the bird is the thread that runs from dzimba dzemabwe to the flag flying over the capital, the proof carried on the very emblem of the state that the land had builders and artists of its own. It belongs to all the peoples of the country in common, Shona and Ndebele alike, the shared sign of a shared name. Without this bird the nation named for the stone city would have no face to carry the builders' memory into every hand.

## Apparent

A carved figure of a bird upon a column, worked in the soft grey-green soapstone of the plateau, stylised, neither wholly eagle nor wholly any living bird, perched still upon its pedestal. In itself a piece of carved stone the size of a forearm; its whole weight is in the unbroken line it draws from the ancient walls to the living nation, and in the hands of the long-dead carver who first freed it from the rock.
