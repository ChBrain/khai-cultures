# Play: Saint Lucia

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the house that holds this staging and answers for it.

## Name

Saint Lucia, a small volcanic island of the eastern Caribbean, in the Windward Islands of the Lesser Antilles, between Martinique to the north and Saint Vincent to the south, an independent state within the Commonwealth. ISO LC, language en in law, with Kweyol, the French-based Creole, spoken in the yard and the market. An island named for a saint, peopled by Kalinago, French, and African, and given a French-British-Kweyol soul that no flag ever quite settled.

## Arc

A small island carrying an outsized story. Before the Europeans the Kalinago held it; then France and Britain fought over it across two centuries until it changed hands fourteen times, the most contested island of the Caribbean, called the Helen of the West Indies for the war waged over her beauty. From that contest came a doubled inheritance: English in law and school, the Roman Catholic faith brought by France, and Kweyol, the French Creole the African majority forged on the land, the tongue of the home and the heart. The island grew its wealth first in sugar and then in bananas, the green gold of the river valleys, a single crop carrying a whole economy on its back. And then, astonishingly, from a population that never reached two hundred thousand came two Nobel laureates: Sir Arthur Lewis, who won the prize in Economics for his work on the growth of poor nations, and Derek Walcott, who won it in Literature for an epic poetry that made the island and its sea into myth. Over it all rise the Pitons, the twin volcanic peaks lifting straight from the sea at Soufriere, the face the island shows the world. Independence came in 1979, and with it a people learning to value the Kweyol soul it had once been schooled to be ashamed of, celebrated each October in Jounen Kweyol and through the year in the flower festivals of La Rose and La Marguerite. Under it all runs one nerve: a gentle, devout, lyrical people who know they are small and have proved they are not, holding their Creole heart against the pull of the wider world.
