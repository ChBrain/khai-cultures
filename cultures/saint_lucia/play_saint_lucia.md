# Play: Saint Lucia

## Name

Sa ka fet, this is Saint Lucia: a small volcanic island of the eastern Caribbean, in the Windward Islands of the Lesser Antilles, sitting between Martinique to the north and Saint Vincent to the south, an independent state within the Commonwealth. ISO LC, language en in law, but is Kweyol, the French-based Creole, that they talking in the yard and bargaining in the market. An island they name for a saint, a people make of Kalinago, French, and African, carrying a French-British-Kweyol soul that no flag ever quite settle.

## Arc

A small island carrying a story bigger than itself. Before the Europeans the Kalinago held it; then France and Britain fall to fighting over it across two centuries until it change hands fourteen times, the most contested island in all the Caribbean, that they call the Helen of the West Indies for the war they wage over her beauty. From that contest come a doubled inheritance: English in the law and the school, the Roman Catholic faith France bring, and Kweyol, the French Creole the African majority forge on the land, the tongue of the home and the heart. The island grow its wealth first in sugar and then in bananas, the green gold of the river valleys, one single crop carrying a whole economy on its back. And then, oui, hear this wonder: from a people that never reach two hundred thousand come two Nobel laureates, Sir Arthur Lewis, who take the prize in Economics for his work on how poor nations grow, and Derek Walcott, who take it in Literature for an epic poetry that turn the island and its sea into myth. Two laureates from one speck in the sea, and the island will never let you forget it. Over it all the Pitons rise, the twin volcanic peaks lifting straight out the sea at Soufriere, the face the island show the world. Independence come in 1979, and with it a people learning to love the Kweyol soul they had once been schooled to shame, kept each October in Jounen Kweyol and through the year in the flower festivals of La Rose and La Marguerite. Under it all run one nerve: a gentle, devout, sweet-mouthed people who know they small and have proved they not, holding their Creole heart against the pull of the wider world.
