# Plot: the Helen of the West Indies

## Owner

## Cue

Long before the Europeans, the Kalinago hold the island they call Iouanalao, the land of the iguana, living by the sea and the forest beneath the twin peaks. Then the ships of France and Britain come, and two empires fix their eyes on the same small harbor and the same green heights, each seeing a prize worth a war.

## Action

For more than two centuries France and Britain fight over the island, taking it and losing it until it changes hands fourteen times, more often than any other in the Caribbean, until it earns the name the Helen of the West Indies for the war waged over her beauty. The Kalinago who held the island first are pushed aside in the long contest. The French plant the land, name it for the saint, and bring the Roman Catholic faith and the French that will become Kweyol; the British take it last and lay over it the English, the law, and the Crown. When the cannon at last fall silent the island is left not with one inheritance but two, the property of neither empire and the heir of both.

## Stage

## Tension

The island is made beautiful enough to fight over and small enough never to choose its own fate, the prize of others' wars rather than the player in its own. The long tug-of-war leaves it a doubled soul, French faith and English law and a Creole tongue forged beneath both, an inheritance richer than any single empire could have given and a wound of never having been its own, so the story after is the work of making one island out of all the hands that held it.
