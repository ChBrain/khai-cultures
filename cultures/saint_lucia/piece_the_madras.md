# Piece: the madras

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On the woman at the festival, the dancer in the square, and the child at Jounen Kweyol: the madras, the bright checked cotton of the Creole national dress, the wide skirt, the foulard at the shoulders, and above all the head-tie folded high, its points talking their old language of the heart. Worn at Creole Day, worn at the flower festivals of La Rose and La Marguerite, worn at every showing of the island's soul, the cloth the nation put on to be itself.

## Load Bearing

It stand for the Creole soul made visible. The madras come with the trade and the island's women take it up into a dress all their own, the checked cloth and the folded head-tie become the sign of the Kweyol heritage, the festival, and the faith. It is the soul the colonial mind once dress in shame turn into a thing the island now wear with pride, the costume of Jounen Kweyol and the flower societies, the body of the Creole self the way the dish is its table. The very points of the head-tie were said to tell you whether a heart was taken or free, a private language folded up into public cloth. Without this dress the island's reclaimed soul would have no body to put on.

## Apparent

A bright madras check in red, yellow, and green, a wide gathered skirt, a foulard crossed at the shoulders, a jupe and a jip, and the tete en l'air, the head-tie folded high in stiff peaks. Plain cotton cloth in itself, gay and cheap on the bolt; its weight is in the soul it carry, the Creole heritage and the festival worn on the body of the woman who tie it.
