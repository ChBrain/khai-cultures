# Saint Lucia: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-record sources behind the plots,
personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md) is written from Saint Lucia's profile on the
model of national culture (read across the Caribbean and Latin American region,
which Saint Lucia is not separately scored within) and names no dimension and
carries no number. The orientation is recorded here so the data is on the record
without entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Saint Lucia orientation (regional, approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | high                                        |
| Individualism                 | low (collectivist)                          |
| Motivation toward Achievement | moderate                                    |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the six-dimensions model of national
culture). Saint Lucia is not separately scored in the published country data; the
orientation above is read from the surrounding Caribbean and Latin American region
and is approximate, used only to tune the pitch and never quoted in the prose.

## Public-record sources

from the public record. They are credited here and never claimed; the staging is
original work. The harder parts of the story - slavery on the sugar plantations,
the long colonial contest between France and Britain, and the Kweyol soul the
colonial school taught the island to be ashamed of - are staged soberly on the
public record, as the gap between promise and reality.

The one defining archetype, [the banana grower](persona_the_banana_grower.md), is
not a historical individual but a composite: the small grower of the green-gold
valleys who carries the island's living on a single crop and the Kweyol soul of
the land, meeting the world that buys the crop in a borrowed English.

The cultural framing throughout - Kweyol as the living soul carried beneath an
official English, the Roman Catholic and Creole reality, the flower festivals and
Jounen Kweyol, and the pride of a small island that gave the world two Nobel
laureates - is drawn from Saint Lucia's own self-understanding and the public
record.
