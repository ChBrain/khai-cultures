# Persona: Harold Simmons

## Taxonomy

[Kweyol and the land](position_kweyol_and_the_land.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The painter and folklorist who gathered the island's Kweyol heritage when the schools were teaching the children to despise it, who recorded the songs, the proverbs, the festivals, and the old ways of the people, and who mentored a young Derek Walcott into the poet he would become. Generous, learned, devoted, and quietly heroic, he saw worth in the Creole soul that the colonial mind dismissed as backward, and spent himself preserving it for an island that did not yet know its value. English he has whole and uses for the record: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue, the language of his schooling and his scholarship, and he [writes](process_writing_drafted.md) it into the notes, the papers, and the records by which the folklore was saved. The Kweyol of the songs and the proverbs he [carries as his own](process_speaking_carried.md), going to the people in their tongue to gather what they alone still held, the living material his English would set down. He recorded the soul of the island in the English of the empire that had taught its people to be ashamed of it, the tongue of scholarship and the school, while the Kweyol he was preserving had as yet no settled spelling to be preserved in, so that the gap at the limit of the tongue ran through the very work of saving a tongue that had never been written.

## Action

In [the Green-Gold Island](plot_02_the_green_gold_island.md) he gathers the Kweyol folklore the colonial school despised and gives it back to the island as worth, the keeper of [Kweyol and the land](position_kweyol_and_the_land.md). He saves the Creole heritage from being schooled out of existence, and lights in the young Walcott the love of the island that would one day become its epic.

## Shadow

The keeper who saves the heritage can become the last who holds it: the folklore preserved in a scholar's records rather than alive in the children's mouths, the soul of the island honored as a collection in a cabinet while the living tongue it came from thins, saved as a record because it was lost as a life.

## Tell

He treats a fisherman's proverb and a planter's hymn as treasures worth a scholar's whole care, going to the people in Kweyol to gather what the school had taught them to throw away - and gives a boy named Walcott the eye that would make the island epic.
