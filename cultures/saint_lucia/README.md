# Saint Lucia: a khai culture

Saint Lucia staged as a full khai play, ISO LC, language en. A small Creole
island fought over fourteen times that gave the world two Nobel laureates, as a
theatre: its history as the plot line, its people as the cast, the roles its
language and faith encode, its places, its practices, and its defining objects,
all tuned by a single pitch.

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the Cultures house
that holds this culture and answers for it. This culture logs the house as its
Estate; a culture with no Estate is not yet a production.

## The production

- **Anchor:** [play_saint_lucia.md](play_saint_lucia.md), the culture itself.
- **Pitch:** [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md), the Hofstede layer, written from Saint
  Lucia's profile (a gentle, devout, lyrical, courteous people, Kweyol-warmed and
  fond of the festival and the proverb, carrying a small island's hard-won pride
  and a single-crop economy's quiet worry) and naming no dimension.
- **Plots (history):** the Helen of the West Indies (the Kalinago and the
  fourteen changes of hands between France and Britain), the Green-Gold Island
  (sugar then bananas and the Creole-Catholic society on the land), the Island of
  Two Nobels (Arthur Lewis and the road to independence in 1979), and the Pitons
  and the Future (Walcott's epic, tourism, and the reclaimed Kweyol soul) - each
  casting the elements it needs, and each staging the gap between promise and
  reality honestly.
- **Personas:** Derek Walcott, Sir Arthur Lewis, Sir John Compton, and Harold
  Simmons as the historic cast, plus one defining archetype, the banana grower
  (the small grower of the green-gold valleys whose Kweyol mother tongue meets the
  world in a borrowed English). Each is wired to the language engine by the
  language-crossing it holds.
- **Positions:** the Helen of the West Indies (the contested French-British island
  that changed hands fourteen times), two Nobels from a small island (the outsized
  achievement in letters and learning), and Kweyol and the land (the
  Creole-Catholic soul and the soil of sugar and banana).
- **Places:** the Pitons (the twin volcanic peaks above Soufriere, the face the
  island shows the world), Castries (the small capital and market, Walcott Square,
  the town that schooled both laureates).
- **Processes:** Jounen Kweyol (Creole Day of language, food, and dress),
  the Gros Islet Jump-Up (the Friday-night street party of the fishing village).
- **Pieces:** green fig and saltfish (the national dish of green banana and salt
  cod), the madras (the bright checked cloth of the Creole national dress).

## Provenance

The Hofstede scores behind the pitch and the public-record sources are recorded in
[REFERENCES.md](REFERENCES.md). The data never enters the prose; the pitch is its
invisible hand. Sources in the public domain are credited, never claimed; slavery
and the long colonial contest are treated soberly on the public record, as the gap
between promise and reality; the staging and the architecture are original work.
