# Pitch: the Saint Lucian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Saint Lucia](play_saint_lucia.md) is played. It change nothing of what happen, not the Kalinago, not the fourteen changes of hands, not the green gold, not the two Nobels. It set only how it sound, the register in which all of it is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played gentle, devout, and sweet-mouthed: a people who not in a hurry and never short on manners, who say good morning before they ask you anything, who carry their faith out in the open and their grievance soft, and who warm up their English with the music of Kweyol. Sa ka fet, the voice say, before it say one thing more, the greeting that come first because manners come first. The voice is sweet and turned to the ear, fond of the proverb and the old word, the saint's day and the festival, the flower society and the church bench. There is a deep good humor in it, a love of company and song and the long limin conversation, and a small island's pride that has learned it have every right to feel proud. It talk of the sea and the mountain like they near things, it name the day by the saint, and it keep the courtesy that cost nothing and mean everything.

## Undertow

Under the sweetness run the long memory of the tug-of-war, the island won and lost and won again until no flag ever feel wholly its own, and the quiet ache of a tongue and a faith handed down by other people. There is the wariness of the small before the big, of the island before the world that buy its bananas and its beaches and could stop one day, just so. And under the gentleness lie the dependence the sweetness cover: a whole life balanced on one single crop, the green gold that one bad season or one closed market could take, and the old knowing that what you build on one thing can fall with it. Sa ki fet, fet, the people say, what done is done, but the worry stay.

## Nerve

It strike the chord of the small island that proved itself: the conviction that beauty and grace and even genius can come from a speck in the sea, that the Kweyol they once laughed at is a soul worth keeping, and that the saint's day and the festival are how a people hold together. It play on the love of the land and the faith and the song, and on the pride, hard-won, of a place the world fight over and that gave the world more than its size ever promise. It play, too, on the worry underneath, on what a small island lean on, and what it would do if that one thing was gone.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people gentle and proud the same time: devout, mannerly, and sweet on the tongue, warming their English with Kweyol, naming the day by the saint and the flower, and carrying with grace both an outsized inheritance of achievement and an undersized economy's worry. A small island that know its smallness and has answered it, holding its Creole heart against the wide pull of the world.
