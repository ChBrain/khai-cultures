# Plot: the Green-Gold Island

## Taxonomy

[play_saint_lucia](play_saint_lucia.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

With the wars settled, the island turns to the land. The plantations want a crop and a people to work it, and on the soil of the river valleys a Creole society takes shape, African in its majority and its tongue, Catholic in its faith, balanced first on the cane and then on a fruit that will be called the green gold.

## Action

The island builds itself on the land. Sugar comes first, worked by the enslaved Africans whose freedom was long withheld and whose descendants would make the island's living after; and when the sugar fades the banana rises in its place, the green gold of the valleys, a single crop carrying a whole people's wage to the boats and the world. On that land a Creole-Catholic society grows, Kweyol in the yard and the market, the saint's day kept and the flower societies singing, the soul of the island lived in a tongue the colonial school taught the children to despise. Against that shame [Harold Simmons](persona_harold_simmons.md) gathers the folklore, the songs and proverbs and festivals, and gives the Kweyol heritage back to the island as worth, while [the banana grower](persona_the_banana_grower.md) works the fruit on which it all leans.

## Stage

- [Harold Simmons](persona_harold_simmons.md): the folklorist who saves the Kweyol heritage the school despised and gives it back as worth.
- [the banana grower](persona_the_banana_grower.md): the Creole worker of the green-gold valleys on whom the island's living rests.
- [Kweyol and the land](position_kweyol_and_the_land.md): the Creole-Catholic soul and the soil of sugar and banana that fed it.

## Tension

The island finds its living and its soul on the same land and is bound by both. The green gold pays a whole people's wage and balances it on a single fruit whose price is set an ocean away, and the Kweyol soul that is the island's truest self is the very thing its schools teach it to be ashamed of, so the work of the green-gold years is to find a living that does not hang on one crop and a pride that does not despise its own tongue.
