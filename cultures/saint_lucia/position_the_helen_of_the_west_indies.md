# Position: the Helen of the West Indies

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The island as the contested prize, fought over until it changed hands fourteen times between France and Britain, more often than any other in the Caribbean, and named the Helen of the West Indies for the war waged over her beauty. From the Kalinago who held the island first, through the French who planted and named it and brought the faith and the Creole, to the British who took it last and gave it the English and the law, the island was less a possession than a battlefield, its harbors and heights worth dying for. The contest left no single inheritance but a doubled one, and a people who learned to be the ground of others' wars before they could be their own.

## Orders

That the island is shaped by the tug-of-war, English and French both pressed into it, Catholic faith and British law laid one over the other, so the Saint Lucian is heir to two empires and the property of neither; that what was fought over so long must be worth holding; and that a people made on contested ground learns to take what each comer left and make of it something its own.

## Loses

Ground when the contest is remembered only as a wound, the long humiliation of being the prize and never the player, so the island reads its past as a loss rather than an inheritance; and ground, too, when the doubled heritage is treated as confusion rather than wealth, the French faith and the English law and the Kweyol tongue made to quarrel in one small place instead of being held together as the one thing no single empire could have given.

## Drives

Lives in [Sir John Compton](persona_john_compton.md), who took the long-contested island out of the contest at last and led it to its own flag and its own name, the father of the nation that the Helen of the West Indies finally became. It drives Saint Lucia to carry the long tug-of-war as heritage rather than only as wound, and to make one island out of all the hands that held it.
