# Position: the Palmetto State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a Lowcountry coastal state built on two intertwined inheritances: the [historic stewardship](position_historic_stewardship.md) of Charleston's port cities, their masonry, their zoning, their tourist economy, and the [plantation resistance](position_plantation_resistance.md) of the Gullah Geechee, the West African descendants who kept their land, their sweetgrass weaving, and their oral histories alive along the same salt marshes that the plantation economy once ran on. It carries the memory of Fort Sumter, where the Civil War opened, and of the indigo and rice trade that built the old port fortunes on enslaved labor. What one holds without choosing it is a coast that is at once a preserved historic district and a homeland still being defended.

## Orders

That the old buildings, the lime mortar, the historic zoning, and the polite, story-driven, formal manner of speaking be maintained as a mark of Southern hospitality and civic pride; that the coastal land, the sweetgrass, and the Gullah language custom be protected as ancestral inheritance and not folklore for tourists; and that the two traditions, the preserving of brick and the preserving of a people, be treated as one shared work of stewardship over the same threatened coast.

## Loses

Ground when the resort and real-estate boom that funds the historic preservation is the very force displacing the Gullah families from the ancestral land the preservation is supposed to honor, when rising property taxes and gentrification push the coast's oldest residents out of the district that bears their history, and when the salt marshes that hold both economies together erode faster than either side's plans can protect them.

## Drives

Drives South Carolina to hold its restored brick and its coiled baskets as two halves of one coastal inheritance, pressing the state to protect Fort Sumter's walls and the Gullah Geechee Corridor's land alike, and to reckon honestly with the plantation past that indigo and rice built before either kind of stewardship can be called complete.
