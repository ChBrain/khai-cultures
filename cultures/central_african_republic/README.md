# the Central African Republic: a khai culture

The Central African Republic staged as a full khai play, ISO CF, official
language French, with Sango as the lingua franca that binds the country. A land
of many peoples at the heart of the continent, held together by a shared tongue
and a single creed, staged as a theatre: its history as the plot line, its
people as the cast, the roles its languages encode, its places, its practices,
and its defining objects, all tuned by a single pitch.

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the Cultures house
that holds this culture and answers for it. This culture logs the house as its
Estate; a culture with no Estate is not yet a production.

## The production

- **Anchor:** [play_central_african_republic.md](play_central_african_republic.md),
  the culture itself.
- **Pitch:** [pitch_fr.md](pitch_fr.md), the Hofstede layer, written from the
  Central African profile (a warm, collective, person-to-person register: family
  and kin first, respect for elders and chiefs, patience and dignity under the
  hardship of a landlocked land) and naming no dimension.
- **Plots (history):** the peoples and the great rivers, the crossroads of the
  Oubangui (where Sango is born), Boganda and the dream of a nation (the founding
  creed, zo kwe zo), and the Sango nation - each casting the elements it needs.
- **Personas:** Barthelemy Boganda, the founding thinker who gave the country its
  name and its creed; Pierre Makombo Bambote, the writer who carried the villages
  into letters; Idylle Mamba, the singer who carries the country's voice in Sango;
  and Marcel Diki-Kidiri, the linguist who gave Sango its letters and rules; plus
  one archetype, the forest singer of the BaAka, keeper of the polyphonic song.
  Each is wired to the language engine by the language-crossing it holds.
- **Positions:** zo kwe zo (every person is a person, the sober national creed),
  Sango (the unifying tongue born of the river), and the peoples (the living
  plurality of Gbaya, Banda, Mandjia, Sara, Mbaka, and the BaAka forest people).
- **Places:** Bangui la Coquette (the capital on the Oubangui) and the great
  forest (the equatorial south and the home of the forest people).
- **Processes:** the polyphonic singing of the BaAka, and fishing the river (the
  life of the Oubangui from which the shared tongue grew).
- **Pieces:** the sanza (the thumb piano of the intimate voice) and the balafon
  (the wooden xylophone that leads the dance).
