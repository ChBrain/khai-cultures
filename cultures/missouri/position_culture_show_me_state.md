# Position: the Show-Me State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Membership in a state defined by the confluence of the Mississippi and Missouri rivers, carrying a double inheritance forged at the geographic crossroads of the country: [gateway west](position_gateway_west.md), the pioneer transit heritage of riverboats and wagon trains that made St. Louis the doorway to westward expansion, and [jazz groove](position_jazz_groove.md), the syncopated big-band swing that Kansas City musicians recorded starting in 1925. Its mother tongue is [Missourian English](position_language_en_us_mo.md), spoken in the melodic river drawl of the St. Louis waterfront and the syncopated urban cadence of the Kansas City clubs alike. It carries, too, the political weight of the Missouri Compromise of 1820, which drew the very boundary lines for the expansion of slavery in the territories the state itself sits astride. What a Missourian carries without ever choosing it is a skeptical, show-me pragmatism, the demand to be shown rather than told, whether the claim comes from a riverboat trader or a jazz promoter.

## Orders

That one bring the same steady, weathered competence to reading a river current as to phrasing a jazz solo, since both are crafts of navigating something that never sits still; that the state's crossroads position be honored as a working inheritance, a place people, goods, and sound pass through and are changed by, rather than a mere waypoint; and that a claim be shown before it is believed, the state's own pragmatic temperament applied equally to a levee engineer's numbers and a club owner's promise.

## Loses

Ground where the divide between the state's rapidly expanding metropolitan centers and its resource-dependent river valleys turns land use and watershed regulation into a fight between state mandate and rural property right; ground where [gateway west](position_gateway_west.md) is remembered only as a monument to westward transit and not as the same crossroads history that carried the Missouri Compromise's bargain with slavery; and ground where corporate music platforms strip local royalties from the very Kansas City clubs that gave [jazz groove](position_jazz_groove.md) its sound.

## Drives

It drives the state to hold the Missouri Compromise of 1820, the Gateway Arch completed in 1965, and the Kansas City jazz boom of 1925 as one continuous story of a place built by people, cargo, and sound passing through and taking root. It shapes those raised here toward a narrative, story-driven warmth paired with a pragmatic refusal to take a claim on faith, whether the daily work is piloting a barge through the confluence or phrasing a saxophone line. And it drives the state to answer whether a river state built on transit and floodplain can manage its own flooding as capably as it once managed the nation's westward traffic.
