# Persona: Marcus Garvey

## Taxonomy

[the resistance](position_the_resistance.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The Pan-Africanist prophet, born in St. Ann's Bay, founder of the movement that told millions of the African diaspora to look with pride to Africa and to rise, and the first National Hero of Jamaica. Visionary, tireless, and grand in his designs, he built a movement and a press, called a scattered people to dignity and self-reliance, and lit the spark from which Rastafari would later grow. English he had whole and turned into thunder: he [spoke](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thought](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue and [wrote](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) it into editorials and orations that crossed oceans, the cadence of the Jamaican pulpit and platform in every line. The Patois of the yard he [carried as his own](process_speaking_carried.md) and the high English of the empire he [read and followed](process_reading_followed.md) deeply, the better to answer it with. He preached the redemption of Africa in the very English the empire imposed, the tongue of the masters turned into the trumpet of the mastered, and Africa itself - the home he bid a people remember - stayed a continent of tongues lost to the crossing, names and languages the slave ships had drowned. So the gap at the limit of the tongue was the gap he named: a people calling home in a borrowed language to an Africa whose own tongues had been taken from them.

## Action

In [Emancipation and Garvey](plot_02_emancipation_and_garvey.md) he raises [the resistance](position_the_resistance.md) from the flight of the few into a vision for the many, telling a freed but still-bound people to look to Africa and to themselves, and setting the island and the diaspora on the long road to dignity and nationhood. He turns freedom from a date on a calendar into a calling, and plants the seed from which the island's faith and music will grow.

## Shadow

The prophet honoured at home was hounded and jailed abroad, and the grand designs he raised - the line of ships, the great return - foundered on the world's hostility and his own reach, so the man can be remembered as a dreamer who failed rather than a seer who saw first. And the pride he preached can curdle, in lesser hands, from the lifting of a people into the scorning of others, the redemption of one root mistaken for a ranking of the rest.

## Tell

He tells a people the ships brought in chains to lift up their heads and look to Africa, and writes the call in the conqueror's English so all the diaspora can read it - summoning home a nation to a homeland whose own tongues the crossing had silenced.
