# Piece: jerk

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On the roadside drum-pan grill, in the yard, at the cookout, and now on menus the world over: meat - chicken, pork, fish - rubbed with a fierce wet spice of Scotch bonnet pepper, pimento, thyme, and scallion, and smoked slow over pimento wood until the smoke and the heat have gone all the way through. From the bush where the Maroons first cooked it to the jerk pan on every Jamaican corner and the island's most-shared gift to the world's table.

## Load Bearing

It stands for survival turned into flavour, [the resistance](position_the_resistance.md) made edible. Jerk was born of the Maroons, who took the seasoning and the slow smoking of the meat into the hills as a way to cook and to keep food on the run from the empire, a method of the free people of the bush. What began as the cooking of a people refusing bondage became the signature taste of the whole nation and one of its most-loved exports, the pimento smoke carrying the island's defiance and its genius for making much of little. Without this fire the island's table would have no dish that the whole world knows on sight and by name as Jamaican.

## Apparent

Meat in a dark, fiery rub, charred at the edges and smoke-deep within, eaten with festival, rice and peas, or hard dough bread, and washed down cold. In itself it is only seasoned flesh over a fire; its weight is in the history packed into the spice - the Maroon bush, the pimento wood, the heat of the Scotch bonnet - and in the hands that have kept the method alive from the hills to the roadside drum.
