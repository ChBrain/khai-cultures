# Persona: Louise Bennett

## Taxonomy

[out of many, one people](position_out_of_many_one_people.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Miss Lou, the mother of Jamaican Patois letters and folk culture, the poet, broadcaster, and performer who took the tongue of the people - long shamed as broken English - and raised it into verse, onto the stage, and into the nation's heart. Warm, witty, and unbowed, she gave the island back its own voice and its own laughter, and taught a colonial people that the way they truly spoke was not a fault to hide but a language to be proud of. The [Patois of the yard](position_language_en_jm.md) was her mother tongue and her whole life's work: she [spoke](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thought](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [heard](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) in it, and she [wrote](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) it down at last as letters, putting on the page the tongue the schools had refused to print. The proper English of the colony she [carried](process_speaking_carried.md) when she had to and [read its canon](process_reading_followed.md) thoroughly, the better to set her own tongue beside it as its equal. She made literature of the speech a whole nation lived in and a whole school system had marked as wrong, and the gap at the limit of the tongue was the one she crossed and closed: she carried Patois over the line from shame into pride, from the unwritten yard into the printed book, and gave the many one tongue they need no longer apologise for.

## Action

In [Likkle but Tallawah](plot_04_likkle_but_tallawah.md) she raises [out of many, one people](position_out_of_many_one_people.md) by giving the many their common tongue in print and on the air, showing the island its own face in its own words. She turns Patois from a mark of the unschooled into the proud bond of the nation, and makes the speech of all the people the literature of one people.

## Shadow

The tongue she lifted is loved on the stage and still marked down in the classroom, honoured in her verse and corrected out of children's mouths, so the island can applaud Miss Lou and go on teaching its young to be ashamed of the very speech she crowned. The pride she won can be kept as nostalgia rather than carried as policy, the mother tongue celebrated in a poem and denied a place in the school.

## Tell

She stands on a stage and tells the island its own story in the tongue of its own yards, and a whole nation that was taught the speech was wrong laughs and weeps and knows, at last, that it was always its own.
