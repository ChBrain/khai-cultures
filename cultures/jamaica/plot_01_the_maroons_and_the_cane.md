# Plot: the Maroons and the Cane

## Taxonomy

[play_jamaica](play_jamaica.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

The island the Taino call Xaymaca is taken by Spain in 1494 and then by England in 1655, and the English turn it into a great machine of sugar, working the cane with the labour of the enslaved carried in their thousands across the ocean from Africa. From the first the bondage breeds flight, and into the wild limestone hills go those who will not be held - the Maroons, the free people of the interior.

## Action

Through the late seventeenth and into the eighteenth century the Maroons build free communities in the hills the English cannot conquer, striking at the plantations and drawing the enslaved to them. In the east the Windward Maroons rise under [Nanny of the Maroons](persona_nanny.md), who reads the land like a weapon and holds the high ground against every column the empire sends. The First Maroon War ends not in conquest but in treaty: in 1739 the British, unable to win, sign a peace that leaves the Maroons free in their own land. A colony built to be all cane and bondage finds at its heart a free people it could not break - and the cane economy grinds on around them, the wealth of the island and the bondage that made it standing side by side.

## Stage

- [Nanny of the Maroons](persona_nanny.md): the Windward leader who fights the empire to a treaty and makes the hills free ground.
- [Cockpit Country](place_cockpit_country.md): the wild limestone interior, the redoubt the British cannot take.
- [the resistance](position_the_resistance.md): the refusal to be ruled, raised in the hills into a free nation.

## Tension

The island is built on the gap between the wealth of the cane and the bondage that produced it - a colony of sugar made by people held against their will. And into that gap the Maroons drive their freedom: bondage is met not only with suffering but with flight, with arms, and with a treaty signed by a free people, so the story of Jamaica begins already with both the wound and the answer, both the cane and the hills that would not be held.
