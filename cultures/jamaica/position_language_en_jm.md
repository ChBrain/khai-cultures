# Position: Jamaican English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

What the Patois end of the tongue can say that plain English cannot: a verb that need not change for tense at all, carrying only a small marker word in front of it, "a" for what is happening now, "did" or "en" for what is already done, so time is shown by a separate word rather than by changing the verb itself; and a plural you all its own, "unu", that plain English lost centuries ago and now has to replace with you all or you guys.

## Orders

It strips the copula from before an adjective, so "im tall" says a man is tall without ever needing a word for is, the missing link understood from word order alone; and it requires reduplication to carry force where standard English would reach for an adverb, "likkle likkle" said twice to mean truly small, so repetition itself becomes a grammatical device and not just an emphasis laid over speech.

## Loses

The single plain past tense of standard English, since the aspect markers of the Patois end mark not when a thing happened but whether it was already done, still going, or only about to start, distinctions standard English folds into one simple verb form. And it loses, at the far edge of the tongue, whatever grammar and words the languages of the crossing carried before English replaced them, so no marker in the sentence can reach back past the English forced onto it.

## Drives

It drives whoever speaks the full span of the tongue to hear aspect before tense, listening for whether an act is finished, ongoing, or only about to happen rather than merely for when it fell in time. And the copula it drops trains an ear to hear a whole claim, "im tall", "im nice", carried by two words alone, so meaning rests on order and marker rather than on any verb of being.
