# Persona: E.O. LeBlanc

## Taxonomy

[the Creole nation's voice](position_the_creole_nations_voice.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Edward Oliver LeBlanc, the premier and cultural nationalist who led the island through its years of self-government and made the lifting up of Creole identity the work of statecraft, championing the small farmer, the Kweyol tongue, and a pride in being Dominican. He took what had been treated as the backward speech and ways of the yard - the Kweyol, the madras, the bele drum - and put them at the center of the nation's idea of itself, so Creole became something to wear and sing and claim rather than hide. Sa ki ta'w se ta'w, what is yours is yours: warm, rooted, and quietly determined, he is the Creole nation's voice raised in pride. English he has whole as the tongue of governance: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue, the [English of the assembly and the law](position_language_en_dm.md), and [writes its policy and its arguments](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) into the record. The Kweyol of the farmers and the markets he [carries as his own](process_speaking_carried.md), the Creole of the people he came up among and never left behind. He raised the Kweyol nation's pride from inside the English machinery of a colonial state, lifting the yard's own tongue with the hand of a government that ran in another, so that the very Creole he championed lived in the homes and the songs while the state that championed it still spoke and wrote in English - the gap at the limit of the tongue running between the language of the people he honored and the language of the office that did the honoring.

## Action

In [the Creole Nation](plot_03_the_creole_nation.md) he leads the island through self-government and makes Creole identity the heart of the national project, lifting the Kweyol tongue and the small farmer's way of life into pride and policy. He carries [the Creole nation's voice](position_the_creole_nations_voice.md) into statecraft, building the cultural ground that independence and the festivals would later stand on, and teaching the island to value what it had once been taught to be ashamed of.

## Shadow

The pride he lifted can curdle into a pride that looks only backward, the Creole identity celebrated in costume and song while the hard work of feeding and keeping the people on the steep, poor land goes underdone; and the championing of the small farmer can become a romance of the yard that outlives the living the yard can actually provide.

## Tell

He stands a Kweyol-speaking premier before a colonial assembly that runs in English, and makes the yard's own tongue and the small farmer's pride the official business of the nation in the making - the Kweyol that was kept for the kitchen now spoken with the head held high.
