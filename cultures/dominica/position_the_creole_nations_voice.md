# Position: the Creole nation's voice

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The Kweyol-Catholic people the island made of herself, and the voice she found in letters and statecraft. Out of the meeting of French and African and Kalinago on the plantation rose a new people with a new tongue, Kweyol, the French-based Creole spoken in every yard, the World Creole Music Festival drawing the whole region back to hear it sung, and a faith that gave the nation her motto and her calendar. That people learned to speak for itself: in the cultural nationalism that lifted Creole from shame into pride, in the stateswoman who carried the small island's word into the world, and in the novelist who took the island's light and loss into the literature of the age. The Creole nation's voice is Dominica claiming her own speech, her own faith, and her own seat at the table.

## Orders

That the Kweyol tongue and the Creole way are not some lesser thing to be hidden but the very identity of the nation, to be spoken with pride and kept alive; that a small island has the right to her own voice in her own affairs and in the world's, and need answer to no larger power for being herself; and that the dignity of the Creole nation rests on her language, her faith, and her leaders speaking plain and for their own.

## Loses

Ground when the Creole is treated as backward beside the metropolitan French or English, the Kweyol pushed back to the yard and out of the school and the office; and ground, too, when the small nation's voice is drowned by the larger powers around it, or when her own leaders speak for the office rather than for the people whose plain Kweyol first gave the nation her tongue.

## Drives

Lives in [Dame Eugenia Charles](persona_eugenia_charles.md), the first woman to lead a Caribbean nation, who gave the island a stateswoman's voice; in [Jean Rhys](persona_jean_rhys.md), who carried the island into the world's letters; and in [E.O. LeBlanc](persona_eo_leblanc.md), who made Creole pride the work of statecraft. It drives Dominica to speak as herself - in Kweyol, in faith, and in her own name - and to claim her place, however small, among the nations.
