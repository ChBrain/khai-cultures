# Position: Dominican English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

English carries a set of small modal words, can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, that alone stand in for whole moods other tongues build into the verb's ending, so a shade of permission, obligation, possibility or doubt turns on choosing one particle rather than conjugating a form. It has no verb ending of its own for the future; it borrows "will" and "going to" to point ahead, and it multiplies short phrasal verbs, put up, put off, put out, where one plain verb and a small word after it carry a meaning the single verb alone does not hold.

## Orders

It forces a choice of modal or periphrasis every time the speaker points to permission, obligation, possibility or futurity, since no single verb ending carries that weight; say "must go" or "have to go" or "should go" and each choice sets the shade of duty differently. It forces the little words after the verb, the phrasal particles, into fixed pairings that cannot be swapped or reordered without breaking the sense, "give up" is not "give down", and it forces subject-verb-object order to carry grammatical roles the noun no longer marks.

## Loses

It marks no formal or familiar address; one word, "you", serves the stranger and the intimate alike, so distance and closeness that other tongues fix in the grammar have to be built instead from tone and phrasing, or go unmarked entirely. Its many phrasal verbs, precise as they are, often resist a single clean equivalent elsewhere, so idiom is what a learner must memorize whole rather than derive from a rule, and a plain verb translated word for word can miss what the phrase actually carries.

## Drives

It trains the speaker to hear obligation, doubt, and possibility as separate small words chosen one at a time rather than as a single verb form learned once, so nuance becomes a matter of vocabulary rather than conjugation. It trains an ear for the exact particle that completes a phrasal verb, since the wrong one changes the meaning outright, and because no grammar marks social distance, it drives the speaker to weigh how familiar to sound in the words themselves, every time she addresses another person.
