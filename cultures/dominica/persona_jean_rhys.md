# Persona: Jean Rhys

## Taxonomy

[the Creole nation's voice](position_the_creole_nations_voice.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The novelist born on the island, who carried its light and its loss into the literature of the age and wrote "Wide Sargasso Sea," giving a voice and a back-story to the silenced Creole woman of an English classic, set in the Caribbean she came from. Watchful, displaced, and unsparing, she spent most of her life away in cold Europe yet wrote the island's heat and green and damaged grandeur into prose no one could forget. She is the Creole nation's voice in letters, the island looking at itself and at the empire from the inside out. English she has whole and made into art: she [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [drafts](process_writing_drafted.md) in her mother tongue, the English of the metropole she would turn back on itself from the margin. The Kweyol of the island's yards and kitchens she [followed on the ear](process_hearing_followed.md) as a child and [deciphered only in part](process_reading_deciphered.md), the Creole of the people around her that she never wholly held but never lost the sound of. She wrote the Caribbean Creole's silenced story in the polished English of the empire that silenced her, giving the island's voice a hearing only in the colonizer's tongue, while the Kweyol that was the island's own speech stayed at the edge of her ear, half-heard and never fully written - so the gap at the limit of the tongue runs between the English she mastered and the Creole she could only listen to.

## Action

In [the Creole Nation](plot_03_the_creole_nation.md) she carries the island into the world's literature, writing the Caribbean and its Creole inheritance into the canon from her exile, and giving the silenced woman of the empire's story a voice and a homeland. She carries [the Creole nation's voice](position_the_creole_nations_voice.md) into letters, making the small island and its damaged colonial grandeur a subject the whole reading world would meet.

## Shadow

The exile who wrote the island best wrote it from far away, and her brilliance can stand for the island's long pull to leave - the gifted carried off to colder, richer places and claimed there, the homeland that makes the artist and then watches the artist made elsewhere, the voice of the island heard everywhere but at home.

## Tell

She writes the green island and its Creole light from a grey room across the sea, decades and an ocean away, and the place she could not stay in is the only place her prose ever fully comes home.
