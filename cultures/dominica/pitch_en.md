# Pitch: the Dominican tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Dominica](play_dominica.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens - not the last refuge of the Kalinago, not the Neg Mawon, not independence, not the storm. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which all of it is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played rugged, warm, and devout: a people shaped by a hard, beautiful land they call by its rivers, who hold together by faith, family, and the yard. Plainspoken and proud, slow to boast and quick to share, they greet a stranger in Kweyol, feed whoever comes, and trust the neighbor and the church before any distant office. There is patience in it, the patience of a small island that has weathered empire and storm and outlasted both, and a quiet, stubborn pride in being the wild one, the green one, the last that kept its own. Under the calm runs resilience worn to the bone: bend in the wind, hold the root, and rise again.

## Undertow

Under the pride runs the hardship of a poor and storm-battered land: the steep slopes that grow little easily, the banana crop at the mercy of the market and the wind, the young who leave for Guadeloupe or away and do not all come back, and the knowing that one night of hurricane can take a year of green in an hour. The wild beauty is real and so is the cost of living in it - the road washed out, the river risen, the rebuild begun again. The patience can shade into the long wait of the small and overlooked; the faith carries a people who have learned to need it.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of the wild held and the storm survived: the conviction that the land is a gift and a trust, that after God comes the Earth and nothing else stands above it, and that a people poor in money can be rich in green, in kin, and in their own Kweyol tongue. It plays on the pride of being the Nature Isle, the unconquered one, the keeper of the last Caribs; on the warmth of the yard and the festival; and on the deep resilience of those who have already lost it all to the wind and planted again at first light.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a small green nation that bends and does not break: devout, rooted, warm, and tough, proud of its rivers and its Creole, watchful of the sky. A people who measure their wealth in rainforest and kin, who have outlasted the empires and the storms before, and who answer the worst the weather can do with the oldest words they have - after God, the Earth - and begin the green again.
