## Cue

As the empires turn parts of the island to plantation, they bring enslaved Africans to work the cane and the coffee on the steep slopes. But the same rainforest that had sheltered the Kalinago lies just behind the estates, and the mountains offer a way out to any who will take the hard road up into the bush.

## Action

The free of the forest, the Neg Mawon, build their camps up in the heights and refuse the plantation. Under chiefs like Jacko they hold out for years, the longest resistance the island ever saw, the forest herself their fort and their ally, until the campaigns against them and the wider tide of history bring the fighting to its end. In time emancipation comes to the whole island, the chains struck from estate and field, and the free of the forest pass into the free of a single people. Out of the meeting of African, French, and Kalinago on this ground a new Creole nation begins to take shape, carrying the dignity of the unowned right at its root.

## Stage

- [Chief Jacko](persona_chief_jacko.md): the Maroon chief whose forest war was the island's longest resistance, honored now as a national hero.
- [the Kalinago Territory](place_the_kalinago_territory.md): the east-coast homeland whose surviving first people shared the island with the new Creole nation taking shape.
- [Neg Mawon](position_neg_mawon.md): the spirit of the free of the forest, the refusal to be owned that becomes the root of the nation.

## Tension

The island's wildness that had been the Kalinago's refuge becomes the Maroon's fortress, the rainforest sheltering the runaway same as she sheltered her first people - and the dignity of the unowned, kept alive in the bush through years of hardship and resistance, passes down into the free nation. The cost was long and hard, the freedom held at the price of the forest and the fight; but the upright neg mawon of the island's memory stands for what the Dominican became, a people who kept on refusing to be without their freedom, no matter what it cost.
