# Position: the plantation legacy

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The sugar estate that made the country and its people: a coast drained below the sea and diked against the Atlantic to grow cane, worked first by Africans held in slavery and, after abolition, by indentured labourers brought from India under contract, and by Portuguese and Chinese besides. The canefield is the ground of the whole society - the village, the logie and the line house, the rum and the religion, the food and the speech all carried from the estate. It is the source of the plural people and of the bond between them, and it is the wound beneath them: a hierarchy of owner and owned, of bound and freed, whose memory still shapes the land it built.

## Orders

That the people of Guyana were made by the canefield, brought across oceans to labour on it, and that the nation owes its existence to the bound and the freed who cut the cane; that the plantation's hierarchy is a thing the country must reckon with and not repeat, the line between owner and labourer never to be drawn again; and that the dignity of those who were owned and bound is the first debt the republic carries.

## Loses

Ground when the estate's old order is denied rather than reckoned with - when the hardship that made the people is hidden behind the romance of the village, or when the divide the plantation set between African and Indian labour is left to harden into the divide of the nation; and ground, too, when the cane that built the country is treated as merely the past, and the people the estate made are not seen as still carrying, in their food and faith and speech, the long memory of how they came to this coast.

## Drives

Lives in [Martin Carter](persona_martin_carter.md), the poet who sang the bound and the freed and named the nigger yard he came from, and in [the canecutter](persona_the_canecutter.md), African then Indian, the bound and freed labourer who is the soul of the estate. It drives Guyana to honour the people the canefield made and to refuse ever to draw its line again.
