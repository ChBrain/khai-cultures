# Plot: the Bound and the Freed

## Taxonomy

[play_guyana](play_guyana.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

The coast of British Guiana is sugar, and sugar is labour. The estates are worked by enslaved Africans brought across the ocean in bondage, the canefield drained and diked below the sea and cut by hand. Then abolition comes, the enslaved are freed, and the estates, needing labour still, look across the world for hands to replace them.

## Action

Slavery is abolished and the freed leave the estates where they can, founding their own villages along the coast; and to keep the cane cut, the planters bring indentured labourers under contract from India in their hundreds of thousands, and from Portugal and China besides, the bound replacing the freed in the same fields. Out of the canefield a uniquely plural people is made - Indo-Guyanese and Afro-Guyanese the two largest, with Amerindian, Portuguese, Chinese, and Mixed among them - all carried or descended onto one thin coast by the same sugar. The plantation that gathers them sets them, African and Indian, in the same fields and the same scarcity, and the line between them begins. [The canecutter](persona_the_canecutter.md), African then Indian, stands for all of them, the bound and the freed who cut the cane that built the country.

## Stage

- [the plantation legacy](position_the_plantation_legacy.md): sugar, slavery, and indenture, the canefield that made the plural people and the bond and the divide beneath them.
- [Georgetown](place_georgetown.md): the port and market town of the sugar coast, where the estates shipped their cane and the peoples came and went.

## Tension

The same canefield makes the plural people and sets them against one another - African and Indian brought to the same coast by the same sugar, and divided in the same fields by the scarcity and the order the estate imposed. The plot stages the making of Guyana's people and the planting of its divide as one act, the bond and the wound of the plantation laid down together, soberly and without blame.
