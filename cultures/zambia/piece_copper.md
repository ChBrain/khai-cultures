## Place

Beneath the northern bush in great red seams, hauled up the shafts of the Copperbelt towns, smelted into bars and shipped down the line of rail to the world: the red metal that gives the country its name in the markets, its weight in its own economy, and an ore on the coat of arms itself. From the headframe over the mine to the wire in half the world's machines, the metal Zambia is known by.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the country's modern making and its hardest lesson at once. The copper drew men from every corner of the land into the mining towns and one shared working life, the first great mixing that prepared the one nation, and it paid, soberly, for the schools and the roads and the rise of the young country. But it also tied the whole nation to a single metal and a single price set far away, so that when the price fell the floor moved under everything, and the country learned that to ride out on one red metal is strength and exposure in the same seam. Without this ore the country would have neither its modern shape nor its standing warning to broaden what it leans on.

## Apparent

A bar of smelted red metal, heavy and bright when new, dulling to brown, or the ore still in the rock, green and blue veined through the stone. In itself just a useful metal traded by the tonne; its whole weight is in what the country built on it and what it learned from leaning on it.
