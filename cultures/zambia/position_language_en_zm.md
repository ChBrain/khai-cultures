# Position: Zambian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

English in Zambia holds, for speakers of the country's more than seventy Bantu mother tongues, a grammar built on fixed subject-verb-object order and a single pattern of subject-verb agreement, in place of the noun-class systems, fifteen to twenty classes deep in languages like Bemba, Tonga, Lozi, and Nyanja, that reshape a noun's prefix and every adjective, pronoun, and verb agreeing with it. It offers, too, the definite and indefinite article, "the" and "a," a grammatical resource none of the country's home languages mark this way, letting a speaker flag whether a thing is known or new in a single small word rather than through noun class or context.

## Orders

English forces, on any noun regardless of its class in the speaker's mother tongue, one uniform way of marking number and agreement, so a Bemba noun-class distinction that would reshape four or five words in a sentence collapses, in English, into a single "s" or none. And it forces the article to be supplied before nearly every singular countable noun, an obligation many speakers still mark imperfectly, since no Bantu language in the country requires a free-standing word for this at all.

## Loses

Crossing into English, the sentence loses noun-class concord entirely, the system in which a whole clause of agreeing prefixes announces, at a glance, what kind of thing is being discussed, animate, plant, tool, abstraction. It loses tone, since English carries no pitch distinctions the way Bemba, Tonga, and Nyanja do, so words and grammatical forms that differ only in pitch in the mother tongue collapse into the same sound in English. And it loses the ideophone, the vivid, sound-mimicking word class rich across Bantu languages, for which English has only weak, occasional equivalents.

## Drives

It drives whoever crosses daily between a noun-class mother tongue and English to re-sort, sentence by sentence, a class-based grammar into an article-based one, translating a concord system into a naming word. It drives the tonal and ideophonic color of the mother tongue back into the home and the proverb, since English carries neither, leaving speakers to hold two structurally unrelated grammars side by side rather than blend them. The one bridge tongue shapes a nation practiced at moving between two different kinds of grammar within a single day.
