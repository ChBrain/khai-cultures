# Piece: nshima

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On every table in the land, in the village and the town, at the wedding and the funeral and the plain weeknight supper: the thick white maize-meal staple, stirred stiff in the pot and turned out in a smooth mound, eaten by hand with a relish of greens, beans, fish, or meat. The food that a Zambian means when they say they have not yet eaten, however much else has passed their lips that day, shared from the common dish across the seventy peoples.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the country itself at the table: the one food the whole land shares across all its tongues and tribes, the plain, filling staple that makes the shared plate possible and the warmth of the home real. In a country of seventy peoples, nshima is the common ground three times a day, the dish round which family and visitor gather as equals, broken by hand and dipped in the same relish. It carries the creed of the human being first into the plainest act there is, for there is always a little more nshima for one more person. Without this food the warmth and the sharing the country lives by would have no table to sit at.

## Apparent

A mound of cooked maize meal, white and smooth, stiff enough to pinch off a lump and shape it in the hand to scoop the relish. Plain and humble on the plate, little to look at; its whole weight is in what it means, the daily bread of a nation, the food of belonging, the centre of every shared meal.
