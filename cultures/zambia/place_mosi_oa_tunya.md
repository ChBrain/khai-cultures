# Place: Mosi-oa-Tunya

## Taxonomy

Parent group: places

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Shown

The great falls on the Zambezi in the south-west, Mosi-oa-Tunya, "the smoke that thunders," where the whole breadth of the mighty river drops into the gorge and throws up a column of spray that can be seen from far off and a roar that the peoples knew long before any map named it Victoria. The largest sheet of falling water on the earth, the country's wonder and its welcome to the world, with the wide game lands and the river country around it where the walking safari was born. The Zambezi at its grandest, the thunder that gives the country its river and its pride.

## Holds

- [the great rivers](plot_01_the_great_rivers.md): the falls and the Zambezi the peoples settled along and knew long before any border.

## Offers

A place where the country shows the world its grandest face and where the Zambian holds the deepest pride, the smoke that thunders standing for the whole wild inheritance of the land; the river that feeds the country, the falls that draw the visitor, and the game lands where the walking safari leads the traveller out on foot to meet the bush at the pace of the animals that live in it.

## Withheld

The idea that the wonder is only a spectacle. Mosi-oa-Tunya carries the charge that the falls and the river are a trust and not just a draw, that the great water must be kept whole and not spent, and that the people who live closest to it, the fisherman on the Zambezi and the villager at the park's edge, are owed their share of the wealth the thunder brings to the world.
