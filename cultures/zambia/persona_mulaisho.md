# Persona: Dominic Mulaisho

## Taxonomy

[One Zambia, One Nation](position_one_zambia_one_nation.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The civil servant who became one of the country's foremost novelists, a man of the office by day and the page by night, who set the young nation and its people into the country's own books, among them the story of a village caught between the old ways and the new. Measured, watchful, and quietly ironic, he wrote the coming-together and its costs honestly, neither flattering the nation nor running it down. A Zambian writing for Zambians and the world, he [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in the home tongue of his people, yet he [writes in the bridge tongue](process_writing_drafted.md) and gives the country its literature in [English](position_language_en_zm.md), the neutral language of the new nation, and [reads and follows without effort](process_reading_followed.md) the wide shelf of the world he answers and adds to. He told the Zambian story in the tongue that bound the seventy peoples rather than in the village tongue it grew from, so that what was thought first in the mother tongue had to cross into English to be read by the whole nation at once, and in that crossing some grain of the home speech is always left on the far bank, the gap at the limit of the tongue between the language a story is felt in and the language it must be told in to be one country's.

## Action

In [the peace held](plot_04_the_peace_held.md) he records the nation's character and its strains in its own books, setting down the coming-together and its costs for the country to read itself by. By writing Zambia in the bridge tongue, he gives the one nation a literature it can all share, the choice of 1964 carried onto the page.

## Shadow

The bridge tongue that lets the whole nation read one book can also thin the home tongues it was meant only to join: the literature made in English while the seventy mother tongues go largely unwritten, their stories carried in the mouth and not the print. The very reach of the shared language can quietly leave the village speech behind on the shelf.

## Tell

He keeps the ledgers of the state by day and writes the soul of the nation by night, and tells the Zambian story in the one tongue all Zambians can read, knowing what the village speech lends it and what it cannot quite carry across.
