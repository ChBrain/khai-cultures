# Pitch: the Zambian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Zambia](play_zambia.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the great rivers, not the Copperbelt, not the choice of 1964, not the long peace. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played warm, slow, and unhurried, in a low and even voice that does not rush to quarrel. A people who put the human being and the family first, who greet you properly before they ask you anything, who would rather settle a thing over a long talk than win it loudly. Patient, gentle, given to humour and to laughing at themselves, they hold one another close, share what is on the plate even when the plate is small, and reckon that a stranger is just a visitor not yet welcomed. Authority is respected and the chief and the elder are given their due, but the tone is never harsh; people work things out the round way, the soft way, so nobody is shamed. Underneath runs a steady, modest pride, sure sure, in the peace they have kept and in being many peoples who became one.

## Undertow

Under the warmth runs the patience that can wait too long, the soft way of avoiding the quarrel that can leave the hard thing unsaid and the hard man unanswered. There is the weight of a country that rode out on the back of one red metal and feels the floor move under it when the copper price falls. And under the pride in the peace runs the quiet worry that the old tribe-feeling, kept down by the motto, is never gone for good, only managed, and that the things that hold seventy peoples as one are gentler than the things that could pull them apart.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of the shared plate and the kept peace: the conviction that the human being comes first, before colour and before tribe, that you greet before you ask and share before you keep, and that a country is held together by warmth and patience as much as by law. It plays on the pride of being one nation out of seventy peoples, on the thunder of the falls on the great river, and on the deep, modest sense that the peace they have kept while the region burned is the thing most worth the keeping.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a gentle, patient people who chose to be one: warm, humorous, slow to anger, proud of their quiet, holding seventy tongues together under one bridge tongue and the human being above all of them. A nation that runs on the faith that being many is no reason not to be one, mwabombeni, and that the plate, however small, is meant to be shared.
