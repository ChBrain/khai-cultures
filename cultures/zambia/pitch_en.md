# Pitch: the Zambian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Zambia](play_zambia.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the great rivers, not the Copperbelt, not the choice of 1964, not the long peace. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

The Copperbelt siren still sets the rhythm even where the mine behind it has gone quiet: shift change, shift change, the day divided by a sound the body remembers before the mind explains it. Mwabombeni greets a person at day's end the way good morning greets its start, thanks given to a stranger as readily as to a workmate, because in a country built on that shift everyone is, in some sense, on it together. One Zambia, One Nation is not a slogan read off a plaque but a thing rehearsed daily in the market, where Bemba answers Tonga answers Lozi and English carries the sentence no single tongue of seventy is trusted to carry alone. Nshima comes from one pot at one table, the same scooping hand whichever kitchen it scoops from. Authority is respected, the chief and elder given their due, but the tone stays level; the round way, the soft way, is preferred so nobody is shamed. Underneath runs a steady, modest pride, sure sure, in a peace kept while the region burned.

## Undertow

The siren that once ordered a shift now sometimes orders nothing, a mine closed or a price collapsed, and the town built around it left listening for a sound that does not come. There is the patience that can wait too long, the soft way of avoiding the quarrel that leaves the hard thing unsaid and the hard man unanswered. And under the pride in One Zambia, One Nation runs the quiet worry that the old tribe-feeling the motto keeps down is never gone for good, only managed daily, at the market, at the mine gate, wherever Bemba and Tonga and Lozi must find the shared word before the different one.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of mwabombeni meant, the conviction that the human being comes first, before colour and before tribe, and that a nation held by one motto and one pot of nshima is held as surely as one held by law. It plays on the pride of being one nation out of seventy peoples, on the memory of the Copperbelt siren that once called the whole country to the same shift, on the thunder of the falls on the great river, and on the deep, modest sense that the peace kept while the region burned is the thing most worth the keeping.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people who chose to be one: warm, humorous, slow to anger, proud of their quiet, holding seventy tongues together under one bridge tongue and the human being above all of them. A nation that runs on the daily rehearsal of mwabombeni and one shared pot of nshima, and on the faith that being many was never a reason not to become, deliberately, one.
