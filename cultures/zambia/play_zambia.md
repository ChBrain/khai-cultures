# Play: Zambia

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the house that holds this staging and answers for it.

## Name

Zambia, a landlocked country at the heart of Southern Africa, sitting high on the plateau where the great rivers run, hemmed by eight neighbours and named for the mighty Zambezi that gives it its border and its thunder. ISO ZM, language en, with more than seventy tongues spoken under that one official English. The capital is Lusaka. A country put together from many peoples and many kingdoms who decided, at the hour of independence, that they would be one nation, kanshi, and that none among them would be the lesser for it.

## Arc

Zambia is not one people who slowly became a state, but many peoples who chose, all at once, to become one nation. Long before the line was drawn, the Bemba and the Tonga, the Lozi of the flood plain and the Nyanja and Chewa of the east, the Kaonde, the Lunda, the Luvale and dozens more lived along the great rivers, each with its own chief, its own ceremony, its own tongue. Then the copper was found beneath the ground, the line of rail came up, and a string of mining towns rose where bush had been, drawing men from every corner of the land to dig the red metal that would carry the country on its back. At independence in 1964 the country took a hard decision: that with seventy languages and no single people large enough to rule the rest, it would hold itself together not by one tribe nor one tongue but by one idea, "One Zambia, One Nation," with English the neutral bridge that no group owns and so every group can share. The founding president, Kenneth Kaunda, "KK," gave the young country a gentle creed he called Humanism, that the human being comes first, before colour, before tribe, before money. And the country has kept the harder promise underneath the motto: through all the years since, while neighbours were torn apart, Zambia stayed at peace, mwabombeni, passing power without blood and keeping the plate shared. Under it all runs one nerve, sure sure: a warm, patient, slow-to-quarrel people, proud of their peace, proud of the falls that thunder on their river, who reckon that being many is no reason not to be one.

## Triggers

**[Plot 1: the great rivers](plot_01_the_great_rivers.md)**

Long before the borders, the many peoples settle along the great rivers, the Lozi raising a kingdom on the flooding plain under a Litunga like [Litunga Lewanika](persona_lewanika.md), each people with its own chief and its own ceremony, the deep human ground on which [the bush](position_the_bush.md) and the country would later stand.

**[Plot 2: the Copperbelt](plot_02_the_copperbelt.md)**

The red metal is found beneath the ground and the mining towns rise on the line of rail, drawing men from every corner of the land into one working life, and out of those townships [Rikki Ililonga](persona_rikki.md) and his generation make Zamrock, the sound of the new urban Zambia.

**[Plot 3: One Zambia, One Nation (1964)](plot_03_one_zambia_one_nation_1964.md)**

In 1964 the country comes to independence and, with seventy tongues and no people large enough to rule the rest, [Kenneth Kaunda](persona_kaunda.md) and his generation choose to hold the nation by one idea, [One Zambia, One Nation](position_culture_one_zambia_one_nation.md), with English the bridge that none own and all can cross.

**[Plot 4: the peace held](plot_04_the_peace_held.md)**

Through the decades since, while neighbours were torn apart, Zambia keeps the peace and passes power without blood, the quiet example its writers like [Dominic Mulaisho](persona_mulaisho.md) record and its people guard as the thing most worth keeping.

## Stakes

Whether a country made of more than seventy peoples, held together not by one tribe nor one tongue but by one idea, can keep [One Zambia, One Nation](position_culture_one_zambia_one_nation.md) whole and keep [the bush](position_the_bush.md) and the great rivers that feed it; whether the gentle creed of [Humanism](position_humanism.md) can keep the human being first when the copper price falls and times are hard; and whether the peace it has kept while the region burned can be carried on, generation to generation, so that being many never again becomes a reason to be divided.
