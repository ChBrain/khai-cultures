# Position: the Pine Tree State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a state split down the middle by its own coastline, half its life set on the rocky Atlantic shore and half set back in the conifer woods, so that being a Mainer means holding two hard-won trades at once. It carries [coastal stewardship](position_coastal_stewardship.md), the harbor discipline of the lobster docks and the trap lines, and it carries [forest independence](position_forest_independence.md), the self-reliant woodcraft of the timber towns and the winters that ask a man to look after his own. It speaks [Maine English](position_language_en_us_me.md), a down east cadence that says little and means what it says. What it hands to whoever grows up in it is a plain, unhurried practicality, and a trust that a person's word and a person's work are the same thing.

## Orders

That one respect the trap limit and the timber line as the same kind of promise, kept to neighbors as much as to the law; that one meet a hard season, a bad catch, an early frost, without complaint and without asking for more than a fair hand; that family labor and local self-determination be treated as the load-bearing walls of any town, harbor or sawmill alike; and that a stranger be judged by whether his word holds, not by where he came from.

## Loses

Ground when commercial overfishing consolidates boat ownership and prices the small operator out of the harbor his family has worked for generations, and when state-wide environmental mandates override local zoning and read as an outside hand overruling a town's own judgment. It loses ground, too, when the resort economy of the coast and the resource economy of the interior stop reading each other as the same state, and treat the other's slower season, whether the lobsterman's ice-out or the sawyer's growth ring, as somebody else's problem rather than the same hard land working two different trades.

## Drives

Lives in the boat captain who reads the harbor by its trap lines and the forester who reads the woods by its rings, two trades off the same coast that neither quite trusts to understand the other. It drives Mainers toward a quiet self-reliance, a patience with hard seasons that never turns into complaint, and a conviction that stewardship, of the lobster grounds or of the timber stand, is a duty owed to whoever fishes or cuts wood there next.
