# Persona: Thérèse

## Taxonomy

[les deux peuples](position_les_deux_peuples.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

An older Quebecoise of the Quiet Revolution generation, a grandmother who grew up when the Church still ran the schools and lived to see Quebec become master in its own house. Proud, warm, plain-spoken, she has spent her whole life inside French and never once had to leave it. French is her whole tongue: she [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [reads](process_reading_mother_tongue.md) in the mother tongue she was born to, the one her parish, her kitchen, and her chansonniers all spoke. The [English](position_language_en_ca.md) of the rest of the continent she [catches by ear](process_hearing_caught.md) from the television and the signs only as much as she must, and on the page she [makes it out word by word](process_reading_deciphered.md), no more. She kept her French whole in a sea of English and never had to leave it, living the two solitudes wholly from the French side, one of the two solitudes who share the country yet not the tongue, and there opens the gap at the limit of the tongue.

## Action

She lives [les deux peuples](position_les_deux_peuples.md) as a daily inheritance, keeping French alive in the home: she raises her grandchildren in the mother tongue, marks the feasts and the songs, and holds the French fact whole simply by never letting it slip. She does it without grand gestures, by the steady fidelity of a life lived entirely in her own language.

## Shadow

Her wholeness can harden into a closed solitude: she holds her French and her Quebec so completely that the English half of the country stays a foreign land she never enters, and she can see in the other peoples and the newcomers only a threat to the tongue she kept rather than fellow citizens of one country.

## Tell

She answers the world in French and waits for it to come to her, measures her life by the parish feasts and the chansons, and holds her tongue whole without ever having had to fight for it the way those who left it did.
