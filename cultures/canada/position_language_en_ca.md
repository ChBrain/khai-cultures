# Position: Canadian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Canadian raising, the sound outsiders hear as "aboot": before a voiceless consonant, the diphthongs in "about" and "write" start higher and tighter than they do south of the border, so "house" the noun and "house" the verb can differ only by this raised vowel. It carries too a spelling caught between two standards, British "-our" and "-re" kept (colour, centre) while American "-ize" is taken, and words of its own, "toque," "chesterfield," "riding," that name things the neighbouring tongue names differently or not at all.

## Orders

It orders the raised vowel onset before a voiceless consonant, a rule the speaker follows without ever choosing to, marking "about" and its kin with a sound the rest of North America does not share. It orders the tag "eh" onto the end of a statement, checking agreement or inviting a response, and it orders the mixed spelling, British endings on some words, American endings on others, as the settled written form.

## Loses

It loses ground daily to the American English next door, whose screens and spellings press on it until the raised vowel and the "-our" ending survive mostly by being defended rather than assumed, so that the accent's most Canadian sound is often the first thing worn down by a life spent watching American television. And its "eh" and its raised diphthongs read to the outside ear as one national marker, flattening the real regional variety, from the Maritimes to the Prairies, that the single label "Canadian English" covers over.

## Drives

It drives the ear raised in it to hear "about" and "out" with a lift that marks the speaker as Canadian before a single word of vocabulary is chosen. It drives the tongue to reach for "eh" to soften a statement into something shared rather than declared, and it drives the hand at the keyboard to hold the British ending on some words while reaching for the American on others, a spelling caught permanently between the two.
