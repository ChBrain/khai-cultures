# Pitch: the Namibian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Namibia](play_namibia.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the first readers of the land, not the gathering of the peoples, not independence, not the conservation nation. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played low, dry, and unhurried, like the country itself. A people of few words and long silences, who do not rush and do not crowd, raised by the space to keep their distance and hold their peace. They take a man as they find him, share what there is, and reckon a greeting is owed to anyone you meet on an empty road. There is a hardiness under the quiet, the toughness of folk who have learned to make do far from town, to fix the bakkie themselves and to wait out the drought. They carry their many tongues lightly, switching from Oshiwambo to Afrikaans to English in a single sentence at the braai, and they hold the old courtesies: you ask after a person's people before you ask after his business. Slow to boast, slow to anger, and content with little, they would rather a thing be done quietly and right than loudly and quick.

## Undertow

Under the calm runs the weight of the dry years, the knowledge that the rain may not come and the land gives nothing for free, so that patience is not always peace but sometimes only endurance. There is the loneliness of the great distances, the long roads between people, and the old habit of keeping things back, of not saying what sits heavy. And under the easy sharing of the many tongues runs the careful work of holding the many peoples as one, the awareness that the lines between them, though laid to rest under one law, are never quite forgotten, and that the equal dignity the nation swears by must be kept up by hand, day after day.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of space and patience: the conviction that there is room enough for everyone, that the land is to be shared and not seized, that the wild is a trust and not a spoil, and that a thing kept slowly is kept well. It plays on the pride of a country that gave the elephant and the rhino back to its own people to guard, on the deep satisfaction of self-reliance far from help, and on the quiet that the desert teaches, the sense that the light and the silence and the open veld are themselves a kind of wealth no busier country can buy.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a hardy, unhurried people at home in a vast dry land: sparing with words, generous with what they have, slow to push and slow to be pushed, holding their many peoples and their wild creatures alike as a trust to be kept. A nation that runs not on hurry but on patience, that reckons the space and the silence a gift, and that would rather keep a thing well than have it fast.
