# Plot: the gathering of the peoples

## Cue

Over the long centuries the peoples came to the dry land and stayed, each finding its ground and keeping its way. To the well-watered north came the Ovambo by their rivers; across the open veld the Herero and the Himba moved with their cattle; the Nama and the Damara held the centre and the south; the river folk settled the green strip of the Caprivi; and in time the Baster and the German- and Afrikaans-speaking communities took their place too.

## Action

Across the wide and thinly peopled country the many nations made their homes, each holding fast to its own tongue and dress and custom: the Herero making the long Victorian gown wholly their own, the Himba keeping the old herding life and reddening with otjize ochre, the San reading the land as they always had, the river peoples by their water and the desert peoples by their patience. None gave up what it was to live here; the land was wide enough to hold them all. And there rose leaders who spoke for their people and, through them, for the whole land, men like [Hosea Kutako](persona_kutako.md), whose steady patient voice held the many to one hope and carried their cause to the wider world. So the country became what it is: many peoples, many tongues, one dry and open land shared between them.

## Stage

- [Mvula ya Nangolo](persona_nangolo.md): the poet who gave the land and its peoples a voice in verse.
- [Gazza](persona_gazza.md): the musician who braided the many tongues into one beat the whole country owns.
- [the many peoples](position_the_many_peoples.md): the truth that Namibia is many peoples held as one in equal dignity.

## Tension

A country of many peoples, each keeping its own, must find how to be one without asking any to stop being itself, and how to share a dry and open land in peace. The wide space gives room enough for all, but the holding of the many as one is never finished work; it must be kept up by hand, the old lines laid to rest and the equal dignity of each tended day after day.
