# Position: Namibian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

It carries no grammatical gender and no noun classes at all, so a single unmarked word for a thing or a person does the work that the click languages and Bantu tongues of this land divide across a dozen separate noun classes. It thrives on borrowed words and phrasal verbs, and a fixed subject-verb-object order that lets a newcomer assemble a correct sentence long before mastering any of the country's other tongues.

## Orders

It orders a fixed subject-verb-object sequence in every sentence, a lone -s on the third-person-singular verb and nothing on any other person, an article, a or the, before nearly every countable noun, and tense and aspect built by stacking auxiliary verbs, has, is, will, rather than changing the verb's own ending. And it orders a single flat you for every listener, high or low, one or many, with no separate word for respect.

## Loses

It cannot make a single click sound, so any name or word built on one arrives flattened or borrowed whole rather than spoken as its own tongue intends. It has no noun classes to mark whether a thing is a person, a long object, or a liquid the way Otjiherero and Oshiwambo do, and its one flat you cannot carry the graded respect a Namibian mother tongue folds into the way a child addresses an elder.

## Drives

It drives whoever holds it to lean on word order rather than word ending to be understood, since so little of the meaning rides on the shape of the word itself. It trains the speaker to keep one tongue for the record, the report, the classroom, and another, often a mother tongue, for the heart, switching between them as the moment asks; and it flattens, in its own grammar, the graded respect that another tongue would have made the speaker choose.
