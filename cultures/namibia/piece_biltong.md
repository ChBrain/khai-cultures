# Piece: biltong

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

## Place

In the cupboard and the glovebox, hung in strips in the dry air of the kitchen, passed round at the braai and packed for the long road: biltong, the air-dried spiced meat that every Namibian household keeps and every traveller carries. Cut from beef or from game, kudu and oryx and springbok, salted and spiced with coriander and hung to cure in the desert-dry air, it is the country's own travelling food, chewed on the long empty roads between far places.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the old desert wisdom of keeping food against the dry and the distance: in a hot land far from anywhere, meat had to be cured to last, and biltong is that necessity turned into a thing the whole nation loves. It carries the self-reliance of a people used to making do far from town, the hunter's and the herder's craft of wasting nothing, and the easy sharing of the braai where a strip is always passed round. It crosses every people and every tongue, kept in the Ovambo kitchen and the German one alike, a taste the whole country owns. Without this dried meat the long Namibian road would be hungrier and the larder thinner against the dry years.

## Apparent

A dark dry strip of cured meat, hard at the edge and red within, dusted with spice, looking to a stranger like little more than dried-out jerky. In itself it is preserved meat; its whole weight is in what it carries, the desert's lesson of keeping against want, the long road it rides in the glovebox, and the hands that cut and cure and pass it round.
