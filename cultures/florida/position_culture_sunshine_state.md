# Position: the Sunshine State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a peninsula built twice over, once by Spanish stone at St. Augustine and again and again by every land boom, retirement wave, and launch schedule since, so that being Floridian means holding a short memory and a long coastline at once. It carries the [gateway identity](position_gateway.md), the bilingual, outward-facing stance of a state that is the hinge between the American mainland and the Caribbean, Latin America, and the orbital frontier beyond the atmosphere, and it carries [the Sunshine Dream](position_sunshine_dream.md), the promise of leisure, sun, and quick fortune that first sold the sand dunes as resorts. It speaks [Floridian English](position_language_en_us_fl.md), loosened at the edges by Spanish and by the drawl of the coast. What it hands to whoever grows up inside it is a taste for reinvention over roots, and a climate that makes indulgence feel less like a vice than a birthright.

## Orders

That one welcome the visitor, the retiree, and the newcomer as a matter of course, since the state's whole economy is built on people arriving rather than staying put; that one keep an eye on the sky every hurricane season without letting the threat slow the next development; that one treat the coastline, the launch pad, and the theme park gate as equally serious business; and that leisure be pursued in earnest, since a place that sells itself as paradise must keep looking like one.

## Loses

Its footing when the rapid overdevelopment that built the boom drains the water table and erodes the coast it was sold on; when the century-old patience of a city like St. Augustine is treated as a slower, lesser thing next to the urgency of the next rocket or the next resort; when the working families who kept the Space Coast running are priced out by the same gentrification the boom depends on; and when the fragile Everglades and its wetlands are read only as a safety metric on a launch dashboard rather than as the mother-water the whole peninsula still depends on.

## Drives

Drives its people to look forward rather than back, to trust the next boom over the last century, and to treat the horizon, whether it is the cruise line's or the rocket's, as the place where the real Florida is still being built. It sets the tour guide and the engineer against each other without meaning to, the one reading the coast in centuries and the other in launch windows, and it drives both all the same toward the same restless conviction: that a state built on sand and sun and speculation can keep remaking itself faster than the tide or the market can take it back.
