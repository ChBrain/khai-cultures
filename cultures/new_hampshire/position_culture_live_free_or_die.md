# Position: Live Free or Die

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The state motto stamped on every license plate and meant in earnest: a granite-hard belief that liberty is worth more than comfort, and that a small state answers to its own town meeting before it answers to anyone else. It carries [granite independence](position_granite_independence.md), the local liberties and first-in-the-nation primary that let an ordinary town moderator's debate carry outsized national weight, and [alpine precision](position_alpine_precision.md), the mountain-engineering exactness of the Mount Washington cog railway and the sugarhouse alike. Its people speak [New Hampshire English](position_language_en_us_nh.md), a spare New England cadence that says little and trusts the listener to fill in the rest. It carries no broad-based income or sales tax, and the frugal, self-reliant habit that goes with paying your own way instead of asking the state to.

## Orders

That every four years the nation's political attention bend toward a state small enough that a candidate must still stand in a living room and answer a neighbor's question directly; that the town meeting, not the state house, be where the real argument over a road or a school budget gets settled; that a gauge be checked twice, a steam valve calibrated to the pound, and a batch of syrup graded honestly, whether the product is a mountain railway or a jug from the sugarhouse. It orders a flinty self-reliance, no one waits for outside help who could reasonably do the work themselves.

## Loses

Ground when the state's fierce localism curdles into gridlock, town meetings unable to agree on the very zoning and watershed protections that a forest under commercial logging pressure needs decided at scale rather than parish by parish. It loses ground in the seam between the fast, tourist-driven cog railway economy and the slower, weather-bound rhythm of the maple harvest, each treating the other's calendar as an inconvenience. And it loses ground to its own low-tax frugality when the roads, schools, and forests it prizes need public money that the state's own motto makes it reluctant to raise.

## Drives

It drives New Hampshire's people to say little and mean it, to distrust the grand gesture and trust the neighbor who shows up and does the work. It drives the state to keep punching above its size every primary season, a first-in-the-nation vote that gives a town of a few thousand the same national hearing as a metropolis. And it drives its people toward an exacting self-reliance, whether that means calibrating a rack-and-pinion gear on a mountain railway or defending, meeting after meeting, the plain right to be left alone and to answer for oneself.
