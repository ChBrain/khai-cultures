# Faroe Islands: references

Provenance for the Faroe Islands culture: the historical sources behind the plots
and personas, public-domain credits, and the Hofstede source data. Sources are
cited so the record is on file; they are credited, never claimed, and the staging
and architecture are original work. The Faroese prose is machine-composed and
flagged for native-speaker review.

## Tuning data (Hofstede)

The Faroese cultural profile is treated as broadly Nordic - a low power distance,
a strong egalitarian and consensual streak, a restrained and long-term
orientation, and a close, communal, kin-and-village collectivism - and is the
silent source behind [pitch_faroe_islands.md](pitch_faroe_islands.md), which is
written from it and never names a dimension or a number.

## History behind the plots

- **The settlement and the Løgting** - the Norse settlement of the islands from
  around the ninth century, the Faereyinga saga, and the ancient assembly at
  Tinganes in Torshavn, one of the oldest parliamentary sites in the world.
- **The language awakening** - V.U. Hammershaimb's written standard for Faroese
  (1846), the national awakening and the Christmas Meeting (Jolafundurin) of 1888,
  and the long "language struggle" (malstridid) for Faroese beside Danish.
- **The occupation and home rule** - the British occupation of the islands during
  the Second World War (1940-1945), which separated them from occupied Denmark and
  deepened their self-reliance, and the Home Rule Act of 1948.

## Personas from the public record

- **Trondur i Gotu** - a Viking chieftain of the Faereyinga saga.
- **V.U. Hammershaimb (1819-1909)** - the priest and philologist who created the
  modern written form of Faroese.
- **Nolsoyar Pall, Poul Poulsen Nolsoe (1766-1808/09)** - seafarer, poet of the
  Fuglakvaedi (the Bird Ballad), and national forerunner who challenged the Danish
  trade monopoly.

The skipper and the young Faroese are composite archetypes, not individuals, drawn
from the common experience of their generations.

## Geography and language

Geographic facts (ISO FO; eighteen islands in the North Atlantic; the capital
Torshavn) are on the public record. Faroese, a North Germanic language closest to
Icelandic, is the national language; Danish is also official and taught, and the
islands are a self-governing nation within the Kingdom of Denmark.
