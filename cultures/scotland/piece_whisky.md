# Piece: whisky

## Owner

## Place

Poured in glens and tenements alike, offered to the first-foot at Hogmanay and passed at the end of a ceilidh, the amber spirit distilled from barley and peat-reek, the uisge beatha, the water of life.

## Load Bearing

It stands for patience, craft, and the turning of a hard land's plain grain into something prized the world over. Made slowly, aged long in the cask, and named for its glen, it carries the thrift and rigour of the country and its quiet pride in doing a hard thing well. Without this shared cup the warmth beneath the dour reserve would have no vessel.

## Apparent

A clear, amber spirit of malted barley, sometimes smoky with peat, sometimes soft and sweet, drunk neat or with a drop of water. Nothing about it is hurried; its whole virtue is the long, patient years it spends in the wood.

## Yearbook

- Distilled from barley and water, often dried over peat, across the country's glens.
- Named uisge beatha in Gaelic, the water of life, from which the word whisky comes.
- Aged for years in oak and offered as the cup of welcome and fellowship.
