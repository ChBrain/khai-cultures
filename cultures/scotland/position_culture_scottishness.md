# Position: Scottishness

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The belonging to a small, hard-won nation that fought free on the field and then reformed its own faith into a stern, levelling church. It carries [the clan](position_the_clan.md), the kinship of glen and Highland that once bound loyalty tighter than any crown; it carries [the Kirk](position_the_kirk.md), the Presbyterian church that answers to no bishop and treats the elder and the ploughman as equal before God; and it carries [egalitarianism](position_egalitarianism.md), the democratic intellect that measures a person by argument rather than rank. It speaks in three tongues at once, [the Scots tongue](position_language_sco_gb_sct.md) of the countryside and the sang, [Scottish English](position_language_en_gb_sct.md) of the school and the office, and what remains of [Scottish Gaelic](position_language_gd.md) in the glens the Clearance emptied. It carries, unchosen, a pride in a small country's stubborn mind and a grievance at what a larger neighbour has taken from it.

## Orders

It orders that nae man is better born than another, and that rank counts for less than a kept word and a level argument. It orders the parish school and the keen dispute in the pub and the kirk session both, since a Scot is expected to think and answer for himself rather than defer to his better. It orders dour reserve in trouble and sudden warmth at the ceilidh and at Hogmanay, and it orders that the glen and the city, Gaelic and Scots and English, be held as one people rather than let fall apart.

## Loses

Ground at Culloden and the Clearances, where the clan system of the Highlands was torn out by the root and the Gaelic tongue was driven from the glens into a handful of ageing mouths. It loses ground in the industrial cities that built and then lost their yards and their mills, and in a levelling pride that can curdle into chippiness, reading every success as showing off and every neighbour's rise as a grievance. And it loses ground in the old national question, never quite settled, of how much of itself a small nation still owes to the union it entered and how much it must now answer for alone.

## Drives

It drives its people from Bruce's field at Bannockburn to Burns's conviction that a man's a man for a' that, and from the stern equality of the Kirk to a poor, devout, schooled nation that gave the world Hume and Adam Smith. It drives a Highland woman to keep her Gaelic and her songs alive against the pull of the city, and a city Scot to cut down pretension with a quick line and treat the famous and the unknown exactly alike. It drives Scotland to hold glen and firth, pride and grievance, together as one stubborn, questioning people.
