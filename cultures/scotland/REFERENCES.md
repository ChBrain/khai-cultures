# Scotland: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_sct.md](pitch_sct.md) is written from Great Britain's profile on
Geert Hofstede's model of national culture - Scotland is a constituent nation of
the United Kingdom and has no separate Hofstede profile, so the national (Great
Britain) scores are the documented base - and it names no dimension and carries
no number. The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the
record without entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Great Britain score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 35                            |
| Individualism                 | 89                            |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 66                            |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 35                            |
| Long Term Orientation         | 51                            |
| Indulgence                    | 69                            |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published
by Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group. No Scottish sub-score is
claimed; the national (Great Britain) profile is the documented base.

## Languages

The play and most of the cast are written in English (`en`). Two personas are
authored in their own tongue: [persona_burns.md](persona_burns.md) in Scots
(`sco`) and [persona_mairi.md](persona_mairi.md) in Scottish Gaelic (`gd`). The
Scots of Burns and the Scottish Gaelic of Mairi are the authors' own composition,
offered in good faith and best reviewed by a native speaker.

The two defining personas [Màiri](persona_mairi.md) and [Eilidh](persona_eilidh.md)
are archetypes, not historical individuals: composites that carry the culture's
living tensions - the older Gaelic-speaking Highlander of the clans and the
crofting life (Màiri) against the younger urban egalitarian Scot of Glasgow and
Edinburgh (Eilidh).
