# Position: Cornhusker culture

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Membership in the Cornhusker State, shaped in one breath by [pioneer trail](position_pioneer_trail.md), the frontier grit of the wagon trains that crossed the Platte by Chimney Rock and the homesteaders who broke prairie sod into farms, and by [prairie forestry](position_prairie_forestry.md), the tree planting stewardship born the day J. Sterling Morton set a million saplings in Nebraska City and gave the world Arbor Day. Its tongue is [Nebraskan English](position_language_en_us_ne.md), the even prairie cadence carried alike in the cattle yard and the conservation office. It carries the grassy dunes of the Sandhills and the memory of a sod brick house raised on a claim with nothing but grit and a deed.

## Orders

That a fence line be walked and mended, a cattle brand logged true, and a claim proved up the way the homestead law required. That a windbreak be planted and tended row by row, the way Arbor Day first asked of a young state bare of trees. And that the county's history, wagon rut and sod cabin and shelterbelt alike, be kept as a shared inheritance rather than a private curiosity.

## Loses

Ground where the quick, high-volume talk of ranching and cattle markets drowns out the slower, season-bound patience of the windbreak and the soil conservation board, so that tree planting gets treated as a holiday rather than a working discipline. Ground, too, where state wide zoning and irrigation mandates meet the suspicion of counties that prize their own fence lines, and the topsoil itself, when the wind finds the gap a windbreak was meant to close.

## Drives

It drives the rancher to measure the year by the herd moved and the fence mended, and the conservationist to measure it by the saplings set and the erosion stopped, and it asks that a Nebraskan hold both counts as one inheritance, not two. It drives the state to keep faith with the trail that first crossed it and the trees that later held its ground, so that the same grit that broke the sod now plants the windbreak that keeps it.
