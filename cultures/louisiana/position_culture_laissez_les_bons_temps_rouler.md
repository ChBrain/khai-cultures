# Position: Laissez les bons temps rouler

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The bond of belonging to a delta culture built on [Creole pluralism](position_creole_pluralism.md), the layered French, Spanish, African, and Native American inheritance that made Louisiana its own civil law territory inside the United States after the Purchase of 1803, and on [bayou resilience](position_bayou_resilience.md), the self-reliance and ecological adaptation born of swamp living. It carries [Louisiana English](position_language_en_us_la.md), threaded with French loanwords like lagniappe and calques like making groceries, and it carries the trumpet's wail, the crawfish boil, and the jazz funeral as the ordinary furniture of a life measured in celebration. It carries, too, the memory of a diverse coalition of Creoles, free Black soldiers, pirates, and militia who stood together at the Battle of New Orleans in 1815.

## Orders

It orders that one savor the day, the meal, and the music before the schedule; that diverse cultural contributions, Creole and Cajun and Native and African alike, be honored rather than flattened into one strand; that family and community close ranks when the water rises or the hurricane comes; and that a rhythmic, narrative way of talking carries as much truth as a plain report.

## Loses

It loses ground to the disappearing wetlands of the Mississippi Delta, acre by acre, and to a commercialized tourism that reduces a living culture to a marketing slogan and prices out the very residents who kept it alive. It loses, too, in the seam between the preservationist who reads the coastal master plan as statistics and the fisherman who reads the changing water with his own hands, each feeling the other has forgotten the harder half of the truth, and in a self-reliance that can slide into suspicion of the state-wide flood mitigation the delta now needs to survive.

## Drives

It drives a people to treat culinary art, music, and family gathering as the true anchors of a resilient life, no matter what the river or the storm does next. It lives in the preservationist who guards the trumpet archives and the balcony ironwork of the French Quarter, and in the fisherman who reads the bayou season by season and adapts his trap to water that will always go where it wants. It drives Louisiana to keep the crawfish boil and the jazz funeral going even as the coast erodes, holding that the good times are worth rolling toward however hard the water pushes back.
