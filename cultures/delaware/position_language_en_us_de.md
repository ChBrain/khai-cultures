# Position: Delawarean English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The double stock English keeps from its own history, an Anglo-Saxon word standing beside a French or Latin one for nearly the same thing, ask and inquire, ask and request, house and mansion, gives the tongue two registers in one vocabulary, a plain word and a learned one, so a speaker can reach for the short Germanic term or the longer Romance one and shift weight without changing language. It has almost no case marking left on the noun, so meaning rides on word order alone.

## Orders

It forces a fixed subject-verb-object order in nearly every sentence, since the noun itself no longer shows by its ending who did what to whom; move the words and the sense breaks. It forces an auxiliary "do" into every plain question and every negation where older English needed none, "do you go", "I do not go", and it forces the bare verb to add only a thin -s for a single present-tense person and a flat -ed for a past, leaving almost no other marking on the verb at all.

## Loses

It has no grammatical gender left on the noun, no der, die, das to sort a thing by kind, so it cannot carry in the article what other tongues carry there. It keeps only one word for "you", singular or plural, familiar or formal, where other languages force a choice of distance at every address, so English must build warmth or formality by phrasing and tone rather than by a built-in grammatical form, and the finer shading some neighbor tongues mark automatically has to be spelled out instead, or goes unmarked.

## Drives

It trains whoever speaks it to listen for order rather than ending, to hear who acts and who is acted upon from position in the sentence, not from a suffix. It trains a habit of reaching between two vocabularies at once, the short native word for the plain thing and the long borrowed word for the weighed or formal one, so the same speaker moves registers by word choice rather than by grammar. And because no grammatical form marks closeness or distance in address, it drives the speaker to build that distinction herself, in the words chosen, every time she opens her mouth.
