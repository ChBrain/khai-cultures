# Place: the Cane Country

## Taxonomy

Parent group: places

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Shown

The rolling interior of the island given wholly to sugar: the green seas of cane running over the gentle hills of the parishes, the great houses of the planters set above them, the windmills that ground the harvest, and the small wooden chattel houses of the workers, built to come apart and travel, standing along the estate roads. The chattel house is the whole story in timber: a man could own the house but not the ground beneath it, so he built it light, pon loose coral blocks, to take up and carry to the next estate when the work moved, and dressed it neat with its little fretwork and its bright paint regardless, because a Bajan keeps a thing proper even when the thing must move. This is the landscape the sugar revolution made, the parish country of St. Philip and St. George and St. John where the plantation covered the land edge to edge, and where the labour that built the island's wealth was bent over the rows.

## Offers

A place where the island's history is written in the land itself: the cane that made Barbados the first plantation society and the model for the Caribbean, the chattel house that shows the worker's life held always provisional, and the great house that shows where the wealth went. It offers the truth of the island plainly, the beauty of the green country and the system that shaped it inseparable.

## Withheld

The innocence of a pretty landscape. The cane country withholds any reading of itself as mere countryside: every field is the ground the plantation worked, every chattel house the dwelling of those who were owned and then freed to little, and the rolling green is the face of the system the island gave the world, handsome and unforgettable in what it was raised to do. Look how green, a visitor will say; the field don't answer, but the people who cut it know what the green cost.
