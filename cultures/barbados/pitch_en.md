# Pitch: the Bajan tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Barbados](play_barbados.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens - not the settling, not the sugar and the code, not the road to independence, not the republic. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which all of it is given.

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played orderly, courteous, and dry. Wunna keep the form and keep the temper both, value the manners and the propriety and the steady running of a thing, and say plenty in little. Reserved and understated where the neighbours loud, British in the bearing and the schooling, the people take pride in being correct, in church pon a Sunday and cricket pon a Saturday, in a thing well kept and a word well chosen. A man asks how you keeping and you answer, not too bad, when the house burn down. Under all that politeness runs the quick dry humour: somebody trip in the road and the watcher don't laugh out loud, he just say soft, well look how she find a short cut. The voice never raise to make the point. Wuh loss wid she, the old heads ask of anybody who carrying on too much, and that settle it. Settled where others restless, holding the place with a quiet earned dignity, the kind that don't need to tell you it there.

## Undertow

Under the orderliness runs the unhealed thing the order was built pon: the plantation Barbados pioneered for the world, the bondage beneath the tidy parishes and the great houses, the cost that bought the calm. There is a propriety that can turn into a politeness too careful to name the wound, an English form so well kept it can stand in the way of reckoning with what the form once served. The same dry tongue that say plenty in little can use the little to say nothing at all when the subject too hard. And under the modest pride runs the smallness of an island that ship its wealth abroad for centuries and must now make its own, the quiet question muttered low in the rum shop: whether the order it kept was ever truly its own to keep.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of dignity earned and order held: the conviction that a people can keep their bearing through anything, that manners and steadiness are themselves a kind of resistance, that a small island can carry itself with more composure than the empires that once held it. It plays on the pride of a thing done proper, the love of the game and the harvest and the parish, the deep Bajan belief in being correct and being free both, the tuk band drum and the Landship marching its mock crew through the village. It plays, too, on the ache underneath - of an order whose foundation is not yet fully faced, the thing everybody know and few will say straight.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people composed and courteous, dry of wit and steady of hand, who took the mould the mother country pressed pon them and made it their own, and who now govern as a republic the ground where the plantation began. A nation that runs pon quiet dignity and good order, that will tell you not too bad when it mean very well and very bad both, and that carries, beneath the calm, the long work of reckoning with the very thing that order was first raised to serve.
