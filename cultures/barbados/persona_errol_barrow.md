# Persona: Errol Barrow

## Taxonomy

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The father of Barbadian independence and the island's first Prime Minister, the wartime airman and lawyer who founded the Democratic Labour Party, built the welfare and the schools and the free education that remade ordinary lives, and carried the colony to nationhood in 1966. Bold and modern where his elders were cautious, dry of tongue where they were grave, he took the freedom whole rather than by careful instalments, and gave the island a national hero who would not be Little England's deputy but its own nation's first man, a Bajan answering to Bajans and to no governor across the water. English was his mother tongue and the Bajan dialect his native register: in it he [spoke](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thought](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) and [wrote](process_writing_mother_tongue.md), the plain, sharp English of an island that had held the tongue for three centuries and now would govern in it as its own. He served the empire in its war and then turned its own language to declare the island free of it. When the doubters asked how so small a place would manage alone, he did not raise his voice; he told them, mild, that those who would mind the people's business should first learn to mind their own, and let the sentence do the cutting. Where that English reached its limit lay the African homeland tongues the cane had taken from the enslaved who built the island, the speech lost so that his English could become Barbados's own, the gap at the limit of the tongue standing in the silence the dialect grew over.

## Action

In [the Road to Independence](plot_03_the_road_to_independence.md) he carries Barbados out of the colonial mould into nationhood in 1966, the first Prime Minister who would take the freedom entire and build a modern nation on it. He carries [Little England made its own](position_little_england_made_its_own.md) to its conclusion, the man who turned the well-kept colony into a self-governing nation answering to itself.

## Shadow

The bold founder who builds the nation can be remembered as the break alone, the rupture with the mother country honoured while the harder, older reckoning - with the plantation the island governs the ground of - waits beneath the new flag. The shadow is the temptation to think that nationhood itself completed the reckoning, when the cradle of the plantation still asks to be faced.

## Tell

He thanked the mother country for what it gave and then took the freedom whole, building free schools for the children of the cane so duh would never want for the learning he had - and declared the island its own nation in the very English the crown had carried ashore.
