# Plot: the Sugar and the Code

## Taxonomy

[play_barbados](play_barbados.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

By the middle of the seventeenth century the planters of the island turn from tobacco and cotton to sugar, and the cane remakes everything. The small farms give way to great estates, the land fills edge to edge with cane, and the labour to work it is brought in numbers from Africa, until the island becomes the first society of the New World organised wholly around the plantation and the enslaved who work it.

## Action

Barbados becomes the model: the first true plantation society, the template the rest of the Caribbean and the mainland colonies copy. In 1661 the island writes its slave code, the legal framework that binds the enslaved and orders the plantation, and that code becomes the template carried across the islands and into the colonies beyond, so the system first set down here shapes a hemisphere. The wealth of sugar flows off the island, and the order that earns it its English name is raised on that foundation. Against it the enslaved resist from the first, in endurance and refusal and rising, and in 1816 Bussa leads the largest rebellion the island has known, the great refusal that no code could finally hold.

## Stage

- [Bussa](persona_bussa.md): the enslaved leader who rises in 1816 against the system and gives his name to the cause of freedom.
- [the cane cutter](persona_the_cane_cutter.md): the labour the plantation consumed and the endurance that outlasted it, the foundation of all the island's wealth.
- [the Cane Country](place_the_cane_country.md): the parish country of cane and great house and chattel house where the sugar was grown and the rising swept the estates.

## Tension

The island gives the world a model of order and wealth and gives it, in the same act, the model of the plantation and the slave code, the system whose template it set for a hemisphere. The wealth was real and flowed largely off the island; the bondage was the foundation; and the resistance and the dignity of the enslaved, from the daily endurance to Bussa's rising, are the island's truer inheritance, the refusal that ran beneath the order from the first day and outlasted the code that tried to hold it. The masters wrote it down in law and thought the matter settled; the people the law counted as property had not finished with it, and never were going to.
