# Position: the Heart of Dixie

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Membership in a Deep South state shaped by the dark, fertile soils of the Black Belt, a land that carries the weight of a plantation past and the pride of two movements it gave the nation: the community organizing that broke segregation from a church pew in Montgomery, and the aerospace engineering that put a rocket on the pad in Huntsville. Its mother tongue is [Alabama English](position_language_en_us_al.md), spoken with an expressive Southern cadence in the courthouse squares and a fast technical drawl in the research corridors. What a native of this state carries without ever choosing it is a double inheritance, [civil rights](position_civil_rights.md) and [space pioneer](position_space_pioneer.md) ambition, held in the same soil, the same slow hospitality, and the same stubborn regional pride.

## Orders

That one honor the historic organizing of the boycotts and the churches that carried them, that one hold engineering precision and safety as a point of pride in the research corridors, and that one keep faith with neighbors and kin through slow, face-to-face courtesy rather than distant bureaucracy. It orders that a person be measured by the patience of their community work and the soundness of their craft, whichever soil they stand in, Black Belt or research corridor.

## Loses

Ground where the two halves of the state's inheritance are set against each other, as when aerospace ambition treats civil rights memory as a quaint relic, or when civil rights vigilance treats technical modernization as a threat to historic neighborhoods rather than a shared inheritance. It loses ground, too, where the plantation past is smoothed into folklore instead of reckoned with, and where superficial diversity policy stands in for the harder work of economic repair in the Black Belt's historic towns.

## Drives

It drives the state to hold its bus boycotts and its rocket launches as one story rather than two, the same soil that produced Rosa Parks' stand producing the engineers who sent Saturn V to the pad. It shapes those raised in it toward a stubborn, courteous self-reliance, an instinct to organize quietly and build precisely, and a loyalty to home ground that persists whether the calling is a pulpit, a picket line, or a test stand.
