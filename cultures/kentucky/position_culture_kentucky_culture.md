# Position: Kentucky culture

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a state built on two different kinds of ground worked with the same hard hands, the limestone pastures of the Bluegrass and the deep hollows of the Appalachian coalfield. It carries [bluegrass pride](position_bluegrass_pride.md), the pedigree, pageantry, and family-farm heritage of the horse country, and [coal solidarity](position_coal_solidarity.md), the union loyalty and mutual defense forged in the mining valleys, side by side without either one outranking the other. It speaks [Kentuckian English](position_language_en_us_ky.md), a tongue that keeps a true second-person plural, y'all, and a mountain register the plain standard has largely dropped. It holds, without ever having chosen it, the pageantry of the first Derby run at Churchill Downs, the legal weight Bourbon carries as a whiskey that can be made nowhere else, and the coal that lit the nation while it emptied out whole Appalachian valleys.

## Orders

That a pedigree in the paddock and a union card in the coalfield are both treated as inheritances earned, not favors granted; that a horse farm's breeding standard and a mine's safety standard are honored in practice, not only in the county fair program or the union hall banner; that a Kentuckian is judged by loyalty kept, to family, to farm, to fellow miner, over loyalty merely claimed; and that the state's prosperity, whether it rides on a racehorse or rides a coal seam, is weighed against what the hills and the water table under both can still bear.

## Loses

Ground when resort speculation prices the historic horse farm out from under its own breeders and mine closures empty a whole valley of the families who worked it, each hollowing out a different Kentucky at the same time; ground when the bluegrass counties and the coal counties talk past each other, the union hall read by the horse country as a relic and the racing purse read by the coalfield as money that never once came down into the hollow; and ground in the stripped and unreclaimed hillside itself, its water fouled long after the coal that was taken from it is gone.

## Drives

Drives Kentuckians to hold the paddock and the coal seam as one inheritance rather than two rival claims on what the state is really worth, judging both by the same measure of a debt honored and a hand kept. It pushes the state to remember that a horse bred right, a whiskey aged right, and a mine worked right all answer to the same patient, unhurried standard, and that the forest and water taken from the hills owe something back to the families who are still there after the coal is gone.
