# Persona: Louis Cha

## Taxonomy

[the Hongkonger](position_culture_hongkonger.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Born in Haining on the mainland into an old scholarly family, he carried the written Chinese of the land of his birth into a life spent almost entirely in the harbour city he adopted as an adult refugee from war and revolution. The mainland's own tongue, [中文](../china/position_language_zh_cn.md), is the one he never had to choose, the language he [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) his plots in before he ever sets brush or pen to paper and the one he [writes](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) fourteen wuxia novels in that would become the common reading of the whole Chinese-speaking world. He made his adult home, his newspaper, and his public life instead in [Cantonese](position_language_yue_hk.md) Hong Kong, [carrying](process_speaking_carried.md) the tongue of his adopted city into the founding and the four-decade running of Ming Pao; and he took up [English](position_language_en_hk.md), [read](process_reading_followed.md) closely enough to follow law and world affairs for the press he ran, a competence he later deepened as an older man studying history at Cambridge. He holds [the Hongkonger](position_culture_hongkonger.md) as the city that gave a mainland-born refugee his newspaper, his readership, and his second life.

## Action

In [the handover](plot_03_handover.md) he sits on the Basic Law Drafting Committee that writes the constitutional terms of the colony's return, bringing to the table both a newspaperman's decades of arguing Hong Kong's interests in print and a novelist's instinct for how a promise, once written down, will actually be read by the people who must live inside it. By putting his name and his standing behind the document that frames [one country, two systems](position_one_country_two_systems.md), he moves from chronicling the city's anxieties to helping draft the bargain meant to settle them.

## Shadow

The editor who spent a career insisting Ming Pao hold an independent line found his own independence questioned when the committee's optimism collided with the crackdown in Beijing in June 1989, and he stepped back from the drafting process in protest before later returning to it, a reversal that left neither his sternest readers nor, by his own account, entirely himself at ease.

## Tell

He wrote his fourteen novels serialized, a chapter at a time, in the pages of his own newspaper, so that a generation of Hong Kong readers first met Chinese history's greatest fictional swordsmen exactly the way they met the day's political news, folded into the same paper over breakfast.
