# Plot: the handover (1997)

## Taxonomy

[play_hong_kong](play_hong_kong.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

The lease on the New Territories was always going to run out, and in 1984 Britain and China sign the Sino-British Joint Declaration, agreeing that the whole territory, leased land and ceded land alike, will return to Chinese sovereignty on 1 July 1997. What China promises in return is not absorption but continuity: a Basic Law, a mini-constitution for the future Special Administrative Region, guaranteeing the territory's own courts, currency, and way of life unchanged for fifty years. The years between the declaration and the date itself are spent drafting that guarantee and wondering, on every side, whether paper can hold what it promises.

## Action

[Louis Cha (Jin Yong)](persona_jin_yong.md), already the most read Chinese novelist alive, sits on the committee that drafts the Basic Law, lending the document the standing of a man trusted by readers on both sides of the border. [Chris Patten](persona_chris_patten.md) arrives as the last governor, pushes to widen the territory's democratic institutions before the transfer, and spends his final years in office arguing, publicly and often bluntly, over how much of the old freedoms can be carried into the new arrangement. At midnight on 30 June into 1 July 1997, sovereignty passes to China under the formula of [one country, two systems](position_one_country_two_systems.md): one nation, but two systems of law, currency, and governance running side by side for fifty years. Patten hands over a city anxious about who will now answer for its way of life, and the fifty-year clock starts running.

## Stage

- [Chris Patten](persona_chris_patten.md): the last governor, who widens the territory's democratic institutions and hands sovereignty over at midnight.
- [Louis Cha (Jin Yong)](persona_jin_yong.md): the novelist on the Basic Law drafting committee, lending the guarantee the trust of a writer both sides believe.
- [one country, two systems](position_one_country_two_systems.md): the fifty-year formula under which the handover is made, one sovereignty, two systems running side by side.

## Tension

The handover settles the question of sovereignty and opens, in the same stroke, a longer one: whether a promise written into a Basic Law and a Joint Declaration can actually hold for fifty years against the pressures of the state that inherits it. Nobody present at the ceremony, drafter, governor, or crowd in the rain, can know in 1997 how the guarantee will be read a decade or three decades on; the handover is the moment the promise is made, not the moment it is tested.
