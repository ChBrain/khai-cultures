# Persona: Anita Mui

## Taxonomy

[the Hongkonger](position_culture_hongkonger.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Born in Hong Kong and on stage in the amusement parks of the city from the age of four, singing to earn money for a family that needed it, she grew into the voice the whole city would come to call its own, "the daughter of Hong Kong." She [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in [Cantonese](position_language_yue_hk.md) as the mother tongue of her childhood on the working-class stage circuit, and she sang in it too, turning the language of the street into the language of Cantopop's biggest ballads and its most theatrical, costume-changing arena shows; she carried, besides, a [Hong Kong English](position_language_en_hk.md) [worn](process_speaking_worn.md) for the interview, the film set abroad, and the world stage she toured once Cantopop's biggest star began selling out arenas outside the city too. She holds [the Hongkonger](position_culture_hongkonger.md) as the identity the whole city projected onto her and that she, in turn, gave back to it in every performance.

## Action

In [the contested autonomy](plot_04_the_contested_autonomy.md) she becomes the figure whose farewell frames the turn from the golden age to the anxious one: her string of televised farewell concerts and her death from cervical cancer in December 2003, the same year as the SARS outbreak and a huge pro-democracy march, close out the confident, glamorous Cantopop era just as the harder, more contested decades of the autonomy question begin in earnest. By dying as she had performed, in public, in costume, refusing to cancel a single farewell date, she gives the city a shared grief that crossed every line the years after would otherwise divide it by.

## Shadow

The stage persona that made her the whole city's daughter left little room for a private life of her own, and a public that mourned her so completely had spent decades before that same devotion picking over her romances, her health, and her choices as though they belonged to Hong Kong before they belonged to her. She kept singing and performing through an illness she did not disclose until near the end, giving the city the farewell it wanted at a cost she alone paid.

## Tell

At her last concert, already ill, she walked down the aisle in a wedding gown and told the audience she was marrying them, the city, since she had found no husband of her own, and sang on to the end of the set list she had insisted on keeping in full.
