# Hong Kong: references

Provenance for the Hong Kong culture: the historical sources behind the plots and
personas, public-domain credits, and the Hofstede source data. Sources are cited
so the record is on file; they are credited, never claimed, and the staging and
architecture are original work.

## Tuning data (Hofstede)

Hong Kong's cultural profile in Geert Hofstede's dimensions of national culture is
the silent source behind [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md): a large power distance, a
markedly low individualism (a collective, family-and-network cast), a strong
long-term orientation, and a low indulgence (disciplined restraint). The pitch is
written from this profile and never names a dimension or a number.

## History behind the plots

- **The cession and the free port** — the Opium Wars and the cession of Hong Kong
  Island (1842), Kowloon (1860), and the lease of the New Territories (1898); the
  founding of the colonial free port and entrepot.
- **The refugees and the factories** — the mid-twentieth-century waves of migration
  from the mainland, the squatter settlements and resettlement estates, and the
  post-war rise of light manufacturing and export industry.
- **The handover (1997)** — the Sino-British Joint Declaration (1984), the Basic
  Law, the transfer of sovereignty on 1 July 1997, and the "one country, two
  systems" framework with its fifty-year guarantee.
- **The contested autonomy** — the constitutional and civic disputes of the
  post-handover decades over the meaning and limits of the autonomy the handover
  promised.

## Personas from the public record

- **Bruce Lee (1940-1973)** — martial artist and film star who carried Hong Kong
  cinema to a global audience.
- **Louis Cha, pen name Jin Yong (1924-2018)** — wuxia novelist and co-founder of
  the newspaper Ming Pao.
- **Anita Mui (1963-2003)** — Cantopop singer and actress, called "the Daughter of
  Hong Kong."
- **Chris Patten (b. 1944)** — the twenty-eighth and last Governor of Hong Kong,
  who oversaw the transfer of sovereignty.

The refugee industrialist and the young Hongkonger are composite archetypes, not
individuals, drawn from the common experience of their generations.

## Geography and language

Geographic facts (ISO HK; the island, Kowloon, the New Territories, and the
outlying islands about Victoria Harbour) are on the public record. Chinese and
English are the official languages; Cantonese is the mother tongue of the
population, with standard written Chinese the written register and English the
language of commerce, law, and the former administration.
