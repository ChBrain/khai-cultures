# Piece: the Star Ferry

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

Crossing [Victoria Harbour](place_victoria_harbour.md) between Central on the Island and Tsim Sha Tsui in Kowloon since 1888, the green-and-white double-ended ferries have carried commuters, shoppers, and visitors back and forth for well over a century, long before any tunnel or railway went under the water. The crossing takes only a few minutes and costs only a few dollars, and from the open upper deck the whole skyline rises on one side and the peninsula on the other.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the harbour as the thing that had to be crossed before it was the thing to admire. Before bridges and tunnels stitched the two shores together underground, the Star Ferry was the only ordinary way over, and its cheapness and its plainness, wooden benches, open sides, the same crossing made a million times, carry the memory of a working harbour city where getting from one side to the other was daily business, not a view. As a deliberate survivor kept running alongside faster, newer crossings, it fits a city that keeps its old, useful things even as it builds past them, letting the low deck of a century-old ferry sit in the same water as the tallest towers on the shore.

## Apparent

A small green-and-white vessel, blunt at both ends so it never needs to turn, chugging across the harbour with its funnel and its open deck, indistinguishable at a glance from any modest passenger boat anywhere. In itself it is a short, cheap, unglamorous ride; its weight is entirely in how long it has been making that ride, and in how many of the city's own crossings, of class, of shore, of era, have happened on its benches.

## Yearbook

- Begun in 1888 as the first regular ferry service across the harbour, when Central and Tsim Sha Tsui had no other ordinary way to reach each other.
- Kept running through the growth of bridges, tunnels, and a cross-harbour railway that each, in turn, could have made it obsolete.
- Become the cheapest, plainest, and most enduring crossing of the harbour, and by that very plainness one of the city's most recognised rides.
