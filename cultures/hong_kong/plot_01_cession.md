# Plot: the cession and the free port

## Taxonomy

[play_hong_kong](play_hong_kong.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

Through the middle of the nineteenth century the Qing court and the British Empire fight two wars over trade, tribute, and opium, and lose them both. Out of the settlements that follow, Britain takes, piece by piece, a corner of the south China coast that the Qing court had called a barren rock: Hong Kong Island ceded in 1842, the Kowloon peninsula added in 1860, and the New Territories taken on a ninety-nine-year lease in 1898. What begins as a strip of imperial spoils sits on one of the deepest natural harbours on earth, and that harbour decides what the place becomes.

## Action

The British administration declares the new colony a free port, open to any flag and any cargo, and the trade of China that used to funnel through a single controlled channel now passes through Hong Kong instead. [The entrepot](position_the_entrepot.md) takes shape as the working instinct of the place: buy, sell, tranship, ask no questions about origin or destination, take the commission, move on. Ships from Europe, India, and the treaty ports crowd into [Victoria Harbour](place_victoria_harbour.md), and around its deep, sheltered water, godowns, docks, and a mixed population of merchants, sailors, and labourers grow up almost overnight. A rock ceded for want of anything better is turned, by the harbour beneath it and the trade routed through it, into the crossroads the whole later city is built on.

## Stage

- [the entrepot](position_the_entrepot.md): the trading instinct, open to any flag and any cargo, that turns a ceded rock into a working port.
- [Victoria Harbour](place_victoria_harbour.md): the deep, sheltered water that makes the cession worth having and the city possible at all.

## Tension

Nobody in the Qing court or the British Foreign Office expects the barren rock to matter for its own sake; it is taken, and kept, only for the harbour under it and the trade routed past it. The whole later city, its wealth, its mixed population, its habit of living off the deal rather than the land, is founded on that first, unsentimental calculation, and the free port built to serve an empire's trade becomes, almost as an afterthought, a place where people actually live.
