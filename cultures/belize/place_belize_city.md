## Shown

The old colonial port at the mouth of the Belize River, the low, hot, wooden city built on the silt and ballast of the logwood trade, where the British Honduras of the Baymen took shape and the colony was run. The swing bridge over Haulover Creek, the clapboard houses up pan stilts against the flood, the cathedral and the courthouse, the harbor where the mahogany was shipped and the Garifuna and the immigrant came ashore. The capital of the colony and the crucible of the nation, crowded and Creole, the city where George Price walked the streets and a people learned to call weself Belizean, and even after the capital moved inland, fi we own beating heart.

## Offers

A place where the colony became a country: the harbor where the many came ashore and the timber went out, the streets where the nationalists organized and the people gathered, the old capital that carries the whole memory of British Honduras and the birth of Belize. The crowded, Creole, sea-level heart of the nation, where its history is densest and the talk pan the corner is loudest.

## Withheld

The safety of solid ground. Belize City carries the gap between the colony's wealth and the cost of it, a port built on the logwood and mahogany trade and the slavery that did the cutting, a low city forever open to the hurricane and the rising sea, so battered by the storm of 1961 that the capital was carried inland, a heart that holds the nation's whole past and stands always at the water's mercy.
