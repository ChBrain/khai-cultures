# Plot: the Maya and the Baymen

## Cue

Long before any sail appeared, the forests and rivers were the heartland of the Maya, whose stone cities rose among the trees and whose people never wholly left them. Into that land, in the seventeenth century, came the British: not great colonizers but a few "Baymen," buccaneers turned woodcutters, drawn by the logwood that the dye trade wanted and then the mahogany the empire prized.

## Action

The Baymen settle the coast and work the rivers for timber, and they do the cutting on the labor of enslaved Africans carried across the sea, the wealth and the bondage grown from the same wood. The Spanish crown disputes the coast, and in 1798 the Baymen and their forces turn back a Spanish fleet at the Battle of St. George's Caye, the day that settles the territory as British and is remembered ever after as the colony's founding. Out of the logwood and the mahogany, the slavery and the battle, British Honduras takes shape - a colony built on the forest, run from a low port on the Caribbean, its first economy and its first deep wound laid down together.

## Tension

The colony is founded on the richest of forests and the hardest of foundations at once: a land that was the Maya heartland claimed for its timber, an economy built on the cutting, and the cutting done in slavery. The famous battle and the mahogany pride sit atop the labor that did the work and the people who held the land first, so British Honduras begins with both the wealth of the wood and the wound of how it was won, and the whole story after must hold the two together.
