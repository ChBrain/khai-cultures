# Persona: Thomas Vincent Ramos

## Taxonomy

[the plural society](position_the_plural_society.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The Garifuna leader, teacher, and activist who gave his people a day to be seen, the founder of Garifuna Settlement Day, the November observance that turned the memory of a landing into a nation's holiday. Devoted, organizing, and proud of a people too long unrecognized, he gathered the Garifuna of the southern towns, the people of Dangriga and Punta Gorda, and won them a place in the colony's calendar and conscience. The Garifuna tongue is his mother tongue, the language of Arawak, Carib, and West African roots that his people carried across the sea and held against every pressure: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) in it, the language of the punta drum, the dugu rite, and the home. The colony's English he [carries as his second tongue](process_speaking_carried.md) and [reads and follows](process_reading_followed.md), the official language he must petition the authorities in and teach his people to hold their ground in. He pleads for his mother tongue's people in the colonizer's English, the case for the Garifuna made in the very language that crowds the Garifuna tongue, so that to be seen at all he must cross a gap at the limit of the tongue, speaking for a language in a language not its own.

## Action

In [the Many Peoples](plot_02_the_many_peoples.md) he turns the Garifuna arrival into a remembered and celebrated thing, founding the Settlement Day that makes [the plural society](position_the_plural_society.md) include his people by name. By winning the Garifuna a day pan the nation's calendar, he makes all a we one without letting the Garifuna disappear into it.

## Shadow

The leader who wins his people a single day carries the shadow of the one day: the danger that recognition becomes a fete rather than a standing, the mother tongue celebrated once a year while it thins the rest of the year, and the long fear that a small people seen for an afternoon can still be unseen the morning after.

## Tell

He sets the date of the landing as a holiday and teaches his people to row the dugus ashore again each year, the drums going, so that a nation that might have forgotten the Garifuna must watch them arrive, in their own tongue, every November.
