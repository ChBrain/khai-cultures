# Position: all a we

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The belief, carried without ever choosing it, that a small land can hold many peoples as one without melting any of them down: Creole, Garifuna, Mestizo, Maya, and Mennonite, each keeping a tongue, a faith, and a day, under [Belizean English](position_language_en_bz.md) and the Kriol that crosses every line. It has [the plural society](position_the_plural_society.md) as its ground, [the Caribbean nation](position_the_caribbean_nation.md) as its late-won standing, and [the forest and the reef](position_the_forest_and_the_reef.md) as the wealth and the wound it must guard rather than spend. It carries the warmth of a stranger greeted like a friend, the ease of a church, a chapel, and a temple on the same street, and the plantation order it grew over, softened but never quite gone.

## Orders

That nobody's Belize is taken for the only Belize, so the Creole, the Garifuna, the Mestizo, the Maya, and the Mennonite are each fully Belizean and none a guest in fi we own land; that difference is lived beside rather than erased; that the smallness of the nation is defended, against the claim at the border and against the appetite of a world that wants a piece of the jewel; and that the many keep speaking for the many, in Kriol on the corner and in whatever tongue the house still holds.

## Loses

Ground when the easy plurality is mistaken for solved and the old hierarchy of color and origin hardens back beneath the warmth; ground when the smallness is taken for insignificance by the claimant who would erase the nation or by the market that would use it up; and ground when the forest and the reef are spent for the quick return, so the jewel that drew the world in is consumed to please it, wealth and wound cut from the same wood without the reckoning ever coming due.

## Drives

Lives in the founder who names a whole people Belizean and carries them to independence late but whole; in the tribune who will not let the young nation be born unguarded; in the leader who wins a day of arrival and remembrance so a people already home is finally seen; in the writer who sets the many lives of the country pan one page; and in the cutter whose forced labor and stolen tongues, fused with the master's English, forged the very Kriol that now holds all a we together. It drives Belize to keep believing that the many can be one without ceasing to be many, and to hold its smallness and its plurality not as weakness but as the whole of weh it is.
