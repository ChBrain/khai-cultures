# Piece: the mahogany tree

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

At the center of the nation's coat of arms and flag, held up by the two woodcutters with their axe and paddle, and standing still in the forests of the country it once made rich: the great hardwood tree whose timber built the colony, the national tree of Belize, and the emblem beneath the motto "Sub Umbra Floreo" - "Under the Shade I Flourish." On the flag, in the forest, and in the name of the trade that gave the land its first economy.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the wealth and the wound of the colony in a single tree. The mahogany the Baymen and the enslaved cut and shipped made British Honduras and gave it its place in the empire, the timber that built the port and filled the empire's drawing rooms; and the cutting of it was done in slavery, the riches and the bondage grown from the same wood. Belize put the tree at the heart of its arms and the woodcutters beside it, naming the labor and the timber as the nation's foundation - so the emblem carries the pride of the forest and, for those who look, the debt beneath it, the tree on the flag that cannot be honored without the hand that felled it. Without this tree the nation would have no symbol of the heritage that both made it and marked it.

## Apparent

A spreading hardwood, tall and broad-crowned, its timber a deep reddish brown prized the world over; on the arms, a stylized green tree above the shield, flanked by two woodcutters of the old trade. Plain enough as a tree and a badge; its weight is in the economy it built, the slavery it was cut in, and the motto of flourishing-under-shade the nation chose to write beneath it.

## Yearbook

- Cut as logwood and then mahogany by Baymen and enslaved Africans, the timber that built the colony of British Honduras.
- Set at the center of the coat of arms with the two woodcutters and the motto "Sub Umbra Floreo," the nation's founding image.
- Kept as the national tree of independent Belize, the emblem of a heritage that holds both the pride of the forest and the wound of the cutting.
