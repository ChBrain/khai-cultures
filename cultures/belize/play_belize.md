## Name

Belize, Bileez on the tongue, the only English-speaking nation of Central America, a small country on the Caribbean coast of the isthmus, tucked between Mexico to the north and Guatemala to the west and south, its face turned to the sea and the world's second-largest barrier reef. ISO BZ, official language English, but Belize Kriol is the lingua franca weh all a we chat, with Spanish, Garifuna, the Maya tongues, and Plautdietsch all alive in the same small land. A nation that holds itself together not by one people but by plenty living side by side, gud neighbors on the same street.

## Arc

A land that begins as the heartland of the Maya, whose stone cities rose in its forests long before any sail appeared, and who never wholly left. Into that forest came the British: not a great colony of settlers but a few "Baymen," logwood and then mahogany cutters who worked the rivers for the dye and the timber the empire wanted, and who did that work on the backs of enslaved Africans carried across the sea. The Battle of St. George's Caye in 1798 settled the coast as British, and British Honduras took shape, a sliver of empire run from a low, hot port on the Caribbean. Then the many came: the Garifuna landing on the southern shore in 1832 with the drum and the punta, Mestizo refugees crossing from the north with their Spanish, the Maya holding the west and south, the Mennonites arriving to farm, the East Indian and the rest folding in, until the land held Creole, Garifuna, Mestizo, Maya, and Mennonite all at once, an easy plurality grown over the old plantation order. Late among nations it found its own voice. George Price and Philip Goldson led it from colony to nation, independence coming only in 1981, and coming under the long shadow of Guatemala's claim to the whole territory. Today it is a Caribbean nation in a Hispanic isthmus, a member of CARICOM, guarding the reef and the rainforest the world prizes, the jewel everybody want a piece of, and weighing the welcome that made it against the neighbor that still claims it. Under it all runs one nerve: a warm, easygoing, plural people, Kriol on the tongue and plenty tongues in the land, who believe all a we, the many, can be one.

## Stakes
