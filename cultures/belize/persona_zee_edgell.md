# Persona: Zee Edgell

## Taxonomy

[the plural society](position_the_plural_society.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The novelist who gave Belize its literary voice, the author of "Beka Lamb," the story of a Creole girl coming of age in a colony coming of age, the book that put the small nation pan the page and in the world's hands. Watchful, exact, and tender toward her own, she wrote the inner life of a plural people at the hinge of nationhood and made a literature where there had been a colony with none. English she has whole and shaping: she [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) and [writes](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) in the [mother tongue of the Creole streets](position_language_en_bz.md), the standard English of the page laid over the Kriol of the talk, and on the page she [catches](process_reading_caught.md) the cadence of how Belizeans actually chat, setting the warm lilt of Kriol into the English line. She read the wider world's literature and [followed it](process_reading_followed.md), and answered it with a Belize of her own. She wrote her small, many-tongued country into English so the world could read it, and into the Kriol music beneath that English so all a we could hear weself, and yet the Garifuna drum and the Maya word and the Mestizo Spanish pressed at the edges of even her page, the whole plural nation never quite containable in one tongue, so her art works always against a gap at the limit of the tongue.

## Action

In [the Reef and the Nation](plot_04_the_reef_and_the_nation.md) she gives the young, plural country a literature and a self-portrait, holding the many lives of [the plural society](position_the_plural_society.md) pan one page and letting Belize see and read itself. By writing the nation into the world's libraries, she makes the small country legible to the world and to itself.

## Shadow

The writer who renders a people carries the shadow of the single frame: the danger that one novel becomes the nation's whole image abroad, the Creole story taken for the only story, the Garifuna and the Maya and the Mestizo left off the page, and the plural country fixed in the world's mind by the page that named it, the many flattened into the one the world happened to read.

## Tell

She writes a Creole girl's small colonial life so truly that a nation finds itself in it, and lets the Kriol slip into the English so the talk on the page sounds like the talk pan the street.
