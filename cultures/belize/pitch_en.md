# Pitch: the Belizean tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Belize](play_belize.md) is played. It changes nothing of weh happens, not the Maya and the Baymen, not the many peoples, not the road to independence, not the reef and the nation. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which all a it is given.

## Tenor

Played warm, easygoing, and unhurried, in Belize Kriol cadence over the old British forms: a small people who take the day as it comes, greet a stranger like a friend, and move at the pace of the heat and the tide. How unu di do, they say, and they mean it. Plural to the bone, they slip between tongues in a single sentence and live easy beside a neighbor of another people and another faith, the church and the chapel and the temple all pan the same street. Hospitable, good-humored, quick to laugh and slow to hurry, they hold their many origins lightly and proudly at once, and carry the courtesy of the old colony loosened into the ease of the Caribbean. Under the calm runs a steady, knowing patience, the small nation's habit of getting along, of holding all a we as one.

## Undertow

Under the easy plurality runs the old plantation hierarchy it grew over, the colony's order of color and origin not gone but softened, and the quiet question of whose Belize it most is. There is the smallness that must always watch a far larger neighbor's claim and a far larger world's appetite for fi we reef and forest, the worry of a place loved by outsiders for weh they can take from it. And under the warmth runs the strain of holding so many peoples as one without any of them disappearing, the easygoing pace shading into the fear that weh is not hurried may be left undone.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of the many made one and the welcome held open: the conviction that peoples can live side by side without becoming each other, that the land is large enough in spirit for Creole and Garifuna and Mestizo and Maya and Mennonite alike, and that a small place can be its own. It plays on the pride of the only English-speaking nation of the isthmus, the Kriol all a we shares, and the deep ease of a people who have learned to get along. It plays, too, on the ache underneath, of being small beside the claim, and of guarding the jewel the whole world wants a piece of.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a warm, plural, unhurried people: many-tongued and many-peopled, easy with difference and proud of the mix, set between a forest and a reef the world covets and a neighbor whose claim never quite lifts. A nation that runs on the promise that all a we, the many, can be one without ceasing to be many, and that holds its smallness and its plurality not as weakness but as the whole of weh it is.
