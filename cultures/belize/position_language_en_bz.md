# Position: Belizean English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The one tongue that runs the length of this small nation and lets its many peoples speak as one: the English of the empire that cut the mahogany here, settled on the Caribbean coast, made official, and grown into something the country's own. It holds two registers braided into a single voice. Above sits the standard English of the lectern, the law, the newspaper, and the schoolroom, the language of the colony that stayed to run the nation. Beneath it, and closer to the heart, runs Belize Kriol, fi we own tongue, the English-lexified creole forged in the forest and on the plantation from the master's English and the African tongues the cutters carried, the lingua franca weh all a we chat across every ethnic line. Neither is complete without the other. Belizean English is the whole of that braid, the tongue in which a Creole and a Garifuna and a Mestizo and a Maya and a Mennonite, each answering at home in a language of their own, still meet on the same street and understand one another.

## Orders

What counts as the national voice: the English of the official record, the constitution, and the classroom, and the Kriol of the yard, the corner, and the song. It orders which tongue carries the business of the state and which carries the warmth of the people, and it sets the one register the whole plural land shares over the many it does not. It makes English the door every Belizean must pass through to be schooled, employed, and heard abroad, and it makes Kriol the sound of being at home, the tongue that tells a Belizean from a stranger, so that the nation writes itself in one register and hears itself in the other.

## Loses

Ground, always, to the tongues it stands over. It loses to the Spanish rising from the north and the isthmus around it, the language of the mainland pressing in on a Caribbean country set in a Hispanic land. It loses to the mother tongues it never replaced: the Garifuna of the southern shore, the Maya of the west and south, the Plautdietsch of the Mennonite fields, each held at home against it. And within itself it is torn two ways: the standard English is faulted as the colonizer's leftover, too foreign to be fully fi we, while the Kriol is still half-denied a place in print and school, spoken by everyone and written by few, so the national tongue loses even to its own lower half, the language all a we chat that the nation is slow to let itself write down.

## Drives

It lives in [George Price](persona_george_price.md), who gave a Caribbean people their nationhood in the English of the empire he was leaving and named them Belizean in the Kriol they actually chat, founding the nation in one register and christening it in the other. It lives in [Philip Goldson](persona_philip_goldson.md), who wrote the newspapers and speeches that woke a colony to itself, the English page read out loud in Kriol pan the corner, turning the ruler's own tongue into the tool of the ruled. It lives in [the mahogany cutter](persona_the_mahogany_cutter.md), in whose mouth the tongue was made at all: forbidden his African languages and forced the master's English, he and his fellows forged fi we own Kriol out of the gap between them, so that the nation's shared voice was first spoken by the enslaved who had every other voice taken. It lives in [Thomas Vincent Ramos](persona_thomas_vincent_ramos.md), who carried it as his second tongue and pleaded for the Garifuna in it, making the case for one people's language in the very language that shares the land with it. And it lives in [Zee Edgell](persona_zee_edgell.md), who wrote the small, many-tongued country into standard English so the world could read it and let the Kriol music slip beneath the line so all a we could hear weself. Belizean English drives this land to hold its many peoples as one people, speaking one tongue in two registers, an official English that opens the door to the world and a Kriol that keeps the door home, and it carries the same unfinished debt in every mouth: the whole plural Belize that no single tongue, not even this shared one, wholly speaks.
