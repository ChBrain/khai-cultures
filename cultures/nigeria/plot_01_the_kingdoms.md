# Plot: the kingdoms before

## Cue

Long before any map ever wrote the word Nigeria, the peoples of this land had built worlds of their own. The story does not begin with the colonizer; it begins centuries earlier, in the forest and the savannah, where great kingdoms, city-states, and republics rose, traded, worshipped, and made art that the world would one day come, cap in hand, to see.

## Action

In the southwest the Yoruba honour [Ile-Ife](place_ile_ife.md) as the cradle where, they say, the world itself was made, and from old Oyo the Alaafin rules a wide empire of horse and trade. To the south the Edo kingdom of Benin casts in bronze and brass the heads and plaques that astonish the world to this day. In the east the Igbo govern their towns not by kings but by the gathering of the people, the elders and the age-grades, holding that Igbo enwe eze; and at Nri a priestly authority radiates without an army. In the north the Hausa city-states trade across the Sahara, and after the jihad of Usman dan Fodio the Sokoto Caliphate rises, with the great walled city of Kano at the heart of its commerce and learning, while Borno of the Kanuri endures as one of the oldest states on the continent. Many peoples, many crowns and many councils, each a full world, all of them what one Nigeria will one day be asked to hold.

## Stage

- [Ile-Ife](place_ile_ife.md): the Yoruba cradle, honoured as the place where the world began and the kingdoms took their pattern.
- [the peoples](position_the_peoples.md): the over two hundred and fifty nations, each with its own ancient order, that the country will later have to make one.
- [the Benin bronzes](piece_the_benin_bronzes.md): the masterworks the kingdoms made, proof of a civilization whole long before any colonizer arrived.

## Tension

The land was never an empty page on which a country was simply drawn; it was already crowded with greatness, each people sure of its own worth and its own way. That richness is the giant's deepest glory and its standing puzzle at once, for the very fact that so many full and proud worlds were here first is what makes one Nigeria so hard, and so worth, the building.
