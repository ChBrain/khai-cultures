## Cue

After the long colonial chapter, the wind of change blows across Africa, and Nigeria, the most populous land on the continent, prepares to take its own destiny in hand. On the first of October 1960 the green-white-green flag is raised, and a young country, made of so many peoples, must now learn the hardest lesson of all: how to be one nation.

## Action

Nigeria wins its independence in 1960 and a few years later becomes a republic, full of hope and the swagger of a giant finding its feet. But a country amalgamated on paper has to be made one in the heart, and the strain of holding so many peoples together proves heavy. The young nation passes through a sober and sorrowful chapter, a civil war in the late 1960s that cost the country dearly and that it grieves to this day with dignity and with a steady refusal to glory in the pain or to relive it in detail. Out of that grief the country drew a hard resolve, captured in the watchword that came after, to keep Nigeria one, and the long work of reconciliation and of rebuilding the trust among the peoples began. [Chinua Achebe](persona_achebe.md), an Igbo writer who lived through it, became one of the honest keepers of that memory, neither forgetting it nor inflaming it.

## Stage

- [Chinua Achebe](persona_achebe.md): the writer and witness who keeps the memory of the young nation honestly, both its glory and its grief.
- [the peoples](position_the_peoples.md): the many nations who, having become one country, had to learn at great cost to remain one.
- [Lagos](place_lagos.md): the capital of the new republic, the seat from which the country governed itself for the first time.

## Tension

Independence gives Nigeria its freedom and its deepest test in the same breath: a giant of many peoples, free at last, must hold itself together as one. The sober chapter it passed through is borne with dignity and without blame laid at any one door, and the country carries it not as a wound to reopen but as a solemn lesson, that the union, once kept, must be kept, and that the many are to be held as one with care, so that such sorrow is never courted again.
