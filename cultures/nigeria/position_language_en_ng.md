# Position: Nigerian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Nigerian English carries syllable-timed rhythm inherited from the country's tonal mother tongues, each syllable given roughly equal weight rather than the stressed-unstressed alternation of British or American speech, so its cadence sounds even and measured to an ear trained on the stress-timed original. Beside it, Nigerian Pidgin holds serial verb constructions and reduplication borrowed straight from Igbo, Yoruba, and Hausa grammar, waka waka for wandering about, sweet sweet for something deeply pleasing, a way of intensifying and narrating action that standard English syntax has no equivalent construction for.

## Orders

Standard English still forces tense marked on every finite verb and a stated subject in every clause, a discipline the schoolroom and the exam paper enforce on top of whatever the mother tongue does differently. Pidgin, in turn, forces its own grammar, verbs carry no tense inflection at all and instead stack in sequence, one after another, to narrate a string of actions as English alone cannot without conjunctions, and reduplication is the obligatory way to intensify a word, not an optional flourish.

## Loses

Standard English cannot carry the tonal contrasts that Igbo and Yoruba use to distinguish one word from another by pitch alone, so proverbs and names that depend on tone for their exact sense lose that whole dimension the moment they are rendered only in English's toneless script. The dense, condensed proverb, the palm oil with which words are eaten, translates into English only as a much longer, flatter sentence, the compression itself is what does not survive the crossing. And Pidgin's serial-verb narration and reduplicated intensifiers have no standard-English construction to fall back on, so a speaker moving from Pidgin into formal English has to give up the very grammar that let the thought move so quickly.

## Drives

It trains a speaker to hold two grammars side by side, one that inflects for tense and demands a subject in every clause, another that strings bare verbs in sequence and repeats a word to make it stronger, switching between them by audience rather than by translation. It carries the even, syllable-timed rhythm of the mother tongues into English prosody regardless of which grammar is running underneath. And it leaves whoever grows up moving between the two able to compress a whole narrated sequence into a run of bare verbs, an economy the single-grammar English speaker has no equivalent shortcut for.
