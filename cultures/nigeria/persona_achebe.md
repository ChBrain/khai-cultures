# Persona: Chinua Achebe

## Owner

## Projection

The father of modern African literature, the Igbo son of Ogidi who, with one slim novel, told the whole world a thing it badly needed to hear: that his people had a deep, ordered, dignified world long before any white man set foot among them, with its own proverbs, its own gods, its own justice, and that the coming of the colonizer did not bring civilization to a people without one but broke a civilization already whole. Wise, measured, and unhurried, with the gravity of a true elder, he wrote so that the story of Africa would no longer be told only by the hunter. His mother tongue is Igbo, which he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) as the very air of Ogidi, and whose proverbs, he said, are the palm-oil with which words are eaten. English he took as the colonizer's tongue and made it carry the weight of Igbo thought, [writing in a drafted English](process_writing_drafted.md) bent and seasoned until it could hold the rhythm of his people, [reading and following](process_reading_followed.md) the great books of the wider world and answering them back. He chose to write in the master's language so the world would listen, knowing the old proverbs would not sit fully at home in it, and so he made English stretch to carry what it was not built to hold, the gap at the limit of the tongue lying just where the Igbo proverb meets the English page.

## Action

In [independence and the war](plot_03_independence_and_war.md) he is the keeper of the story, the one who set down both the glory of his people and, with a heavy and honest heart, the sober sorrow the young country passed through, refusing both to forget and to inflame. He carries [the peoples](position_the_peoples.md) into the world's reading by insisting that each of Nigeria's nations is a full and dignified world, and that one Nigeria can only be built if none of them is told its story does not count.

## Shadow

The writer who gives his people back their dignity must also count the cost of the union honestly, and there the shadow falls: the temptation to tell only the proud half and bury the painful one, or to let the wound of one's own people harden into a quarrel with the country. Achebe walks the narrow road between, neither flattering Nigeria nor abandoning it, and the strain of that road is the country's own.

## Tell

He opens the modern African novel with a man's whole world falling apart, and tells it not as the colonizer's triumph but as a people's tragedy, putting more truth of his nation into one fictional village than a shelf of colonial reports ever held.
