# Position: the peoples

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The plain, founding fact of Nigeria: that it is not one people but more than two hundred and fifty, each with its own tongue, its own land, its own history older than the country. The Yoruba of the west with Ile-Ife and old Oyo; the Igbo of the east who ran their towns by the gathering of the people and held that Igbo enwe eze, the Igbo have no king; the Hausa-Fulani of the north with the emirates of the Sokoto Caliphate and the great trading city of Kano; the Edo of Benin who cast the bronzes; the Kanuri of Borno, the Ijaw of the creeks, the Tiv, the Nupe, the Efik, the Ibibio, and a long roll call besides. The British drew one border round all of them in 1914 and called it Nigeria, and ever since the country has been the great work of being one out of so very many.

## Orders

That to be Nigerian is to be two things at once and proud of both, fully your own people and fully of the one country, the Yoruba man Yoruba to the marrow and Nigerian to the marrow with no contradiction; that no single people owns the house, and the giant belongs to all its tenants; that difference is a richness, a country of many tongues and many dishes and many drums, the unity-in-diversity the green-white-green flag was raised over; and that the long argument over how the many shall share the one is itself the country's central, never-finished business.

## Loses

Ground when the many is set against the one, when a person is reduced to only his tribe and the country forgotten, when the question of who gets what is asked along the lines of blood instead of the common good; and ground, too, when the diversity that should be a feast is treated as a threat, so that suspicion of the other people, the one who is not your own, eats the brotherhood that holds the giant together.

## Drives

Lives in the Igbo writer's telling that his people had a deep, ordered world long before any colonizer arrived, and who weighed, with a heavy heart, the hard cost of holding the many as one; and in the Yoruba musician's song in the Pidgin that belongs to every people at once, the one tongue the whole giant shares. It drives Nigeria to keep being one country of many nations, neither melting them down nor letting them fly apart.
