# Persona: Wole Soyinka

## Projection

The playwright, poet, and lion of the Nigerian conscience, the Yoruba son of Abeokuta who in 1986 became the first African to win the Nobel Prize for Literature, and who all his long life has refused to keep quiet when a tyrant wanted silence. Fierce, brilliant, and stubborn as iron, with a great white mane and a tongue that fears no power, he reached past the church and the mosque to the older Yoruba gods, and made of Ogun, the god of iron, of the road, and of creation through destruction, the very engine of his drama. His mother tongue is Yoruba, which he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in, and whose deep cosmology, its orisa and its rituals, he carries as the marrow of his work. [Nigerian English](position_language_en_ng.md) he [writes in a high and deliberate drafted](process_writing_drafted.md) hand, dense as a thicket, and reads the European canon to [follow it and answer it](process_reading_followed.md), bending the Greek tragic mask onto a Yoruba face. He set the Yoruba gods on the world stage in the colonizer's English, and there is the gap at the limit of the tongue, for the masquerade and the drumbeat and the trance of the shrine never translate whole, and the deepest mystery of Ogun stays, in the end, in Yoruba. He belongs to [Naija](position_culture_naija.md) as the conscience who lifts the ancestral way up beside the church and the mosque as one more pillar of the same believing people.

## Action

In [the giant roars](plot_04_the_giant_roars.md) he is the conscience and the crown of the country's letters, the Nobel that told the world African literature stands with any on earth, and the voice that would not be bought or frightened. He carries [faith](position_faith.md) by lifting the ancestral Yoruba way up beside the church and the mosque, insisting that the gods of the land are no superstition to be ashamed of but a living wisdom worthy of the highest art.

## Shadow

The artist who is also the nation's conscience pays for it, and the shadow is the lonely cost of the one who speaks when others duck: prison, exile, and the weariness of always being the one to say what power does not want said. And there is the harder shadow of the prophet whose own people do not always heed him, the lion roaring in a house that sometimes turns away.

## Tell

He goes to prison rather than swallow his words, and writes his way out, proving that in Nigeria the pen of one stubborn man can outlast the boot of any strongman who thinks he can shut it.
