# Pitch: the Nigerian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Nigeria](play_nigeria.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the kingdoms, not the amalgamation, not the war, not the rise of the giant. It sets only how the whole thing sounds, the register and the swagger in which the show is given.

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played loud, warm, and full of life, the way a Lagos street is full before the day even starts. Here a person is never alone but always somebody's son, somebody's daughter, somebody's in-law, held inside the family and the community that come first before the self. The elder is greeted on the knees or with both hands; the visitor is fed before he is asked his business; the stranger is called brother. People talk in proverb and in indirection, going round a matter the way you walk round a compound to enter by the proper door, because the one who throws a stone in the market does not know whose mother he has hit. There is hierarchy and there is respect, but there is also the hustle that levels everybody: the trader, the okada man, the big oga and the small boy all chasing the same daily bread, and all sure that with God and small-small patience, tomorrow go better. How far, my brother. No wahala. We move.

## Undertow

Under the laughter runs the weight of carrying so many on one back: the man who makes it must feed the whole village, and the burden of the extended family can grind the one who rose to the top. The respect for the elder and the big man can shade into a fear of speaking up, where the junior swallows his truth and the powerful are not checked. The faith that runs so deep can be leaned on to bear what should be fixed by hand, the everything-go-better-by-God that waits when it should work. And the very bigness, the giant of Africa swagger, can curdle into a country that knows it is great and aches at the gap between that greatness and the daily grind, the abundance of a blessed land that does not always reach the table of the ordinary person.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of the family that never lets you fall and the country that refuses to carry last: the certainty that you are not one person but a whole lineage, that whatever happens your people are behind you, that the door of the home is always open and the pot always has small extra. It plays on the swagger of belonging to the giant, the joy that bursts out at every owambe and every Super Eagles goal, the entrepreneur's faith that there is money in every market for the one who hustles, and the deep, lived trust that God dey, that the same God who brought you this far will not abandon you halfway. And it plays on the ache underneath, the proverb-quiet knowledge that the one who eats alone dies alone.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people impossible to ignore: warm, loud, faithful, generous, and proud, who carry one another and carry their country, who turn suffering into a song and a hustle into a calling, and who will tell you with their whole chest, looking you dead in the eye, that whatever else may be, naija no dey carry last.
