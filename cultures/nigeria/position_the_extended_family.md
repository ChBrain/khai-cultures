# Position: the extended family

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The truth that in Nigeria a person is never one person but a whole lineage walking about. The family is not the small thing of mother, father, and two children; it is the grandparents, the uncles and aunties who are also called father and mother, the cousins who are called brother and sister, the in-laws, the village, the kindred stretching out wide. Your auntie's child is your sibling; your father's brother is your father; the elder is honoured with both hands or on the knees and his word carries weight, for as the people say, what an old man sees sitting down a young man cannot see standing up. Out of this comes the great noise of Nigerian togetherness: the naming ceremony when a child is born, the aso-ebi when the family wears one cloth to show it is one body, the owambe party that swallows a whole weekend, and the open door where the visitor eats first and is asked his mission after.

## Orders

That you owe your people and your people owe you, so the one who rises must lift the others, pay the school fees, settle the in-laws, and never eat alone; that age is to be respected and the elder deferred to, for he carries the wisdom of the road already walked; that the individual matters most as a thread in the cloth of the family, and shame and pride are shared by the whole house; and that no joy and no sorrow is private, every birth, marriage, and burial belonging to the entire kindred who gather to mark it.

## Loses

Ground when the duty to the family hardens into a weight that crushes the one who rose, drained by the many hands held out; when respect for the elder shades into a silence where the junior dares not speak even the needed truth; and ground, too, when the warmth that should be a shelter turns inward against the outsider, so that the wide embrace of one's own people leaves no room for the stranger who is not kin.

## Drives

Lives in [Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie](persona_adichie.md), who writes the inner lives of women inside the great web of family and tradition, honouring it and questioning it in the same breath; and in [Mama Market](persona_mama_market.md), the trader who feeds not only her own children but a whole compound of relatives on what she turns over at her stall. It drives Nigeria to hold the person inside the family, the family inside the community, and the community inside the giant, so that nobody is ever truly alone.
