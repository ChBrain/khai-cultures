# Piece: the Benin bronzes

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On the walls and altars of the old royal court of the Kingdom of Benin, and today, sorrowfully, in the glass cases of museums far across the sea: thousands of plaques and heads and figures cast in bronze and brass by the master guilds of Benin City, recording the kings, the court, and the history of the kingdom in metal. Works of an art so fine that when the wider world first saw them it could scarcely believe an African kingdom had made them, and that it has taken Nigeria a long campaign even to begin to bring some of them home.

## Load Bearing

They stand for the deep, ancient greatness of the peoples who were here long before any Nigeria. The bronzes are proof, cast in metal that does not lie, that this land carried high civilizations, master craft, and ordered courts centuries before the colonizer arrived to claim he found none. They carry the giant's pride in its own deep past, and also its ache, for they were carried off and not freely given, and they stand now far from the altars they were made for. To Nigeria they are both a glory to point to and a debt the world still owes. Without these works the country's claim to an ancient, equal greatness would lose its surest witness.

## Apparent

Plaques and heads of dark bronze and brass, some green with age, cast by the lost-wax method to a smoothness and a likeness that astonish the eye, kings in coral regalia, court attendants, leopards, and the long record of a kingdom in metal. Just old castings in a case, to the careless glance; their whole weight is in the civilization that made them, the manner of their leaving, and the homecoming the country still seeks for them.

## Yearbook

- Cast over centuries by the master bronze guilds of the Kingdom of Benin to record its kings and its court.
- Carried out of Benin City at the end of the nineteenth century and scattered into museums and collections far across the world.
- Made the object of a long Nigerian campaign for their return, the slow, dignified bringing-home of the country's own.
