# Play: Antigua and Barbuda

## Name

Antigua and Barbuda, a two-island nation in the eastern Caribbean, right at the top of the Leeward Islands where the Atlantic meets the Caribbean Sea. The larger isle, Antigua - the Arawak called her Wadadli, and we call her so still - ringed by reefs and counted at three hundred and sixty-five beaches, a beach for every day of the year; the lower, flatter sister, Barbuda, a frigate-bird sanctuary held on common land; and the small uninhabited Redonda between them. ISO AG, language English, with a sweet Antiguan Creole talked in every yard. A nation that came up out of the sugar estates and made itself free.

## Arc

A story that begins long before the cane: the Siboney and then the Arawak settled these islands and named them - Wadadli for the bigger one - fishing the reefs and working the land. The English came in the seventeenth century and made Antigua a sugar colony, the cane covering the island and the great estates - Betty's Hope chief among them - running on the labour of enslaved Africans carried across the sea. At English Harbour the Royal Navy built Nelson's Dockyard, the careening base Britain held the Caribbean from, while on the estates the gap between the crown's prosperity and the bondage that paid for it ran wide. The enslaved never accepted it: in 1736 Prince Klaas, the one they called King Court, stood at the head of a planned island-wide rising for freedom, and though the plot was broken his name, not the planters', became the nation's. Emancipation came in 1834, and the freed people built villages and a life of their own on small wages and hard ground. Out of that came the great labour movement of the twentieth century, the trade union that gave the people their first power and carried Vere Bird and his generation to the head of the nation; independence followed in 1981. A small place, but oh, a wide reach: Antigua sent out Sir Vivian Richards, the Master Blaster the cricketing world feared and loved, and the writer Jamaica Kincaid, whose books told the island plain-plain. The modern nation lives on its beaches and its visitors, on the Antigua Carnival that dances the memory of emancipation in calypso and mas, on Sailing Week out of the old dockyard - while Barbuda, the sister isle, holds its land in common and was emptied and battered by Hurricane Irma in 2017 and is rebuilding still. Under it all runs one nerve: an easy, cricket-proud, yardie-warm people who know exactly what their freedom cost and mean to keep it.

## Stakes

Whether a likkle two-island nation, free now of the sugar economy that built it and bound it, can share the wealth its beaches earn beyond the lucky few, can rebuild and keep faith with Barbuda and the common land of the sister isle, can honour the labour and the emancipation that made it rather than cut its whole past down to a backdrop for visitors, and can keep its outsized voice in the world - in cricket, in letters, in song - or whether the pretty paradise sold to the world is covering over a inequality and a neglect that the freedom of 1834 and 1981 was meant to put an end to.
