# Plot: the Beaches and Barbuda

## Cue

The free nation, no longer a colony of cane, must make its living a whole new way. Antigua counts three hundred and sixty-five beaches, one for every single day of the year - a beach for every day, as we like to say - and the world wants them bad; tourism becomes the nation's livelihood, the resorts rising up along the shore and the visitors arriving by the planeload and the cruise ship.

## Action

The modern nation lives on its beaches, its harbours, and its visitors, and trades the warm welcome of a easy, sociable island for the wealth that keeps it going - while underneath runs the question of who really shares in that wealth, the resorts plenty times running right past the same yards that staff them. Across the channel the sister isle, Barbuda, keeps its own way: land held in common by all Barbudan people, the great frigate-bird sanctuary on Codrington Lagoon, a quieter and a poorer life. In 2017 Hurricane Irma strikes Barbuda head-on and empties it clean, the whole population carried over to Antigua, and the long work of rebuilding begins - the common land, the houses, the sanctuary - with the sister isle watching close to see whether the wide-reaching nation will reach back across the water to the smallest of we own.

- [a small place, a wide reach](position_a_small_place_a_wide_reach.md): the wide-reaching small nation, asked to turn its reach back toward home.

## Tension

The nation sells the world a paradise of beaches and lives off it, and the same pretty image that earns its keep can wipe away the people who live and work behind it and run the wealth straight past their yards. And the wide reach that carries the island's name across the world can spend itself all outward while the sister isle, lower and farther and storm-struck, rebuilds its common land alone - the small place's deepest test being whether it will remember the smaller place that is its very own.
