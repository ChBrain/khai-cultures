# Plot: Independence and the Field

## Owner

## Cue

By the late twentieth century the long road from emancipation through the labour movement and self-government reaches its end at last. On the first of November, 1981, Antigua and Barbuda takes its independence and becomes a sovereign nation - a likkle two-island country newly free to carry its own name out into the world.

## Action

The free nation shows the world just how wide a small place can reach. On the cricket field Sir Vivian Richards masters the game, batting the island's name onto every ground on earth as part of the great West Indies side - and when the Master Blaster put bat to ball, all Wadadli stop to watch - and a long line of Antiguan cricketers comes up behind him. On the page Jamaica Kincaid tells the small place plain-plain, her books read clear across the world, refusing the postcard and naming the colonial wound and the sold paradise alike. A nation counted in the tens of thousands sends out a voice the whole earth hears - in the bat, in the book, in the song - the proof that smallness of size is no smallness of spirit at all.

## Stage

- [Vivian Richards](persona_vivian_richards.md): the master batsman who carries the newly free nation's name onto the world's cricket grounds.
- [Jamaica Kincaid](persona_jamaica_kincaid.md): the writer who tells the small place plain and carries it into the world's reading.
- [Nelson's Dockyard](place_nelsons_dockyard.md): the old colonial base, now a restored national landmark of the free nation that came up out of the cane.

## Tension

The small free nation reaches the wide world by mastery - the famous bat, the famous book - and risks being known abroad for nothing but them, the island shrunk down to its celebrated sons and its sold shores while the people who raised them stay back in the shadow. The wide reach is the small place's pride and its temptation both: to spend itself all outward on the world's notice, and to forget to turn that reach back toward the home and the sister isle that need it bad.
