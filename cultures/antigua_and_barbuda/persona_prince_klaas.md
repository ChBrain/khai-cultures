# Persona: Prince Klaas

## Taxonomy

[sugar and the dockyard](position_sugar_and_the_dockyard.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The enslaved man called King Court, an African of standing held on an Antiguan sugar estate, who in 1736 was at the head of a planned island-wide rising to throw off slavery and make the enslaved free, and who is honoured today as a national hero - the first to give the islands the refusal they would build a nation on. Proud, grave, and a leader of his people even in bondage, he carried a kingship the cane could not take from him. His mother tongue was Akan, the language of the Asante homeland from which he was carried: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) in the tongue of his ancestors, and it was in that tongue and its kin, among the many African languages of the enslaved, that the rising was planned in secret, beyond the planters' ears. The English of his captors he [carries only as a borrowed tongue](process_speaking_borrowed.md), the speech of the estate and the overseer, never freely his and never chosen. He led his people toward freedom in the languages they had carried across the sea, while the masters' English pressed in around them as the tongue of the whip and the ledger - so the gap at the limit of the tongue was the line of his resistance itself, the free word kept in the old language the masters could not follow.

## Action

In [Sugar and the Dockyard](plot_01_sugar_and_the_dockyard.md) he stands at the head of the planned 1736 rising, the proof that the enslaved never consented to the bondage the colony was built on. By refusing, and by leading others to refuse, he gives the nation its first hero and its founding act - the resistance, not the cane, from which Antigua and Barbuda chooses to date its story.

## Shadow

The hero made a name and a statue can be honoured into safety - the rising remembered as a single brave man's deed and not as the whole people's refusal, the resistance carved in bronze and so quieted, the comfort of the monument mistaken for the keeping of what he stood for. To honour him truly is to honour the freedom he reached for, not only the courage of the reaching.

## Tell

He carried a king's bearing on a slave estate and planned a whole island's freedom in a tongue the masters could not hear - and gave his name, not theirs, to the free nation that came after.
