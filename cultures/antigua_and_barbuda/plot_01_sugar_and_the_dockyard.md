# Plot: Sugar and the Dockyard

## Taxonomy

[play_antigua_and_barbuda](play_antigua_and_barbuda.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

Long before the cane, the Siboney and then the Arawak settled and named these islands, fishing the reefs and working the land. In the seventeenth century the English came, found the soil and the climate made for sugar, and turned Antigua into a colony of cane - the estates spreading, the great houses rising, and at English Harbour the Royal Navy choosing a sheltered base from which to hold the Caribbean.

## Action

The cane covers the island and the estates - Betty's Hope chief among them - run on the labour of enslaved Africans carried across the sea, the colony's wealth raised on their bondage. At English Harbour the navy builds Nelson's Dockyard, the Georgian careening base from which Britain guards its Caribbean trade. The gap between the crown's prosperity and the people who paid for it runs wide, and the enslaved do not accept it: in 1736 Prince Klaas, called King Court, is at the head of a planned island-wide rising for freedom. The plot is discovered and broken before it can rise, but the refusal it carried becomes the founding act of the nation that would come.

## Stage

- [Prince Klaas](persona_prince_klaas.md): the enslaved leader who stands at the head of the planned 1736 rising and gives the islands their first refusal.
- [Nelson's Dockyard](place_nelsons_dockyard.md): the Georgian naval base at English Harbour from which Britain held the Caribbean and guarded the sugar trade.
- [sugar and the dockyard](position_sugar_and_the_dockyard.md): the colonial foundation of cane and navy, built on bondage and refused from within.

## Tension

The islands are built into a colony of beauty and profit - the cane, the great houses, the Georgian dockyard the world now admires - and built on a bondage that was never consented to. The same estates that made the colony's wealth made the resistance that would unmake it, so the foundation holds both the chains and the first reach for freedom, and the nation that comes after chooses to date itself from the rising and not from the cane.
