# Position: Antiguan English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

What the Creole spoken in every Antiguan yard can do that the schoolroom English beside it cannot in a single word: mark a habitual action with the bare particle does, he does go meaning he goes as a rule, without needing a separate adverb to say so, and gather a whole group with dem, the man dem, in place of an added ending. Standard English, alongside it, keeps its wide borrowed vocabulary and its freedom to turn any noun into a verb.

## Orders

In the schoolroom register, a fixed subject verb object order and the auxiliary do to ask a question or deny a thing. In the yard Creole, a small word set before the verb, does, done, going, to carry tense and aspect that the verb's own ending no longer marks, since the verb itself stays the same shape whoever or whenever is speaking.

## Loses

The Creole drops the little verb to be where standard English requires it, she nice needs no is, an economy other tongues mistake for carelessness though it follows its own rule every time. And when that yard grammar is shamed as broken English rather than heard as a second, orderly one, the sense it carries goes unheard along with it.

## Drives

It drives whoever moves between the council and the yard to carry two grammars at once, marking tense by a verb ending in one and by a small word set before the verb in the other, and to know, without pausing to think, which one the moment calls for. It habituates the ear to hear does not as mere repetition but as the mark of a habit, a shade of meaning standard English must otherwise spell out in a whole added phrase.
