# Position: Virginian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Virginian English keeps a repair most of English lost, y'all, a plural you built to replace the old thou and ye that English let merge into a single word centuries ago, so Virginia speech alone still marks, in the second person, whether one listener or several are being addressed. It carries too the long, gliding vowels of the Southern shift, so time or nice take on two vowel targets rather than one, a drawl that turns a single syllable into a small, unhurried melody, and it keeps fixin' to as a tense of its own, marking an action about to start in a way plain going to does not quite capture.

## Orders

Like all English it holds a fixed subject verb object order and a spoken subject in every clause, since the verb's plain ending does not by itself say who is meant. It orders sir and ma'am into nearly every exchange with a stranger or an elder, a politeness English cannot mark grammatically since it lost its old formal you, so Virginia speech does with a whole word what German or French does with a single pronoun. And in much of the state it merges pin and pen into one sound before a nasal, so the two words are told apart only by what surrounds them, not by the vowel itself.

## Loses

It loses the very vowel distinction its own merger erases, so pin and pen become homophones and the listener must recover the difference from context, much as English elsewhere lost the gender or case another tongue still keeps. And where the drawl retreats before the flat, national broadcast standard, the long gliding vowel gets trimmed toward a single flat sound, so time starts to lose the very glide that once made it recognizably Southern and recognizably Virginian.

## Drives

Raised where y'all still marks a plural the standard tongue cannot, the Virginia speaker keeps a distinction other English speakers must repair with an extra word or lose to guesswork, and grows used to naming, with sir and ma'am, the deference the grammar itself cannot express. The long Southern vowel teaches an unhurried delivery, one that lets a single word take its time, so the speaker learns to let politeness and pace carry the social weight that the grammar of English, stripped of its old formal address, no longer carries on its own.
