# Play: South Sudan

## Estate

[khai-cultures](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-cultures): the house that holds this production and answers for it.

## Name

South Sudan, the young country at the heart of Africa, landlocked under the equator, where the White Nile comes up from the great lakes and spreads itself wide into the Sudd before it gathers again and carries on north. ISO SS, capital Juba on the river, language en on the books, with Juba Arabic the talk of the market and town, and the many mother tongues of the many peoples carried beneath them all. A land of savanna and swamp and cattle, and the newest flag the world has raised.

## Arc

This is a land far older than the nation that sits on it. Long before any border was drawn, the peoples of these rivers - the Dinka and the Nuer, the Bari and the Shilluk, the Azande and dozens more, each held here in equal dignity - lived by the cattle and the water, moving with the seasons between the high ground of the dry months and the river when the floods came down. The cow is wealth and name and song; a man knows his finest ox the way another man knows his own child, and sings its praises into the night. The Sudd, one of the great swamps of the earth, holds the river and the fish and the cattle camps along its edge, and the savanna beyond holds the wildlife on its long migrations through Boma. For a long age the wider world drew its lines across this country without asking the country, and the south was held far from any seat of power, the road north long and the road to its own voice longer still. After a hard and patient road that this staging does not walk in detail, taking no side and dwelling on no wound, the people of the south at last came to vote, and on the ninth of July, 2011, the newest nation on earth was raised, the long-awaited dawn that the elders had sung would surely come. Under it all runs one nerve: a people of the cattle and the river, many in tongue and one in their waiting, who hold their dignity in song and wrestling and the named ox, and who reckon that the morning, long in coming, has come.

## Stakes

Whether a land far older than its nation, a people of the cattle and the river held together by a long waiting and a shared dignity rather than by one tongue or one blood, can carry the many peoples it holds in equal honour into one country, can keep the cattle camps and the praise-songs and the wrestling-grounds alive as the towns grow and the wider world presses in, and can make the long-awaited morning into a home worthy of the waiting - or whether the newness of the nation and the bigness of the task outrun the hope that raised it.
