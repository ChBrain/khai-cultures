# Position: English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

What English gives the new nation that no single mother tongue could: one shared syntax and vocabulary that belongs fully to none of Dinka, Nuer, Bari, Shilluk or Azande, so any two speakers of different first languages can meet on ground that favours neither. Learned mostly as a second language through school, it carries the plain article and tense system of standard English into a country where several of the mother tongues instead mark meaning by tone, so the same sentence can be built two different ways depending on which tongue a speaker's ear was first tuned to.

## Orders

It requires the tense ending, the article and the subject-verb agreement of standard schoolroom English wherever the statute, the lesson and the ledger are written, obliging a fixed grammar regardless of which tone-carrying mother tongue the speaker grew up marking meaning in. Learned as a second language by nearly every speaker, it forces a constant translation between a tongue that spreads meaning across word endings and one that, for many speakers, first carried meaning in pitch.

## Loses

Where a mother tongue like Dinka or Nuer marks a difference by tone alone, that pitch is often carried over into English speech as habit rather than rule, so a listener trained only on standard English can miss distinctions the speaker still means. The precise pastoral vocabulary of the cattle camp, words for the colour and pattern of an ox, the grief of a long journey, has no ready English equivalent and simply does not travel into the second language at all, and the language spoken by the state stays a stranger's tongue to whoever never reached the schoolroom.

## Drives

It trains whoever passes through school to hold two grammars side by side, one carrying meaning in endings and articles, the other in the tone learned first at home, and to translate between them without noticing the switch. It makes a shared, neutral schoolroom grammar the price of reaching across the country's many mother tongues at once, so fluency in it, not birth, is what lets a speaker address the whole nation rather than one clan alone.
