# Persona: Emmanuel Jal

## Taxonomy

[the river](position_the_river.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

A son of the rivers who grew up to be a musician known beyond the country's borders, carried here soberly as an artist whose songs turn a hard beginning toward hope and toward the home he sings of. Driven, open-hearted, and tireless on a stage, he took the rhythms of the home ground into a music the wider world could dance to, and never stopped pointing his songs back toward the rivers and the peace he wishes them. His mother tongue of the river country he still [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) in the songs he carries from home, and the common [English](position_language_en_ss.md) of the wide stage he [carries](process_speaking_carried.md) and [reads and follows](process_reading_followed.md) as the tongue that took his music out to the world. He sings to the world in the tongue the world shares, yet the heart of the song, the names and the praises of the river and the cattle, lives in the mother tongue the world does not have, so even the song that crosses every border meets the gap at the limit of the tongue, and carries home in it what cannot be wholly carried over.

## Action

In [the dawn of independence (2011)](plot_04_the_dawn.md) he is the voice that carried the country's joy and its hope out to the world, his music of [the river](position_the_river.md) and the home ground turning the long waiting into song at the very moment the morning came. By singing the dawn to the world, he gives the young nation a face and a sound beyond its borders.

## Shadow

The song that travels can outrun the home it came from: a music made for the wide stage can be loved abroad and grow faint at the river, and the artist who carries the country out into the world can find the country harder to carry whole, the home ground always smaller in the song than it is on the ground.

## Tell

He took the rhythm of the rivers onto the world's stages and aimed every song back home, singing the country's hope to people who had never seen the Sudd - and kept the deepest words in the tongue of the river all the same.
