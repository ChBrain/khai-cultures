# Plot: the rivers and the cattle peoples

## Owner

## Cue

Long before any nation or any drawn border, the White Nile comes up from the southern lakes and spreads itself into the great wetland of the Sudd, and along its waters and the savanna beyond a gathering of peoples makes its home.

## Action

The cattle-keeping peoples of these rivers settle into a life set by the water. In the dry months they bring the herds down to the river and the Sudd for grazing and fish; when the floods rise they fall back to the high ground, the whole year breathing in and out with the flood. A young man takes the name and the colour of his finest ox and sings its praises into the night, the herd standing for wealth and marriage and peace alike. The Dinka and the Nuer, the Bari and the Shilluk, the Azande and dozens more, each its own and each held in equal dignity, share the cattle and the water and a way of life far older than the country that would one day sit on it.

## Tension

This is the country's first and deepest layer, the life of the cattle and the river that all that comes after rests on. Its beauty is its age and its balance, a people reading their whole year off the water and singing their wealth in the praises of an ox; its quiet weight is that this old life is fragile before the press of the town and the road, and that the morning the country waited for would ask it to carry the camp and the praise-song forward into a world that does not move with the flood.
