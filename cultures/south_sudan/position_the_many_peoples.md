# Position: the many peoples

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The country of many tongues held in one house. The land carries dozens of peoples, each with its own language and its own way: the Dinka and the Nuer of the cattle and the wide plains, the Bari around the river at Juba, the Shilluk of their old kingdom on the bank, the Azande of the forests to the south-west, and many more besides, each proud and each its own, none above another. They share the cattle and the river and the long waiting, and they share the markets where Juba Arabic became the common talk that lets a Bari trade with a Nuer and a Zande greet a Shilluk. Held here in equal dignity, the many are the country's first fact: South Sudan is not one people but a gathering of peoples who came to wait, and then to vote, together.

## Orders

That every people of the land is held in equal honour, the small beside the great, none the owner of the country and none the guest in it; that a person is first a child of a clan and a tongue, and proud of it, and a citizen of the young nation second and as well; that the common talk of the market lets the many trade and greet across their tongues without any one tongue swallowing the rest; and that the strength of the house is the carrying of the many in one, not the melting of the many into one.

## Loses

Ground when the pride of one people is set above another, when the count of cattle or the weight of numbers is made a claim to stand over the rest rather than beside them; and ground, too, when the young of the town lose the mother tongue of the camp for the common talk alone, so that a grandchild can trade in the market but can no longer answer a grandmother in the tongue of the clan.

## Drives

Lives in the writers who wrote across the tongues and held the whole gathering in their pages, and stands behind the founding vision, carried from the struggle for independence, of a country that all its peoples could call their own. It drives South Sudan to carry its many peoples in equal dignity, to keep the mother tongues alive beneath the common talk, and to be a house large enough to hold them all.
