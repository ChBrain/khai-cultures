# Panama: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind the
plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_es.md](pitch_es.md) is written from Panama's profile on Geert
Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no number.
The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the record without
entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Panama score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 95                     |
| Individualism                 | 11                     |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 44                     |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 86                     |
| Long Term Orientation         | 38                     |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published by
Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group; Panama's strongly collectivist,
high-power-distance, uncertainty-avoiding, and indulgent profile underlies the warm,
plural, close-knit, and quick-witted tone of the pitch.

## Historical sources

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public historical record. They are credited here and never claimed; the
staging is original work. The contested chapters at the heart of the story - the
1903 separation, the US Canal Zone and the long struggle for sovereignty, the 1977
treaties and the 1999 handover, and the events of 1989 - are staged soberly on the
public record, naming the fact without graphic or atrocity detail and taking no
political side.

The defining archetype, [el canalero](persona_el_canalero.md), is not a historical
individual but a composite: the Afro-Antillean and local canal worker, descended
from the West Indian laborers who dug the cut, who carries the labor that built and
runs the crossing. The historic personas - Balboa, Victoriano Lorenzo, Torrijos,
and Ruben Blades - are public figures drawn from the record and credited, never
claimed. The cultural framing - the pollera and the sombrero pintao, the Carnival
and the tamborito, the Congos of Portobelo, and the Guna of Guna Yala - is staged
with respect and credited, never claimed.
