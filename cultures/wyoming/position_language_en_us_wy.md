# Position: Wyoming English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Wyoming English carries the low back vowel merger to completion: "cot" and "caught," "don" and "dawn," "stock" and "stalk" are pronounced with the same vowel, a merger finished across nearly the whole Mountain West that lets the speaker drop a distinction other American dialects still work to keep separate. It is also, by the measure of American dialectology, one of the least regionally marked forms of American speech, a flat, portable pronunciation with little of the sharp local color that Appalachian, Southern, or Northeastern speech carries.

## Orders

The merger is not a choice; once complete, the dialect forces "cot" and "caught" into the identical vowel, and the speaker has no way, within this dialect, to signal by sound alone which of a merged pair is meant. The dialect also forces full rhoticity, every syllable-final "r" pronounced, in a country where whole other dialects, Boston, New York, parts of the Deep South, drop it; here it cannot be dropped.

## Loses

Because the vowel merger is complete, the tongue loses outright the ability to distinguish, by ear, words like "don" and "dawn," a pair some American dialects still keep apart; and because it carries so few other locally marked features, it loses the phonetic means to signal, on first hearing, that the speaker is from Wyoming at all, the way a Boston "r" or an Appalachian a-prefix signals home ground instantly.

## Drives

It drives whoever speaks it toward a flattened, unmarked pronunciation that travels without announcing where it came from, a portability that trades local color for ease of being understood anywhere in the country; and it drives every syllable-final "r" to be said rather than dropped, so the mouth never eases off a sound the way East Coast dialects do. The one Mountain West tongue shapes a plain, low-friction way of speaking that carries distance and directness in its very lack of ornament.
