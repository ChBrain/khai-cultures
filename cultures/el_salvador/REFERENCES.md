# El Salvador: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_es.md](pitch_es.md) is written from El Salvador's profile on
Geert Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no
number. The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the record
without entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | El Salvador score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 66                          |
| Individualism                 | 19                          |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 40                          |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 94                          |
| Long Term Orientation         | 20                          |
| Indulgence                    | 89                          |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published by
Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group; El Salvador scores high on
Uncertainty Avoidance and Indulgence and low on Individualism, the profile of a
collectivist, family-centred and outwardly warm society.

## Historical sources

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public historical record. They are credited here and never claimed; the
staging is original work. The painful chapters of the story - the conquest of
Cuzcatlan, the 1932 events, the long civil war, and the martyrdom of Monsignor
Oscar Romero - are staged soberly on the public record, with no atrocity detail
and no political side; Romero is treated reverently as the canonized saint and
voice of the voiceless, and the inequality and the diaspora are framed as the gap
between the country's promise and its reality.

The one defining persona drawn as a type, [el hermano lejano](persona_el_hermano_lejano.md),
is an archetype, not a historical individual: a composite that carries the
condition of a nation with roughly a quarter of its people abroad, the distant
brother whose remittances sustain the homeland and whose absence leaves an empty
chair at every table.
