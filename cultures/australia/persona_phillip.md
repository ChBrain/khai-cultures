# Persona: Captain Arthur Phillip

## Taxonomy

[terra nullius](position_terra_nullius.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Naval officer and commander of the First Fleet, who in 1788 sailed eleven ships of convicts and marines across the world and founded the colony at Sydney Cove as its first governor - the founding act of the nation, made on the doctrine that the land belonged to no one. Capable, dutiful, and by the standards of his moment comparatively humane, instructed to live in amity with the natives even as the settlement he raised dispossessed them, he is the founder in whom the founding contradiction is fixed: the careful, well-meaning officer planting an empire on a fiction of emptiness over a peopled shore. English he has whole and commanding: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [writes](process_writing_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue, [Australian English](position_language_en_au.md), the tongue of the Admiralty and the Crown, in which his orders, his dispatches, and the very claim to the continent were composed. The hundreds of languages already spoken across the land - the oldest tongues on earth, sung into the country for tens of thousands of years - he could not [hear as more than noise](process_hearing_followed.md), could not read or write, and did not learn, and so the land he declared empty was loud with speech he could not understand, the gap at the limit of the tongue marking the founding line between the colony and the country it was raised upon.

## Action

In [the First Fleet (1788)](plot_01_first_fleet_1788.md) he plants the colony at Sydney Cove and founds the nation on [terra nullius](position_terra_nullius.md), the doctrine that the land was no one's to claim. By raising a settlement on a peopled shore declared empty, he gives Australia both its beginning and the gap it will spend its whole history trying to close.

## Shadow

The decent officer who can mean well and dispossess all the same is the nation's own shadow: the temptation to believe that good intentions absolve the act, that the fiction of emptiness can stand because no one wished the harm, and to let the carefulness of the founder excuse the wrong of the founding.

## Tell

He is instructed to live in friendship with the people of the land, and raises his flag on the doctrine that the land has no people - meaning the friendship sincerely even so, and leaving the country a founding it must one day face.
