# Persona: the battler

## Taxonomy

[the fair go](position_the_fair_go.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The Aussie battler: the ordinary working man or woman who has a go, does it tough, and never expects a handout but expects a fair crack - the digger on the goldfields, the shearer in the shed, the worker in the suburb, the family making ends meet at the edge of the dry country. Hard-working, stoic, dry of humour, and quietly proud, they ask only for a fair go and give one in return, back the underdog as a matter of course, and have no time for those who put on side. They are the human face of the egalitarian creed, the person the fair go is for and the person who keeps it honest. English they have whole as their working tongue, plain and unpretentious: they [speak](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [think](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in the laconic, understated [Australian English](position_language_en_au.md) of the shed and the pub and the suburb, the tongue with no airs in it. The languages of the land they live on - the first tongues sung into that country for tens of thousands of years - run mostly [unheard beneath their plain English](process_hearing_followed.md), so the battler stands the fair go on ground whose oldest names they cannot say, the gap between the fairness they live by and the deeper belonging they have yet to hear marking the limit of their own tongue.

## Action

In [the Gold Rush](plot_02_the_gold_rush.md) the battler is the digger who comes for gold and stays to demand a say, raising the flag at Eureka and forging [the fair go](position_the_fair_go.md) into the democratic temper of the colonies. By having a go, standing up for the level field, and backing the next person down on their luck, the battler carries the egalitarian creed from the goldfields to the suburbs and keeps it the living faith of ordinary people.

## Shadow

The decent battler who lives by the fair go can also draw its circle tight: the same loyalty to their own can shade into suspicion of the newcomer and a blindness to those the fair go never reached, the underdog backed only when the underdog looks like them. The faith that no one is anyone's better can curdle into a levelling that resents the one who rises and ignores the one still locked out.

## Tell

They ask for nothing but a fair go and give one to the next bloke as a matter of course, doing it tough without complaint - and stand their plain, level creed on the oldest ground on earth, whose first names they have not yet learned to say.
