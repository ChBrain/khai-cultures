# Plot: Anzac and the Reckoning

## Taxonomy

[play_australia](play_australia.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

A young nation barely more than a decade old goes to a world war, and on a distant shore finds the legend it will tell itself by. Then, across the rest of the century, the door it had shut by race is thrown open, the nation drawn from a hundred origins remakes itself - and the first peoples it was founded by dispossessing carry their cause at last into the nation's law and conscience.

## Action

At Gallipoli in 1915 the Anzac soldiers, in a campaign that ends in defeat, forge a self-image of mateship, endurance, and irreverent good humour under fire, the [larrikin](persona_the_larrikin.md) digger giving the nation a legend rooted not in victory but in how its people stood by each other. After the Second World War Australia opens its doors to mass migration - from Europe first, then from Asia and everywhere - and the White Australia policy is dismantled, a British outpost remade into one of the most successful multicultural societies on earth. And the long Indigenous reckoning unfolds: in 1992 [Eddie Mabo](persona_mabo.md) overturns [terra nullius](position_terra_nullius.md) and native title is recognised in law; in 2008 the nation makes a formal National Apology to the first peoples; and in 2017 the Uluru Statement from the Heart calls for Voice, Treaty, and Truth.

## Stage

- [the larrikin](persona_the_larrikin.md): the irreverent Anzac digger who forges the national legend of mateship from the defeat at Gallipoli.
- [Eddie Mabo](persona_mabo.md): the Torres Strait Islander who overturns terra nullius and opens the long reckoning with the first peoples.
- [terra nullius](position_terra_nullius.md): the founding fiction, overturned in 1992, that the reckoning of the century must answer.
- [Uluru](place_uluru.md): the sacred heart of the continent, handed back in 1985 and the place from which the Statement from the Heart is given.

## Tension

In a single century the nation finds its proudest self-image in the mateship of the trench, throws open the door it once shut by race to become genuinely multicultural, and begins at last to face the gap planted at its founding. Yet the reckoning is unfinished: the fiction is overturned and the apology made, but the gap in health, in justice, and in belonging stays open, and the Uluru Statement waits on an answer. The easygoing nation is asked whether its fair go can be made true for the first Australians and for every newcomer alike - the closing of the gap still the unfinished work.
