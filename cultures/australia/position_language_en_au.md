# Position: Australian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Australian English carries a vowel system pushed and raised further than its British or American relatives, so that a short front vowel drifts toward a diphthong an outsider mishears entirely; and it carries an unusually productive habit of clipping and suffixing ordinary words with -o or -ie, so that afternoon becomes arvo, a service station becomes a servo, and a barbecue becomes a barbie, a trick that can be applied to almost any noun on the spot.

## Orders

Like all English it forces fixed word order, an obligatory article, and do-support for questions and negatives; and it forces, on top of that, the near-automatic habit of shortening and suffixing common nouns, so that the long form can sound oddly formal beside the clipped one, and it forces the well-known rising note at the end of a plain statement, so that a fact can come out sounding like a question whether the speaker means it to or not.

## Loses

It has no grammatical gender or case, and no T/V split for address, the same losses general English carries; and its habit of understatement, calling a disaster 'not ideal' or a triumph 'not bad', can flatten the very feeling the plain words would otherwise carry, so that the true weight of the news has to be read from context rather than from the words themselves.

## Drives

The clipped, suffixed vocabulary trains a laconic, self-deprecating habit of naming things, cutting the grand and the ordinary down to the same short size; and the rising terminal note trains a tentative, checking-in cadence into speech, so that even a flat statement of fact comes out sounding like it is asking to be agreed with.
