# Position: Australian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The authority of the shared national voice - the English the First Fleet carried that became its own. Laconic, dry, slang-rich, and level, the plain speech of the shed, the pub, and the suburb, it holds the idiom in which a whole people recognise one another: the vernacular that no one owns and everyone speaks, the sound of the country talking to itself.

## Orders

What counts as Australian English, its vernacular and its register - which words, which turns of phrase, which flat and unhurried cadence belong to the national tongue. It orders the plain speech from the pretentious, the fair-dinkum from the put-on, and sets the level, unpretentious measure by which the country hears whether a voice is one of its own or is putting on side.

## Loses

Ground to the hundreds of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander first tongues it displaced and could not hear - the oldest languages on earth, sung into this country for tens of thousands of years, over which the settler English was laid as though the land were silent. And it loses, from above, to the global and American English pressing in through screen and market, the imported idiom that thins the local vernacular even as the older tongues it never learned press up from beneath.

## Drives

It drives the whole cast, living differently in each of the voices that carry it. It lives whole as the founding lawyer's English that wrote the nation's law and drew its lines of belonging; it is the imperial English of the Admiralty, planted commanding on a peopled shore it declared empty. It is the level, unpretentious working tongue of the ordinary battler, plain and with no airs in it, and the irreverent, slang-rich vernacular of the larrikin that cuts every grand thing down to size. And it is the borrowed English a Torres Strait Islander leader - whose own mother tongue was one of this country's own - had to win his people's ancient title in: the conqueror's tongue turned, at last, to admit the truth. So the one national voice shows in each speaker how it was carried, imposed, lived, or borrowed under duress.
