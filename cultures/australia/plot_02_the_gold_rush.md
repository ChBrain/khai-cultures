# Plot: the Gold Rush

## Taxonomy

## Owner

## Cue

By the 1850s the penal colony has become a string of growing settlements, and then gold is found - in New South Wales and richly in Victoria - and the world hears of it. Free immigrants pour in from Britain, Europe, China, and the Americas, the population leaps, and the goldfields fill with diggers who have crossed the earth not as convicts but as free people chasing a fair chance at fortune.

## Action

The diggers work their claims under a colonial administration that charges them a heavy licence fee and polices it harshly, and resentment hardens into revolt. In 1854, at the Eureka Stockade near Ballarat, the diggers burn their licences, raise the flag of the Southern Cross, and swear to stand by each other and their rights; the stockade is stormed and many are killed, but the cause carries, and the reforms follow. From the goldfields comes a new temper: democratic, egalitarian, suspicious of authority and proud of the ordinary working person, the conviction that everyone deserves a fair go and a say. The free, self-reliant digger replaces the convict as the figure of the colony, and the egalitarian creed is forged in the diggings.

## Stage

## Tension

The gold rush makes the colony free, democratic, and egalitarian, the fair go born in the diggings and the convict stain washed off by the self-reliant digger - and yet the level field is drawn with a line through it from the start, the anti-Chinese feeling on the goldfields prefiguring the White Australia the new democracy will write into its founding. The fair go is born real and born bounded, the gap between fairness for all and fairness for some present even in its making.
