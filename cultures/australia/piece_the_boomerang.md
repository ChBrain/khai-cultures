# Piece: the boomerang

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

In the hands of Aboriginal peoples across the continent for thousands of years, on the rock art of the oldest cultures on earth, and now on tea towels and souvenir shelves the world over: the curved throwing tool of the first Australians, shaped from wood and balanced so that the returning kind, when thrown right, comes back to the hand. A real implement of hunting and craft long before it was an emblem, and the most widely known object the first peoples gave the world.

## Load Bearing

It carries the deep time of the land - the sixty-odd thousand years of continuous human culture that the founding fiction tried to declare away. The boomerang is the oldest cultures made portable, the proof in the hand that this country was peopled, skilled, and knowing for longer than almost any human story anywhere, the land never empty and never no one's. Against [terra nullius](position_terra_nullius.md) it is a quiet, returning refutation: a thing made by the first peoples that the whole nation now claims as its own, the symbol of a belonging older than the colony that denied it. Without this curved piece of wood the nation would have no everyday emblem of the antiquity it stands upon.

## Apparent

A curved length of hardwood, smoothed and weighted, light enough to throw and shaped so the air carries it: in itself a simple tool, a piece of the bush worked by patient hands. Its weight is entirely in the depth of time it carries and in whose hands first made it, the oldest technology of the oldest cultures, picked up by a young nation as the image of the land it shares.
