# Position: Nauruan English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Nauruan English is co-official with Nauruan and, in practice, the dominant written tongue: government business, law, and schooling are conducted in it, an inheritance from a century of German and then Australian administration before independence. Inside it, Nauruan proper nouns keep their place untranslated, Naoero, Angam Day, Buada Lagoon, ibija, names an English rendering would only flatten, and this variety has coined its own vocabulary too, most visibly Topside, the English name given to the mined interior, a word with no Nauruan original behind it at all.

## Orders

It orders nearly every official document, every school lesson, and most of a newspaper's column inches into English, whatever language was actually spoken where the news happened; it orders a Nauruan proper noun kept exactly as spoken, never translated, whenever the place or the day itself is Nauruan; and it orders a greeting like ekamawir omo to stay in Nauruan even set inside an English sentence, since some things this variety declines to translate at all.

## Loses

It loses, in the same stroke that made it dominant, most of Nauruan's own written record: a tongue nearly everyone at home still speaks has almost nothing published in it, no broad literature, no daily press of its own, so a Nauruan word most often survives in print only by riding along inside an English sentence such as this one.

## Drives

It drives every persona who has had to draft a law, negotiate a treaty, or read a balance sheet in a tongue they were not raised in: DeRoburt drafting the phosphate rights case, Dowiyogo deciphering the finance and the law of the bust. And it marks, in the same breath, everyone who still calls the island Naoero at home while writing Nauru on the form, keeping one tongue for the paperwork and another for the place itself.
