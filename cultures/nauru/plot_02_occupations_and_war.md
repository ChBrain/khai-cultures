# Plot: Occupations and War

## Cue

Germany annexed the island in 1888 and administered it as part of German New Guinea; phosphate was found soon after, around the turn of the century, and mining began under foreign license before the tribes who owned the ground had much say in the terms. After the First World War, administration passed to Australia, Britain, and New Zealand jointly, and phosphate export continued under a commission the islanders did not control. Then came the Second World War: Japanese forces occupied Nauru in 1942, and in 1943 most of the population was deported to forced labour on Chuuk, an atoll far to the north, staged here soberly and without atrocity detail.

## Action

German rule set the phosphate trade in motion, granting mining rights that paid the Nauruan landowners little of what the rock was worth; Australian administration after 1919 continued the arrangement much as it stood. The Japanese occupation of 1942 tightened control of the island for the war's duration, and in 1943 nearly two thirds of the Nauruan population was shipped to Chuuk to labour under harsh conditions, a deportation from which a significant share never returned. The survivors came home in 1946 to an island short of the people who had lived on it before the war, and the memory of that near loss became one the republic would later choose to mark rather than bury.

## Stage

## Tension

Two successive foreign administrations profited from phosphate the tribes had never agreed to sell at that price, and then a war arrived that nearly emptied the island of its own people. What this decade leaves standing is the question of who the land and its money belonged to, a question the republic would spend its first years as an independent state answering.
