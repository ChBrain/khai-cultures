# Plot: After the Money

## Cue

By the 1990s the phosphate was running out, the trust fund built to replace it had been drained by years of bad investment, and Topside sat largely mined through, a field of coral pinnacles with nothing left to dig. Bernard Dowiyogo, president through much of this stretch, presided over a republic that had gone from among the richest per head on earth to one searching for whatever income it could find next, while Angam Day kept marking the same anniversary it always had: the population's own survival.

## Action

The phosphate that had built the boom thinned out through the 1990s, and the trust fund meant to carry Nauru past the mining years turned out to have been spent on ventures that failed as often as they succeeded. Dowiyogo's governments tried one scheme after another to fill the gap, from offshore finance to hosting an Australian run processing centre for asylum seekers, arrangements that brought income back onto the island but drew scrutiny from outside it. Through all of it Angam Day kept being marked every year regardless, a headcount that had once been about surviving disease and war and was now, just as steadily, about surviving the loss of the fortune that followed.

## Tension

A republic that had briefly been one of the richest on earth per head found itself, one generation later, dependent again, this time on foreign funded arrangements rather than its own rock. What endures through the loss of both the money and the land it came from is the same population that marks Angam Day each year, still asking what a small nation does once the thing that made it rich is gone.
