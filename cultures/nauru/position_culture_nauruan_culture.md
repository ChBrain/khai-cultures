# Position: Nauruan Culture

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Nauruan culture carries [the Twelve Tribes](position_the_twelve_tribes.md), the clans whose number still marks the flag and whose custom still orders standing and land. It carries [Phosphate and the Price](position_phosphate_and_the_price.md), the mined out interior and the trust fund the boom left behind, and [the Smallest Republic](position_the_smallest_republic.md), the sovereignty Hammer DeRoburt won in 1968. And it carries [Nauruan English](position_language_en_nr.md), the tongue the republic writes and governs in, alongside Angam Day, the yearly count that marks the population's own survival, Buada Lagoon, and frigatebird catching, a custom still handed down hand to hand.

## Orders

It orders every Nauruan to hold custom, language, and faith at once, since a nation this small has no separate audience for any of the three; Angam Day orders the whole population to mark its own survival together, once a year, without exception; and the phosphate years order every household to reckon with what the boom paid and what the bust then cost. It orders sovereignty and Nauruan English into the same daily life, since the republic's independence is spoken about in a tongue most of its citizens learned as a second one.

## Loses

It loses ground to the boom's own aftermath: land mined out at Topside, a trust fund spent down to almost nothing, and a self reliance that outlived the money meant to support it. And it loses ground to a longer history before that: German and then Australian administration, the Second World War, and the deportation to Chuuk all thinned the population and the custom together, and Nauruan itself keeps losing ground to English in exactly the domains, law, finance, government, where a small nation's future gets decided.

## Drives

The life of Nauru's founding president, Hammer DeRoburt, who fought the phosphate rights fight and won independence in 1968, and of Bernard Dowiyogo, president through the boom's decline, both drive this position. Nauruan culture is what stays custom, faith, and language even as frigatebird catching, Angam Day, and Buada Lagoon keep tribe and skill in the same hands they always were, while the phosphate and the sovereignty it bought keep asking what a small nation does with what is left after the money.
