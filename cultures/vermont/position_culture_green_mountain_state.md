# Position: the Green Mountain State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a state that measures itself by two stubborn habits at once, the will to govern itself and the patience to boil sap down to syrup one drop at a time. It carries [green mountain liberty](position_green_mountain_liberty.md), the independent local voice and town-meeting pride that go back to the militias who defended their land grants against New York and stood up an independent republic before Vermont was ever a state; and it carries [maple craftsmanship](position_maple_craftsmanship.md), the sugarhouse discipline and dairy-farm cooperation that turned a hard forested ridge into a livelihood. It speaks [Vermont English](position_language_en_us_vt.md), carried alike by the historic-site coordinator and the sugarhouse boiler. What it hands to whoever grows up in it is a trust that self-governance and hard, patient craft are the same discipline practiced on two different mornings.

## Orders

That every town settle its own road, budget, and zoning at its own meeting before it defers to a state house far away; that a batch of syrup be graded honestly and a boiling schedule kept exactly, because a sugarhouse's reputation is only as good as its last batch; that local property rights and cooperative sourcing be treated as load-bearing walls of any farm or forest township; and that a newcomer be judged by whether he shows up to do the work, not by how long his family has held the ridge.

## Loses

Ground when the same fierce localism that makes each town meeting sovereign leaves it unable to agree, town by town, on the zoning and watershed protections that a lake fouled by agricultural runoff needs decided at scale. It loses ground, too, in the seam between the fast, tourism-and-resort economy of the historic sites and ski country and the slower, weather-bound economy of the sugarhouse and dairy farm, each reading the other's calendar as an inconvenience rather than as the same hard state working two different trades; and it strains when global brand consolidation buys out the family farms and cooperatives that gave the maple economy its name.

## Drives

Lives in the site coordinator who reads the state through its historic militia records and the sugar maker who reads it through her boiling logs, two trades off the same green ridge that neither quite trusts to understand the other. It drives Vermonters toward an independent self-reliance that would rather settle its own affairs at its own meeting than take an order from outside, and toward a craftsman's patience that will not rush a batch of syrup or a season's harvest past what the sap and the soil allow. And it holds both together as one inheritance: a state that governs itself the way it boils its syrup, slowly, exactly, and answerable to no one but its own neighbors.
