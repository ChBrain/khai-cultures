# Position: South Dakota English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The Upper Midwest holds its long vowels level where most American English lets them glide, boat and no said as one steady sound rather than two, and it keeps a stock of fixed affirmations, you betcha, oh yah, that close a sentence with plain agreement rather than building one from scratch. On Pine Ridge, English carries loanwords straight from Lakota for kinship, ceremony and place that have no plain English equivalent, alongside a rise and fall in the sentence that Lakota prosody lends it.

## Orders

The plains vowel holds the tongue level through what elsewhere breaks into a glide, marking a speaker as plains-raised from the first long o or a spoken, and it fixes a short set of ready-made phrases for agreement and surprise rather than leaving the speaker to compose one. Reservation English carries an intonation contour, rising and falling differently than the surrounding broadcast standard, inherited from the prosody of Lakota itself.

## Loses

The level vowel and the fixed affirmations get smoothed first under the national broadcast standard, which glides every long vowel the same way no matter where the speaker is from, so the plains sound thins fastest in the young and the widely travelled. The Lakota-derived words and the borrowed intonation get heard by outsiders as accent rather than as a distinct contact variety, and get set aside as merely rural rather than named as its own thing.

## Drives

It builds a level, unhurried delivery into whoever grows up on the plains, and it keeps a handful of Lakota-derived words alive in the mouths of English speakers who never learned the language itself. It trains the ear to hear agreement in one short fixed phrase rather than to build a fresh reply each time, and to carry, in reservation speech, a rise and fall the surrounding standard does not share.
