# Position: the Mount Rushmore State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a state where two claims sit on the same granite and neither one has ever quite let go: [monument preservation](position_monument_preservation.md), the engineer's pride that keeps Mount Rushmore's faces and the old Homestake gold shafts standing safe through the tourist season, and [black hills heritage](position_black_hills_heritage.md), the Lakota memory of a mountain range promised forever by treaty and taken anyway. It carries the 1876 gold rush that broke the Treaty of Fort Laramie before the ink had cooled, and the seventy-one days at Wounded Knee in 1973 that made the country reckon with it. What comes with the inheritance, asked for or not, is a mountain range that reads on a postcard as a national memorial and reads on a treaty map as a homeland still being fought for.

## Orders

That the granite face, the drill logs, and the park safety record get kept up with the same unhurried, dutiful care a rancher gives a fence line; that the tribal councils, the treaty archive, and the reservation forestry crews be treated as a living sovereign claim and not scenery for the tour buses; and that both traditions, the one that carved the mountain and the one that remembers whose mountain it was first, be carried as a single debt still open on the same range.

## Loses

Ground when the same tourist dollars and mining leases that keep the monument's lights on are the very thing straining the aquifer the reservation depends on downstream; ground when wildfire smoke and a summer's worth of traffic shut the same two-lane roads both economies need open; and ground, worst of all, when the treaty dispute the gold rush opened gets waved off as settled history instead of named for what it still is - a bill nobody in Pierre or Washington has paid.

## Drives

Drives South Dakota to keep its granite faces and its treaty maps as two halves of one Black Hills account, holding Mount Rushmore's rock stability and Pine Ridge's land claim to the same standard of upkeep, and pushing the state to settle honestly with the broken treaty the gold rush opened before either kind of preservation can be called finished.
