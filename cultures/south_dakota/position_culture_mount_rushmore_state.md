# Position: the Mount Rushmore State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a Black Hills state built on two inheritances carved out of the same granite ground: [monument preservation](position_monument_preservation.md), the engineering and tourist-safety pride that keeps the presidential faces of Mount Rushmore and the mine shafts of the gold rush standing and safe, and [black hills heritage](position_black_hills_heritage.md), the tribal land sovereignty and treaty memory of the Lakota reservation communities whose sacred mountains were carved into a national monument without their consent. It carries the memory of the Homestake Gold Rush that broke the Treaty of Fort Laramie, and of the Wounded Knee occupation that forced the country to answer for it. What one holds without choosing it is a mountain range that is at once a national memorial and a homeland still under treaty dispute.

## Orders

That the granite monuments, the safety drills, the crack telemetry, and the park administration standards be kept up as the state's mechanical, dutiful pride; that the tribal councils, the treaty archives, and the reservation forestry programs be honored as sovereign inheritance and not folklore for visitors; and that the two traditions, the engineering that carves the mountain and the sovereignty that remembers whose mountain it was first, be reckoned with as one unresolved inheritance of the same Black Hills.

## Loses

Ground when the tourism and mining economy that funds the monument's upkeep is the very force straining the aquifers and the watersheds the reservation communities depend on, when wildfire smoke and heavy visitor traffic close the roads that both economies share, and when the treaty disputes the gold rush opened are treated as settled history rather than a debt the state still owes.

## Drives

Drives South Dakota to hold its granite faces and its treaty maps as two halves of one Black Hills inheritance, pressing the state to protect Mount Rushmore's stability and Pine Ridge's land rights alike, and to reckon honestly with the broken treaty that the gold rush began before either kind of preservation can be called complete.
