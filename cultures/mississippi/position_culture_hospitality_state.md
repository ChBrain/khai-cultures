# Position: the Hospitality State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Membership in a Deep South state shaped by the fertile silts of the Yazoo Delta, carrying a double inheritance the nation still reckons with: [blues legacy](position_blues_legacy.md), the twelve-bar structures and slide guitar born on the porches and plantations of the Delta around 1900, and [civil rights solidarity](position_civil_rights_solidarity.md), the voter registration drives and freedom schools of the 1964 Freedom Summer that challenged segregation at its hardest edge. Its mother tongue is [Mississippian English](position_language_en_us_ms.md), spoken in the slow Delta cadence of the blues porch and the formal Southern drawl of the civil rights record alike. It carries, too, the weight of the cotton boom that began in 1830 on land taken by forced cession and worked by enslaved labor, the plantation past the blues itself first answered. What a Mississippian carries without ever choosing it is hospitality laid directly over hardship, a formal warmth that does not erase the history beneath it.

## Orders

That one honor the oral tradition, family storytelling, and community memory that carried the blues and the movement both forward when no other record was kept; that one bring the same disciplined craft to a slide guitar and to a voter registration drive, since both are forms of testimony; and that hospitality be practiced as a real welcome extended across the color line the state's own history drew, not a performance for the visitor passing through.

## Loses

Ground where the commercial consolidation of music streaming denies delta artists the royalties their own tradition of [blues legacy](position_blues_legacy.md) earned, hollowing out the local craft networks that first carried the sound; ground where state-wide restrictions on voting access override the local registration work that [civil rights solidarity](position_civil_rights_solidarity.md) demands, thinning out the representation Freedom Summer fought to win; and ground where the cotton boom's plantation past is smoothed into festival nostalgia instead of reckoned with as the hardship the blues rose out of.

## Drives

It drives the state to hold the cotton boom of 1830, the Delta blues birth of 1900, and the Freedom Summer of 1964 as one continuous story, the same soil that forced a people into bondage and cotton fields later giving the world its music and its most hard-won lesson in organizing for the vote. It shapes those raised here toward a patient, narrative, story-driven manner and a stubborn loyalty to family and community memory, whether the calling is a guitar, a classroom, or a registration table. And it drives the state to answer whether hospitality can hold hardship in full view rather than covering it, so the porch and the schoolhouse are remembered as the same ground.
