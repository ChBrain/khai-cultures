# Position: Mississippian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Mississippi English keeps y'all, a true second-person plural that standard English lost centuries ago and never replaced, so a Delta or Jackson speaker can address a room without stretching the same worn "you" over one listener and fifty. It carries the Southern drawl, which breaks a short vowel into two held sounds, so "yes" opens toward "ye-uhs" and a single syllable can carry the slow, unhurried weight the region is known for.

## Orders

It forces, in its own grammar, what standard English forbids: a double modal, "might could," "used to could," stacking two shades of possibility onto one verb where the standard allows only one modal at a time. The Southern merger before a nasal also forces "pin" and "pen" onto the identical vowel, so the distinction must be rescued by context or by naming, "ink pen," rather than by the sound itself.

## Loses

The long "i" loses its glide entirely in this speech, so "time" and "ride" flatten from a two-part vowel into one long held "ah," a smoothing that erases a distinction most other English keeps. Older Delta speech let the r after a vowel go silent as well, in the plantation-country manner shared with the coastal South, a softness the encroaching standard now corrects out of the young.

## Drives

The stacked modal trains a speaker to weigh possibility in layers rather than settle it in one word, and the drawl's slow, broken vowels give a sentence room to be turned over before it closes. So growing up inside it teaches an ear to expect qualification piled on qualification, and a mouth in no hurry to reach the verb's end.
