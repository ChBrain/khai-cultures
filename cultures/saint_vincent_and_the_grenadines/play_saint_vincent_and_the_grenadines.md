## Arc

This is the story of one archipelago that carry a wound deep down at its root and a fire right in its middle. Before the white man come it was Hairouna, the land of the blessed, home to the Kalinago, the island Caribs. Onto that same island the Africans reach, some by shipwreck, some by run-away, and out of African meeting Carib a free people come up, the Garifuna, the Black Caribs, that hold the land as their own. When Britain come for it they put up a hard fight, under the paramount chief Joseph Chatoyer, their national hero, till they get beat and the deportation of 1797 carry away thousands across the sea, seeding the Garifuna communities that living to this day along the coast of Central America: one deep wound, and the root of a diaspora. The island that remain was worked for sugar, then for arrowroot, that the place grow better than anywhere in the whole world, then for bananas, the green gold of the small farmer down under the volcano. Over the mainland La Soufriere stand up, a living volcano whose eruptions in 1902, 1979, and 2021 mark the nation's life like a heartbeat, the land blessed and burning the same time. South run the Grenadines, the islands of sailormen and boatbuilders, of Bequia and its old whaling and sailing heritage, where the sea is the road and the trade. The country walk the long road from colony through the rise of labour to independence in 1979, and every year it does give itself over to its festivals, the summer Carnival of Vincy Mas and the pre-dawn carols of the Nine Mornings before Christmas, a thing no other country keep. Under all of it run one nerve: a warm, God-fearing, neighbourly people, calm on top over a buried history of resistance and loss, that ride out the volcano and hold the scatter-out islands together as one.
