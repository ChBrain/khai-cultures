# Position: Hairouna and the Garifuna

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The deepest root of the nation: the island the Kalinago called Hairouna, the land of the blessed, and the free people who grew on it. Onto that Carib island came Africans, by shipwreck and by escape, and out of their meeting with the island Caribs grew the Garifuna, the Black Caribs, a free people who held the land as their own and called no one master. It holds, too, the wound at that root: that this free people resisted Britain to the last under their chief, were defeated, and in 1797 were carried away across the sea into exile, seeding the Garifuna communities of Central America. Hairouna is the blessing and the Garifuna the pride and the grief together, the land that was held free and the people who were broken for holding it.

## Orders

That the nation is older and deeper than its colonial name, rooted in a free people who were here first and held the land as their own; that the chief who died resisting is the national hero and the heart of the country's pride; and that the deportation is a wound to be remembered soberly, not buried, the root of a diaspora that ties the island to kin scattered across the Caribbean sea.

## Loses

Ground when the deep root is forgotten beneath the easy calm, when the Garifuna heritage is left half buried and the wound unspoken, so the surface forgets what lies under it; and ground, too, when the grief alone is remembered and not the blessing, when Hairouna is reduced to its loss and the living pride of a free people is let slip away.

## Drives

Lives in [Joseph Chatoyer](persona_chatoyer.md), the paramount chief who led the resistance and died for the land, the national hero in whom the pride and the wound are one. It drives Saint Vincent and the Grenadines to remember that it is the land of the blessed and the home of a free people, to honour the chief and the heritage, and to carry the deep root forward beneath the calm.
