# Position: the Treasure State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The ground under a Montanan's boots is always doing double duty: the deep-shaft wealth of [copper mining](position_copper_mining.md) that built Butte into the "Richest Hill on Earth," and the glaciated peaks of [Montana wilderness](position_montana_wilderness.md) that pull the world toward Glacier National Park. It carries the self-reliance of a state where the next town is an hour off at best, where a family's word and a family's labor still outweigh anything printed on a resume. It carries, too, the older claim of the Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho, who held this same ground at the Little Bighorn before any mine was sunk or any park was fenced off. The Treasure State's people talk in [Montanan English](position_language_en_us_mt.md), a mountain cadence and a prairie drawl both, and they size up a life by whether it was spent close to the land - underground in a hoist shaft or aboveground on a trail crew, it doesn't much matter which.

## Orders

That work gets done with your hands and your word gets kept, whether it was given in a union hall, a ranch contract, or a tribal council. That safety and stewardship come before speed, in the mine shaft and on the trail both. That the land - mineral vein, alpine valley, or reservation ground - gets treated as something held in trust for whoever comes after, not a resource to be run through. That the old rivalry between the mining towns of the west and the ranching plains of the east gets hashed out across a table, not left to sour into something permanent.

## Loses

Its footing whenever an out-of-state mining outfit slides around a union wage contract or a safety agreement, hollowing out the same solidarity that built Butte in the first place. It loses ground when resort money and second-home buyers price the working families and tribal communities out of the valleys they were raised in, turning shared, working land into vacation real estate nobody local can afford to stand on. It loses ground in what a century of copper smelting left behind - the heavy metals still sitting in the Clark Fork, still waiting, decades on, for the cleanup it was promised. And it loses ground every time the boardrooms setting mineral quotas treat conservation and tribal land rights as a line item to get to later, if there's time.

## Drives

Drives Montanans to hold mining, ranching, tourism, and tribal sovereignty together in one uneasy but working balance, rather than letting any single one of them claim the whole state's story for itself. It keeps the mining towns telling their union fight as a live source of pride, not a chapter that's closed and shelved. It keeps the wilderness advocates and tribal liaisons pushing for a seat at whatever table decides how much copper leaves the ground and where it goes. And it pushes the whole state, mine and mountain and reservation alike, toward one reckoning it can't quite duck: whether a place that got rich taking wealth out of the ground can also learn to give the ground back its health, and the water running off it besides.
