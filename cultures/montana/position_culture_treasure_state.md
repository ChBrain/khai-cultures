# Position: the Treasure State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The ground beneath a Montanan's feet is always doing double duty: the deep-shaft wealth of [copper mining](position_copper_mining.md) that built Butte into the "Richest Hill on Earth," and the glaciated peaks of [Montana wilderness](position_montana_wilderness.md) that draw the world to Glacier National Park. It carries the mountain self-reliance of a state where towns sit hours apart, where a family's word and a family's labor still count for more than a resume. It carries, too, the older claim of the Lakota, Cheyenne, and Arapaho, who defended this same ground at the Little Bighorn before any mine was ever sunk or any park was ever fenced. The Treasure State's people speak in [Montanan English](position_language_en_us_mt.md), a mountain cadence and a prairie drawl both, and measure a life by whether it was spent close to the land, whether underground in a hoist shaft or aboveground on a trail crew.

## Orders

That work be done with your hands and your word be kept, whether in a union hall, a ranch contract, or a tribal council. That safety and stewardship come before speed, in the mine shaft and on the trail alike. That the land, whether mineral vein or alpine valley or reservation ground, be treated as something held in trust for the next generation rather than a resource to be used up. That old rivalries between the industrial towns of the west and the resource-dependent plains of the east be worked out at the table, not left to fester into permanent division.

## Loses

Its balance whenever corporate mining giants sidestep union wage contracts and safety agreements, hollowing out the same labor solidarity that built Butte. It loses ground when resort development and tourist money price out the working families and tribal communities who were there first, turning valleys into vacation real estate instead of shared, working land. It loses ground in the after-effects of a century of copper smelting, in the heavy-metal contamination of the Clark Fork River that still waits, decades on, for the restoration promised to it. And it loses ground whenever the fast, technology-driven world of mining boards and mineral markets treats the patient, law-bound work of conservation and tribal land rights as an afterthought.

## Drives

It drives Montanans to hold mining, ranching, tourism, and tribal sovereignty together in one uneasy but working balance, rather than letting any one of them own the whole state's story. It drives the mining towns to remember their union struggle as a source of pride, not just a chapter closed. It drives the wilderness advocates and tribal liaisons to keep pushing for a seat at the table where copper production and mineral export are decided. And it drives the whole state, mine and mountain and reservation alike, toward the same reckoning: whether a place built on taking wealth from the ground can also learn to give the ground itself, and the water running off it, back its health.
