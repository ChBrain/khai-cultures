# Position: the Golden Stool

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The sacred seat of the Asante, the Sika Dwa Kofi, said to have been brought down from the sky by the priest Okomfo Anokye to hold the very soul of a whole people, and with it the whole order of chieftaincy that came before the state and still stands beside it. The stool is no throne to be sat upon; it is the nation made visible, kept apart and reverenced, never touched to the bare ground. Around it stands the order it anchors: the Asantehene and the chiefs, the queen mother who names the king, the okyeame who carries the chief's word, the elders and the clans bound by blood through the mother's line. It is authority held as a sacred trust handed down from the ancestors, where the chief is custodian and not owner, and where the dead are reckoned to be watching still.

## Orders

That authority is a sacred trust and not a private property, the chief seated by the elders and the queen mother and bound to serve the stool and not himself; that the matter is taken to the elders and settled in council, the difficult word folded in proverb so that nobody is shamed; and that the ancestors and the living and the not-yet-born are one people, so what is held is held in trust for those still to come.

## Loses

Ground when the custodian forgets he is custodian and treats the trust as his own, when the chieftaincy is set against the republic rather than carried alongside it, and when the young, doing their thinking now in English and their living now in the city, come to see the stool as a relic of an older time rather than the soul of a people still alive.

## Drives

Lives in [Yaa Asantewaa](persona_yaa_asantewaa.md), the queen mother who rose to defend the sacred seat when the foreigner reached for it, and in [the Okyeame](persona_okyeame.md), the linguist whose mouth carries the chief's word and keeps the order of the council. It drives Ghana to honour the elder and the chief, to settle the matter in council, and to hold authority as a trust owed back to the ancestors and forward to the children.
