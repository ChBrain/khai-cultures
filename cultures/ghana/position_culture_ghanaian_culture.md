# Position: Ghanaian culture

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The belonging to Ghana, the Black Star raised over a young republic wearing an old name borrowed whole from an empire a thousand years gone, so that the strength of an ancestor might walk again in a nation of many mother tongues, Akan and Ga, Ewe and Dagbani, gathered under one flag and one office tongue. It has [akwaaba](position_akwaaba.md) as the law of welcome that comes before the law of the state, and the deeper truth beneath it, that a person is a person through other persons and that the living, the dead, and the not-yet-born sit at one table. It has [the Golden Stool](position_the_golden_stool.md) as the memory that chieftaincy came before the republic and stands now beside it, and [the proverb](position_the_proverb.md) as the whole cast of mind that would rather wrap the hard truth in an image than say it bare. It carries the sorrow of the castles at the coast, held with dignity and not paraded, and the pride of being first below the Sahara to lift the flag of its own freedom, in 1957.

## Orders

That the stranger is greeted before he is questioned and fed before he is asked anything, so that akwaaba is meant and not merely performed; that the matter is taken to the elders and the hard word folded in proverb so that no one is shamed in the settling of it; that authority is held as a trust from the ancestors and answerable to the queen mother and the council, never as a private thing; and that the one who has risen carries the many, opening a hand to family, hometown, and stranger alike, the door of the household kept as open as the door of the nation.

## Loses

Ground where the welcome thins to a performed smile and the chair is no longer pulled for the stranger; ground at the old seam between the chieftaincy that answers to the stool and the republic that answers to the ballot, when the two are set against each other rather than carried together; ground, too, at the coast, where the castles hold a history the house carries with dignity but must not let harden the heart; and ground in the children who do their thinking now in English and on the small screen, so that the grandmother's proverb and the mother tongue that carried it a thousand years fall silent in a single generation.

## Drives

Drives whoever is raised in it to greet before they question and to hold the household, living and dead and unborn, as one table nobody sits at alone; drives them to take the difficult thing to the elders and to say it gently, in an image, rather than bare; drives the nation, as it did the leader who flung the door open to a whole continent and the diplomat who carried its quiet courtesy to the world's largest stage, to carry Ghanaian warmth outward without losing the mother tongue in which the proverb and the welcome were first learned; and it drives the Ghanaian, above all, to measure a stranger and a countryman alike by the same warm and unhurried law of the table.
