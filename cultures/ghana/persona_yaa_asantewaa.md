# Persona: Yaa Asantewaa

## Taxonomy

[the Golden Stool](position_the_golden_stool.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The queen mother of Ejisu, an elder woman of the Asante, who, when the foreigner demanded the sacred seat itself and the men of the council hesitated, stood up and shamed them into courage, taking up the defence of the Golden Stool when others would have given way. Fearless, dignified, and rooted utterly in the soul of her people, she is the elder who knows that some things are not for sale and not for surrender, and that the stool is not a throne but the very life of a nation. Her tongue is Twi, the Akan of the Asante, and she has it whole: she [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), she [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and she [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) the matter of the council in the mother tongue, and the proverbs she hurled at the wavering men were Twi proverbs, sharp as any blade. The foreigner who came for the stool came speaking [English](position_language_en_gh.md), the tongue of the empire, and what passed between them passed through interpreters, the chief's word and the colonizer's demand each [carried across](process_speaking_carried.md) by another's mouth; and so even the reaching for the soul of a people was conducted through the gap at the limit of the tongue, where one side could not hear, in the other's own words, what the stool truly was. She belongs to [Ghanaian culture](position_culture_ghanaian_culture.md), the Asante queen mother who will not let its Golden Stool be surrendered.

## Action

In [the war of the Golden Stool](plot_02_the_golden_stool.md) she rises to defend [the Golden Stool](position_the_golden_stool.md) when the sacred seat itself is demanded, rallying the Asante to stand for the soul of their nation rather than surrender it. She makes plain, once and for all, that the stool is not a thing to be handed over, but the very people made visible.

## Shadow

The courage that defends the sacred can also be asked to defend what cannot, in the end, be held by courage alone; the elder who shames the council into a stand carries, too, the cost that stand will exact. To love a thing so much that you will not yield it is a glory, and it is a weight, and Yaa Asantewaa carries both.

## Tell

When the men of the council fell silent, she told them that if they would not go forward, then the women would, and she would call on her own sex to fight, putting the soul of the nation above her own safety and shaming a hesitant council into courage.
