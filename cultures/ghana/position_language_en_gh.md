# Position: Ghanaian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

What English lets a speaker do that Akan, Ga, Ewe and Dagbani each do differently: mark meaning through stress and word order rather than through tone, and reach for a wide technical and abstract vocabulary, ready-made for statute, ledger and lesson, that a home tongue must otherwise build fresh or borrow whole. Ghanaian speech has bent this borrowed stock back toward home rhythms, folding Akan proverbs and turns of phrase directly into English sentences so that a saying keeps some of its salt in translation.

## Orders

A fixed subject-verb-object order, since English leans on position rather than case ending to show who does what to whom; a single word, you, for every listener, so address carries no built-in choice of rank the way some greeting and kinship terms at home still do; and tense marked through helper verbs, has gone, is going, will have gone, rather than through tone or affix.

## Loses

The tone that in Akan or Ewe can change a word's very meaning by a rise or fall of pitch, since nothing in English pitch carries that grammatical weight; the compressed, allusive shape of the proverb, which a straight English rendering can flatten into plain statement, losing the puzzle that gives the saying its bite; and the fine grades of kin-based and age-based address that the home tongues mark and that the single word you erases.

## Drives

Speaking it trains the ear to listen for stress and word order rather than tone, since a speaker who grows up with a tonal mother tongue at home must learn to let go of pitch as a carrier of meaning when the sentence turns to English. It lets the speaker greet elder and stranger alike without first weighing kinship into the words. And it builds, in whoever holds both tongues, the constant reflex of carrying a proverb or a thought from a tonal home language into an untoned public one, a small act of translation repeated every day.
