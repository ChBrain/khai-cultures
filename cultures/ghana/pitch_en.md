# Pitch: the Ghanaian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Ghana](play_ghana.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the golden land, not the war of the stool, not the sorrow at the coast, not the lifting of the flag in 1957. It sets only how the whole thing sounds, the register in which the matter is given.

## Tenor

Played warm, played together, and never in a hurry, oo. Here the person is not an island; a person is a person through other persons, and the one who walks alone is to be pitied, not praised. The greeting comes before the business, always, for the elder taught us that you do not start eating before you have asked after the household; and when a stranger arrives, akwaaba, you are welcome, is the first word, and a chair and a little water and a little food follow it whether the house has much or little. Age is honoured, the chief is honoured, the matter is taken to the elders, and the difficult thing is never said straight to the face but folded inside a proverb so that nobody is shamed. Faith is worn openly, God is thanked in the same breath as good morning, and joy spills out loud, in the highlife and the hiplife and the drum, at the naming and the wedding and even, with respect, at the funeral.

## Undertow

Under the warmth runs the weight of the coast, the sorrow that the house carries with dignity and does not parade, and the long memory of having been taken and traded and ruled by others. Under the welcome runs a watchfulness too, for the smiling face is the courtesy and the true thought stays wrapped, and to read a Ghanaian you must learn to hear what the proverb leaves unsaid. Under the togetherness runs the quiet cost to the one who would go his own way, the pull of the family and the hometown that asks much of the one who has done well, the way the big man must carry many. And under the Black Star runs the ache of the promise not yet fully kept, the freedom won and the prosperity still being chased, the young ones looking outward at a hard and hurried world.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of belonging and of welcome: the certainty that you are held, that there is a household and a hometown and a God that claim you, that no door is shut to the one who comes in peace. It plays on the dignity of the elder and the chief, the comfort of the proverb that says the hard thing softly, the pride of a freedom won first and a Black Star raised for a whole continent. It plays, too, on the warmth that says you for come, that pulls the extra chair to the table, that will not let a guest go hungry, and on the deep, lived faith that carries a people through both the joy and the grief.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people warm, courteous, and together: quick to welcome, slow to shame, faithful out loud, and patient with the long road. A nation that keeps the elder's seat and the grandmother's proverb even as the children think in English, that holds its sorrow with grace and its joy with a drum, and that says akwaaba and means it, sure that a person is only ever a person through other persons.
