# Piece: adinkra

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

Stamped in dark dye on cloth for the funeral and the solemn occasion, carved on the linguist's staff and the chief's stool, printed now on walls, on books, on pottery, and on the screen: the symbols of the Akan, each a small picture that stands for a whole proverb, sankofa the bird first among them, turning its head to fetch what is good from behind.

## Load Bearing

It carries the wisdom of a people made into a sign you can read. Each adinkra symbol is a proverb in a single image, gye nyame for the supremacy of God, sankofa for going back to fetch what you have left behind, and scores of others, a whole philosophy set down in shapes. Once stamped on the cloth worn to bid the dead farewell, it lets a people speak their deepest convictions without a word, and teaches the young the proverbs of the old. Without these symbols, the Akan love of the proverb and of the wisdom handed down would have no visible body; in the adinkra it becomes something stamped, carved, and seen.

## Apparent

Simple black shapes, geometric or pictured, pressed onto cloth with a carved calabash stamp and a dark bark dye, or carved and printed wherever a Ghanaian wishes to speak in signs. In itself each is a small abstract figure; its whole weight is in the proverb it carries and the philosophy a people read in it at a glance.
