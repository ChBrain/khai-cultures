# Ghana: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md) is written from Ghana's profile on Geert
Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no number.
Where a country-specific score is not separately published, Hofstede reports a
regional estimate for West Africa; the figures below are recorded here so the data
is on the record without entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Ghana / West Africa score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 80                                  |
| Individualism                 | 15                                  |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 40                                  |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 54                                  |
| Long Term Orientation         | 16                                  |
| Indulgence                    | 77                                  |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published by
Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group; several are reported for the West
Africa region rather than for Ghana alone, and Ghana's communal, low-Individualism
and high-Power-Distance profile is consistent with that published data.

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public historical record. They are credited here and never claimed; the
staging is original work. The memory of the Atlantic slave trade carried through
the coastal castles is staged soberly and with dignity, neither hidden nor
paraded.

The one archetype persona, [the Okyeame](persona_okyeame.md), is not a historical
individual but a composite: the chief's linguist and spokesman, keeper of the
gold-topped staff and master of the proverb, an office carried in the traditional
Akan courts and drawn here from the public record of Ghanaian chieftaincy.
