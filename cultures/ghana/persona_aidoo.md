# Persona: Ama Ata Aidoo

## Taxonomy

[the proverb](position_the_proverb.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The daughter of a chief's house at Abeadzi Kyiakor in the Fante country, the writer who took the spoken arts of her people, the proverb, the dirge, the tale told at night, and carried them onto the written page and into the world's tongue, becoming one of the great voices of African letters and a tireless advocate for the dignity of African women. Sharp, witty, and unafraid, she wrote with the cadence of the talking drum and the bite of the ebe, never saying the hard thing straight when a proverb would say it better. Her mother tongue is Fante, the Akan she [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md), the tongue in which her grandmother's stories first reached her; but the page she was given to write on was [English](position_language_en_gh.md), the colonizer's tongue, which she [drafts](process_writing_drafted.md) with mastery and yet bends back toward the rhythms of her own, [carrying](process_speaking_carried.md) the Fante proverb and the oral cadence into it until the borrowed tongue learns to speak with an Akan mouth. She wrote the African world in the very tongue that had tried to write over it, knowing that every line she set down was a small wager against the gap at the limit of the tongue, where the wisdom carried for a thousand years in the grandmother's mouth must either cross over into the written word or fall silent in it. She belongs to [Ghanaian culture](position_culture_ghanaian_culture.md), carrying its proverb and its warmth onto the printed page.

## Action

In [independence (1957)](plot_04_independence_1957.md) she belongs to the generation that came of age with the free nation, claiming [the proverb](position_the_proverb.md) for the page and carrying the spoken wisdom of her people into the world's reading. She makes the inherited oral arts new in the written word, so that the grandmother's ebe is not lost but given a second life in print.

## Shadow

The page that saves the proverb can also change it, the spoken wisdom fixed and flattened the moment it is written, and the world's tongue that carries it abroad can quietly become the tongue the children think in instead. To rescue a story into print is to risk the very mouth it came from falling silent, and Aidoo wrote always against that risk.

## Tell

She writes a novel that opens with the rhythms of the talking drum and answers the colonizer's literature in its own language, bending English back toward Fante until the borrowed tongue learns to carry the grandmother's proverb.
