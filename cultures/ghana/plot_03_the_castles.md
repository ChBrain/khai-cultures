## Cue

For the gold that gave this coast its names the Europeans came and built their forts and castles along the shore, at Elmina and at Cape Coast and at many points between, the oldest of them raised more than five centuries ago. In time the trade that passed through their gates turned from gold to a far graver traffic, and the coast became one of the doors through which the children of this land were carried away across the sea.

## Action

The castles by the water are the witnesses to a sorrow this house carries with dignity and does not parade: the long centuries in which men, women, and children of these lands were taken from their people and borne away never to return, the breaking of countless households, the scattering of a people across the whole Atlantic world. Ghana holds this memory soberly, neither hiding it nor dwelling on its horrors, but keeping the castles standing as places of remembrance. And in the keeping of it, a strange grace: the descendants of those who were carried away come back across the sea to stand in these rooms, and the nation that first took its freedom says to them, over a grief that no word can fully hold, akwaaba, welcome home.

## Tension

A sorrow too deep for any words must somehow be carried by a people who will not let it harden them. The coast holds a grief that cannot be undone and will not be paraded; and against it, the house sets the one thing it has always had to give, the welcome, saying akwaaba to those whose forebears were carried away, so that the place of the deepest wound becomes also a place of return.
