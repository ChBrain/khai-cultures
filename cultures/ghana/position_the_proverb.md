# Position: the proverb

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The Akan love of the ebe, the proverb, and the whole habit of mind that goes with it: that wisdom is inherited and not invented, that the hard truth is best said softly and sideways, and that the deepest things are folded inside an image so that the hearer may unwrap them in his own time. It is the art of indirection raised to a courtesy, where one does not point at the matter but lets the proverb point for him, so that nobody is shamed and the elder's dignity and the listener's are both kept. It carries, too, the whole storytelling house of Kweku Ananse the spider, the trickster whose tales teach by laughter and by his comeuppance, and the adinkra symbols stamped on cloth, each one a proverb made visible, sankofa first among them, the bird that turns its head to take from the past what is good and carry it forward.

## Orders

That you do not say the hard thing to a person's face but wrap it in a proverb, so the truth lands without the wound; that wisdom is older than the one who speaks it, drawn from the ancestors and the elders and given on loan; and that a people keep their soul in their stories and their symbols, so that to forget the proverb is to begin to forget who you are, and sankofa bids you go back and fetch it.

## Loses

Ground when the indirection thins into mere evasion, the truth so wrapped it is never delivered at all; ground, too, when the children, doing their thinking now in English and their reading on the small screen, no longer learn the grandmother's ebe or the spider's tales, so that a wisdom carried in the mouth for a thousand years falls silent in a single generation.

## Drives

Lives in the writer who carried the proverb and the spoken tale onto the written page and into the world's tongue, and in the linguist whose whole craft is to wrap the chief's word in the right proverb. It drives Ghana to teach by image and by story, to say the difficult thing gently, and to keep turning, sankofa, to fetch what is good from behind.
