# Persona: the Okyeame

## Taxonomy

[the proverb](position_the_proverb.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The chief's linguist and spokesman, the keeper of the gold-topped staff, who stands at the chief's side and carries his word to the people and the people's word back to the chief, never letting the two speak to one another raw and unwrapped. He is the master of the proverb above all men, the one who can take a plain command and dress it in an image so fitting that it lands without wounding, and who knows the histories, the praise-names, and the ten thousand ebe by heart, an archetype carried in every traditional court of the Akan and beyond. Courteous, deep-voiced, and quick, he is wisdom made into an office. His tongue is the Akan of the court, which he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md), [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md), and [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) with a richness few can match, [carrying](process_speaking_carried.md) the chief's bare word over into the proverb that fits it, and, when the court must speak to the wider nation, into the [Ghanaian English](position_language_en_gh.md) of the modern state as well; for the chief, by custom, does not address the gathering directly, and so his meaning must pass through the okyeame's mouth to be received with the proper dignity. His whole office stands at the gap at the limit of the tongue and bridges it, the man whose craft is to make sure that what the chief means is exactly what the people hear, and that no word ever crosses between high and low except wrapped in the courtesy that keeps both their dignities whole.

## Action

In [the golden land](plot_01_the_golden_land.md) he carries the chief's word out to the gathered people, the living voice of [the proverb](position_the_proverb.md) at the heart of the court, dressing each command in the image that fits it. He keeps the order of the council, where the matter is taken to the elders and the hard thing is said softly, so that authority speaks always through wisdom and never raw.

## Shadow

The mouth that carries another's word holds great power over it, and the linguist who wraps every command in proverb can also, if he wills, wrap the truth out of sight; the office made to keep meaning whole can be the very office that bends it. To stand between the chief and the people is to be trusted utterly by both, and that trust is a thing easily abused.

## Tell

He takes the chief's blunt word, turns it over once in his mouth, and gives it back to the gathering as a proverb so apt that the matter is settled and no one is shamed, the wisdom of a whole people speaking through one man's craft.
