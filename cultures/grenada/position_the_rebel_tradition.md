# Position: the rebel tradition

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The long line of refusal that runs through the island's story: the Kalinago who would not yield, the enslaved who rose, the free-coloured and the field who took up arms with Julien Fedon in 1795, and the statesmen who carried the same spirit into politics and the page. It is the conviction that a small island need not accept what the powerful hand it, that it may resist, organise, and imagine itself differently. From Fedon's rebellion it runs through emancipation to T.A. Marryshow, who turned the refusal into a vision: that the islands of the Caribbean, each too small alone, might stand together as one West Indian federation, and that Grenada should think of itself as part of a region and a people.

## Orders

That resistance is in the island's marrow, that to be Grenadian is to have inherited both the rebellion of 1795 and the federation dream that followed; that a small place may refuse the terms it is given and imagine a larger belonging; and that the rebel and the statesman, the rising and the vision, are two motions of the same long tradition.

## Loses

Ground when the tradition curdles from principle into mere strife, when refusal forgets the vision that should give it shape; and ground, too, to the discouragement of the small, the sense that an island so little cannot in the end resist the wide world's weight, and had best keep its head down and take what it is given.

## Drives

Lives in [Julien Fedon](persona_julien_fedon.md), who raised the free-coloured and the enslaved against the British in 1795, and in [T.A. Marryshow](persona_marryshow.md), the father of the West Indian federation, who turned the refusal into a vision of the islands as one people. It drives Grenada to carry its long tradition of resistance and its dream of a larger Caribbean belonging.
