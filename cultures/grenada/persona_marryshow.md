# Persona: T.A. Marryshow

## Taxonomy

[the rebel tradition](position_the_rebel_tradition.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

Theophilus Albert Marryshow, journalist, orator, and statesman, called the father of the West Indian federation, who turned the island's old spirit of refusal into a vision of the Caribbean as one people, and taught a small island to think of itself as part of a region. Eloquent, far-seeing, and tireless, he founded a newspaper and built a political life on the conviction that the islands, each too small alone, must stand together. English he has whole and shaping, the tongue of the colonial press and the public platform he made his own: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue, and he [writes it drafted](process_writing_drafted.md) into the editorials and the addresses that carried the federation dream across the islands. The wider library of British letters and Caribbean argument he [read and followed](process_reading_followed.md), and brought home to the cause. He made the case for a West Indian people in the English the empire had imposed, turning the coloniser's own tongue into the instrument of the islands' coming together, and yet the federation he argued for was a belonging the English language alone could never supply, so that the gap at the limit of the tongue was the distance between a region that shared a language and a region not yet one people.

## Action

In [Fedon and the Long Resistance](plot_02_fedon_and_the_long_resistance.md) he carries [the rebel tradition](position_the_rebel_tradition.md) from the rising into the vision, turning refusal into the federation dream and teaching the island to imagine a larger Caribbean belonging. He gives the long tradition of resistance its statesman and its idea, the proof that a small island could think beyond its own shore.

## Shadow

The vision of the one people can outrun the people themselves, and the dream of federation can become a thing argued more than built: the danger that the larger belonging stays an idea on the platform, that the eloquence is mistaken for the union, and that the small islands praise the dream and remain apart.

## Tell

He builds a newspaper and a public life on a single conviction, that the islands are one people, and argues the case in the coloniser's English until a small island learns to think of itself as part of a region and a Caribbean.
