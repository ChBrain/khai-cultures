# Piece: oil-down

## Place

In the big pot over the fire, at the beach lime, the family Sunday, and the village fete: the national dish, breadfruit and salted meat and dumplings stewed down in coconut milk with callaloo, turmeric, and the island's own spice, cooked until the milk cooks away and the oil settles to the bottom, where the name comes from. The one-pot that feeds a crowd, made on the hills, the shore, and wherever Grenadians gather.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the island's plenty made from little and shared by all. Built on the breadfruit the poor man could grow and the salted meat that kept without ice, layered into one pot and spiced with the nutmeg the hills give up, the oil-down is the food of a people who made abundance out of modest means and made it together, everyone adding to the pot and everyone eating from it. It carries the island's resourcefulness, its love of the gathering, and its spice, the Spice Isle on a plate. Without this one-pot the island's table would have no dish the whole nation calls its own.

## Apparent

A heavy iron pot crowded with breadfruit, dumplings, salted pigtail or saltfish, callaloo and ground provisions, simmered down in coconut milk gone golden with turmeric and fragrant with grated nutmeg, until the liquid cooks away and a sheen of oil gathers at the base. Plain country food in the pot; its weight is in the gathering it feeds and the island it stands for.
