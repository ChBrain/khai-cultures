# Play: Grenada

## Estate

## Name

Grenada, the southernmost of the Windward Islands, a small mountainous nation in the eastern Caribbean of three inhabited islands, Grenada itself with its sister isles Carriacou and Petite Martinique, lying north of Trinidad and south of St. Vincent. ISO GD, language en, with the Grenadian dialect spoken everywhere over it. The Spice Isle, the only nation on earth that carries a nutmeg on its flag, a small place that has lived a large history.

## Arc

A small green island that the wide world has fought over and that has fought, in its turn, for itself. The Kalinago held it first and made their last stand at Sauteurs, leaping from the cliff rather than yield, and the place still carries that memory in its name. France took it and named it, Britain took it from France, and the two empires traded the island back and forth while sugar and the enslaved who were brought to cut it filled the estates. In 1795 Julien Fedon raised the free-coloured and the enslaved against the British in the rebellion that bears his name, and a long tradition of resistance was set down that ran on through emancipation to T.A. Marryshow, the father of the West Indian federation, who taught a small island to think of itself as one with its region. The island found the spice that would name it, nutmeg and mace climbing the wet hills, and put the nutmeg on its flag. Independence came in 1974, and then the chapter that the nation still carries soberly, the revolution of 1979 to 1983 and the intervention of 1983, named on the public record and held now in the work of memory and reconciliation. Hurricane Ivan tore the island apart in 2004 and the island rebuilt, and through it all run Spicemas and the calypso and the soca, the Big Drum on Carriacou, and the oil-down bubbling in the one pot. Under it all runs one nerve: a warm, devout, resilient people, soft-spoken and proud, the sweetness of the spice carried over a chapter of memory not yet wholly healed.
