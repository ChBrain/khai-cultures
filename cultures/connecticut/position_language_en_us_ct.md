# Position: Connecticut English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The older Yankee vowel system of the Sound, in which cot and caught still keep two distinct vowels rather than merging into one, a short, clipped vowel length, and, in its oldest form, an r dropped at the end of a syllable, car and yard losing their r the way Boston's does but Hartford's mostly no longer does.

## Orders

It forces, in its traditional form, the postvocalic r to be dropped, so that the r at the end of a word or before a consonant reads as silent, a mark the speaker cannot help; it forces cot and caught to stay two different vowels, never merging as they do further west; and it forces a narrow, contained pitch range that reads as reserve, so that the same understated contour carries both the routine remark and the serious one.

## Loses

It loses the dropped r itself, receding decade by decade as General American rhoticity spreads inland from the interior and the coast alike, so the r returns in speakers who would once have swallowed it; it loses the broad, open vowel of aunt and half that marked the old Yankee accent, flattening toward the same vowel as General American; and it loses distinctiveness to the corporate and technical registers that iron every clipped New England vowel into the same neutral delivery.

## Drives

It trains the ear to hold cot and caught apart long after neighboring dialects have let them collapse into one; it trains the voice to a narrow, even pitch that reads as careful and undemonstrative rather than warm; and it trains the speaker toward understatement, so that the shortest, flattest sentence carries exactly as much weight as a longer one would elsewhere.
