# Position: Flickertail

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to the Flickertail State means answering to the same rule whichever half of it you're born into: a family earns what the ground under it gives up, wheat or oil, and does the work itself. It carries [prairie agriculture](position_prairie_agriculture.md) - the bonanza wheat farms of the Red River Valley that made the state a grain exporter to half the world, the mechanical thresher, the county elevator board - alongside [shale energy](position_shale_energy.md), the horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing that turned the Williston Basin into a boomtown of drill rigs almost overnight. It carries too the harder memory of the Garrison Dam, whose reservoir drowned Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara land and forced a relocation the state doesn't get to set down.

## Orders

That the harvest log and the drilling log both get kept up with the same unhurried care, that family labor and technical skill are counted as what actually holds a life together out here, and that the grain co-op and the county safety board are treated as the things that keep a hard, resource-dependent land livable. It asks the wheat farmer and the oil worker to recognize the same discipline in each other even when the ground under them is pulling two different directions.

## Loses

Its balance when the oil patch filling up out west and the farm valleys working it out east stop seeing each other as anything but the other side of the groundwater fight, state ambition running up against a farmer's instinct to hold onto what's his. It loses ground when railway tariffs and a crude price swing wipe out the margin the drilling crews and the farm families were both counting on, and it loses the most when the Garrison reservoir's history - the tribal land it put underwater - gets told as an engineering story instead of a debt still owed.

## Drives

Shows up in the wheat farmer who tunes his grain-drying fans and figures the oil patch is somebody else's weather to worry about, and in the drilling technician who logs a well's own limits and figures the wheat co-op's business carries about as far as the county line. It drives North Dakota to hold the plains and the drill rigs, the bonanza farm and the Bakken boom, in one state that still has to manage its river, its water table, and the ground it once put underwater, without losing either the grain or the oil its people's work turns into a living.
