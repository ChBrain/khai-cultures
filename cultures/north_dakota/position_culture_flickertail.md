# Position: Flickertail

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

The belonging to the Flickertail State, a land of flat river valley and rolling basin held together by the same conviction: that a family works the ground itself makes, whether that ground gives up wheat or oil. It carries [prairie agriculture](position_prairie_agriculture.md), the bonanza wheat farms of the Red River Valley that made the state a global grain exporter, the mechanical thresher and the county elevator board, alongside [shale energy](position_shale_energy.md), the horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing of the Bakken that turned the Williston Basin into a boomtown of drill rigs. It carries, too, the harder memory of the Garrison Dam, whose reservoir flooded Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara land and forced a relocation the state does not get to forget.

## Orders

That one coordinates the harvest log and the drilling log with the same formal, story-driven competence, that family labor and technical skill are both counted as the true anchors of a stable life, and that the grain co-op and the county safety board are respected as the institutions that keep a hard, resource-dependent land livable. It asks that the wheat farmer and the oil worker recognize a shared discipline even when the ground under them pulls in different directions.

## Loses

Its balance when the rapidly expanding western oil patch and the agricultural eastern valleys read each other only through the fight over groundwater pumping and watershed regulation, state-led development met by local instinct to protect the family farm. It loses ground when global railway tariffs and crude price swings override local margins and fracture the worker and farmer networks that depend on them, and when the reservoir's history, the tribal land it drowned, is remembered only as an engineering achievement rather than a debt still owed.

## Drives

Lives in the wheat farmer of the Red River Valley who calibrates high-output grain-drying fans and reads the oil patch's history only as a distant regional update, and in the drilling technician of the Williston Basin who logs the well's own geological limits and hears the wheat co-op's talk only like a faint echo across the state. It drives North Dakota to hold the plains and the drill rigs, the bonanza farm and the Bakken boom, inside one state that still has to manage its river, its water table, and the land it once flooded, without losing either the grain or the oil that its people's labor turns into a living.
