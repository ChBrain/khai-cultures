# Position: the Last Frontier

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Membership in a vast arctic state where distance and cold are the first facts of life, a land of glaciated fjords and endless caribou range that holds two inheritances at once: the subsistence knowledge of the Inupiat and other Native peoples who read the ice and the herd, and the industrial ambition of the oil rush that built the pipeline from Prudhoe Bay to Valdez. Its mother tongue is [Alaskan English](position_language_en_us_ak.md), carried with a technical drawl at the pump stations and a warm drawl in the coastal villages. What an Alaskan carries without ever choosing it is this double ground, [arctic survival](position_arctic_survival.md) and [frontier wealth](position_frontier_wealth.md), both drawn from the same hard land.

## Orders

That one respect the subsistence harvest and the mutual aid of small communities as much as the safety of the pipeline and the discipline of resource work, that one read the land and the season before the market, and that one treat self-reliance, family, and the sharing of the catch as marks of belonging wherever one lives, coastal village or pipeline corridor. It orders a wariness of anyone who claims this land without first respecting its cold and its distances.

## Loses

Ground where oil wealth is treated as the whole of the state's story and subsistence tradition as a quaint holdover, or where industrial development crowds native communities off traditional hunting and fishing grounds. It loses ground, too, when market swings in oil revenue starve the same rural infrastructure that subsistence communities depend on, and when the caribou and salmon that both halves of the state's life depend on are treated as an obstacle to logistics rather than a shared inheritance.

## Drives

It drives the state to hold its whale hunts and its oil strikes as one frontier rather than two, the same hard land producing both the hunter who reads the ice and the technician who reads the gauge. It shapes those raised in it toward a rugged self-reliance, a patience with distance and season, and a wariness of outsiders who mistake the frontier for empty ground rather than a living, working home.
