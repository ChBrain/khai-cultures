# Position: West Virginian English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

West Virginian English carries real Appalachian resources that flatter, more standard dialects lack outright: a-prefixing, which stresses the first syllable of a present participle with an "a-" ("she was a-cookin', they went a-huntin'") to mark an action as vivid and ongoing; the perfective "done," which packs completion and emphasis into the verb itself ("I done told you twice") without needing an adverb to carry it; and "anymore" used mid-sentence in a positive statement to mean "nowadays" ("Anymore, we just drive to town"), a use that sounds backward to outsiders but lets the speaker mark a shift from an older way of doing things in three syllables.

## Orders

The dialect forces a choice between the leveled past tense, one form standing in for what standard English splits into "saw" and "seen," "did" and "done," and the fuller standard set; it forces, across the mountain counties, r-coloring on nearly every vowel where it occurs; and it forces the diphthong in words like "fire," "tire," and "time" to collapse toward a single long vowel, so "fire" comes out closer to "far." None of these are optional flourishes; they are the shape the words take every time they are said.

## Loses

The leveling of past-tense forms loses the distinction standard English keeps between "saw" and "have seen"; the monophthongal vowel loses the glide that keeps "tire" and "tar" apart for other American ears, so a word one dialect hears as two sounds, another mishears as one. And once heard outside the hills, the positive "anymore" and the a-prefixed verb are taken for mistakes rather than markers, so precisely the forms that carry the most local meaning are what outside listeners strip out first.

## Drives

It drives whoever speaks it to load completion and vividness into the verb form itself, "done" and "a-," rather than reach for a separate adverb, a habit of packing rather than adding. It drives an ear tuned to hear "anymore" pointing backward at what used to be, mid-sentence, without a pause to explain it. The one Appalachian tongue shapes a way of talking that treats the verb as the place where emphasis, completion, and time all get marked at once.
