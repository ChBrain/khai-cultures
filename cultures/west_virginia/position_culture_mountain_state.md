# Position: the Mountain State

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Belonging to a state that broke itself away from Virginia rather than follow it out of the Union, and has been proving its own separate mind ever since. It carries [mountain sovereignty](position_mountain_sovereignty.md), the coal miner's hard-won claim to a fair wage and a fair say against the company that owns the land beneath his feet, and it carries [gorge stewardship](position_gorge_stewardship.md), the newer discipline of guarding the same steep river gaps for the rafting and tourism economy now built on them. It speaks [West Virginian English](position_language_en_us_wv.md), the Appalachian cadence of the coal camp carried into the raft dock and back. What it hands to whoever is raised in it is the plain fact that the same mountains that were fought over, dug into, and shipped out can still be run, rafted, and lived among, sometimes by the same family in the same generation.

## Orders

That a person's word, a union card, or a safety check be honored the way the mountains themselves are honored, as things that hold or fail all at once; that the state's breakaway founding be remembered as proof it can choose its own path even against its own former capital; that whoever profits from the mountain, whether by the ton of coal or the seat on a raft, answer for what is left of the mountain and the river afterward; and that the miner and the river guide be measured by the same plain standard of grit and care, neither trade thought lesser than the other.

## Loses

Ground where the extraction economy and the tourism economy read each other as rivals rather than two trades worked off the same coal-seamed rock, the miner's high-output metrics blind to the slow river cycles the rafting economy depends on, and the guide's conservation instinct blind to the union hall's fight for wages and safety; ground in the chemical runoff that industrial extraction leaves in the Kanawha and the rivers beside it, a debt owed to the water long after the profit from what fouled it is gone; and ground in a state-wide suspicion of outside zoning and environmental mandates that can, at its hardest edge, shade into indifference toward the very reclamation the state itself now needs.

## Drives

Drives West Virginians to hold the coal seam and the river gorge as one inheritance rather than two separate claims on what the mountains are worth, judging both by whether what was taken out is honestly accounted for and what is left behind is honestly cared for. It carries the memory of an armed labor uprising into every later fight over a fair wage and a safe shift, and it carries the newer instinct of the river guide into every argument over how the same steep land should be used next, pushing the state to prove, again and again, that a mountain people can break away, dig deep, and still keep faith with the water running through it.
