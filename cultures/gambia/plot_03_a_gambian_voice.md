# Plot: a Gambian voice

## Taxonomy

[play_gambia](play_gambia.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

For long years the thin land along the river is administered as a British colony, run from Bathurst at the river's mouth, its affairs decided largely without its people's say. In the town a new kind of Gambian comes up, schooled, working and unwilling to stay silent, and begins to ask why the people of the land should have no voice in its running.

## Action

Edward Francis Small stands up and puts the case plainly: a Gambian voice must be heard in the affairs of the Gambian land. He founds a newspaper, organises the working people, and presses through the colonial forms for the colony to have a real say in itself, the first to argue it so squarely. From these beginnings the long, patient road runs on, and in time the small country comes to its own flag and its own seat among nations, the change made without a shot fired, the river nation taking the running of itself into its own hands in peace.

## Stage

- [Edward Francis Small](persona_edward_small.md): the pioneer who stands up and says the people of the river must be heard.
- [Banjul](place_banjul.md): the river-mouth town, then Bathurst, from which the colony is run and the early voice is raised.
- [the river](position_the_river.md): the land along the water that comes, in peace, to the running of itself.

## Tension

A small colony argues its way to its own voice through the very forms of the power that rules it, and comes to its own flag in peace, without a shot fired, the change patient and bloodless where elsewhere it was not. The pioneer who presses the case in the coloniser's English and through the coloniser's channels wins a real voice for the many, yet must do so on terms not wholly his own, the patience that makes the peace also the patience the impatient would not abide.
