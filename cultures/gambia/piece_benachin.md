## Place

In the great shared bowl set down on the mat at the heart of the midday meal, the family and the guest gathered round it, each eating from the side before them with the right hand: benachin, the one-pot rice cooked together with fish or meat, tomato and vegetables, its Wolof name meaning one pot, the dish the whole country knows. Beside it on other days stands domoda, the rich groundnut stew, the other great dish of the Gambian kitchen, but it is the one bowl, shared, that says most of how the nation eats.

## Load Bearing

It is the meal that gathers people, the one pot that says the nation is a thing shared from a common bowl. In a land held by its welcome, the food is teranga made edible: the bowl always stretches to one more, the guest is fed before he is asked his business, and to share the bowl is to be taken in. Cooked together in one pot and eaten together from one dish, benachin is the family and the community made a meal, the everyday enactment of the welcome the small land lives by. Without the shared bowl, the warmth of the place would have no table.

## Apparent

Rice, fish or meat, tomato, oil and vegetables, cooked down together in a single pot and heaped in one wide bowl, simple food a household makes from what the market gives. In itself it is a one-pot rice; its whole weight is in the gathering round it, the hands reaching to the common bowl and the place always kept for one more.

## Yearbook

- Carried across the region as jollof in many lands, but known here as benachin, the one pot, in the Wolof of the country.
- Set beside domoda, the groundnut stew, as one of the two great dishes by which the Gambian kitchen is known.
- Served from the one shared bowl at the heart of the meal, the everyday form of teranga and the way the nation likes best to eat, together.
