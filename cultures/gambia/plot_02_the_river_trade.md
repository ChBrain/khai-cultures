## Cue

From across the sea the trading ships find the mouth of the River Gambia and sail up it, for the river that is the land's own road is also the way inland for those who come to trade. They build their posts along the water, and an island in the river's lower reaches becomes a place of trade and of holding.

## Action

For long years the river carries trade up and down its length, in goods and in gold, and in the time of the Atlantic slave trade it carries away its own sons and daughters, who go down the water and across the ocean and do not return. The Gambia holds this loss soberly and with dignity, marked at the island in the river and at the village of Juffureh on its bank. There, generations on, the keeping of the word bears its strangest fruit: when a searcher comes from across the ocean looking for the people his family was taken from, the elder griot of Juffureh recites the long oral history of the Kinte family and names the son lost to the river two centuries before, and the river's hardest memory becomes, for some, a road home.

## Tension

The river that gives the land its life is the same river that carried its people away, the giver and the taker one water, and The Gambia carries that doubleness soberly, neither hiding the sorrow nor dwelling on it. And the spoken word, frail as it is, proves true keeping after all: the oral history held in one elder's mouth for two hundred years reaches across an ocean to the very ones it was for, turning a memory of loss into a road home.
