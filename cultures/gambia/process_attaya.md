# Process: attaya

## Taxonomy

Parent group: processes

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Initiated by

Anyone with a small charcoal stove, a little metal teapot, green tea, plenty of sugar and a few hours to give: friends gathered in the shade, young men at the bantaba under the tree, a household with a guest to keep. Someone sets the coals going and the pot on, and the long ritual of the three rounds begins, no one in any hurry, the whole point being to stay.

## Direction

Toward sitting together and letting the time run long, for attaya is not a drink so much as an occasion to stay. The tea is brewed and poured high from glass to glass to raise the foam, then brewed again, in three rounds that take the afternoon: the first round bitter, the second milder, the third sweet, and the talk running on between them. It is teranga in a glass, the welcome that keeps a guest by the unhurried promise of three rounds and the company in between.

## Lever

It is driven by the deep Gambian sense that the worth of a thing is in the company it keeps and the time it takes, and by the little stove and pot that any hand can set going. It holds against the pull to hurry and the temptation to get to the business first; against it stand the long afternoons it asks and the patience it demands, the very slowness that is its whole point.

## Echo

Attaya makes audible the whole tone of the small land: the person before the business, the staying before the leaving, the welcome that holds a guest not with grandeur but with time. Down the three rounds, from the bitter through the mild to the sweet, the talk runs long and the company is the point, the slow tea keeping people sitting together in the way the nation likes best to be.
