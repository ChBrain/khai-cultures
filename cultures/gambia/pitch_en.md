# Pitch: the Gambian tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [The Gambia](play_gambia.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the river kingdoms, not the river of trade, not the coming of a Gambian voice, not the Smiling Coast. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Played warm, unhurried and close. We are a people who put the person before the clock and the greeting before the business, who hold the family and the elders high and the stranger close, and who reckon nothing good is done in a rush. You do not pass a man without you greet him: salaam aleikum, and the long courteous exchange that follows, how is the home, how is the body, how are the people of the house, peace only, peace only, asked and answered until the asking itself is the welcome. Authority is held with respect, the elder spoken to softly and the chief and the marabout shown their due, and a matter is best settled by sitting together until the talk is finished. We are not a loud people but a generous one, quick to share a bowl and slow to send a guest away hungry, for the small land is held by its big welcome, and we would rather be poor with company than rich alone.

## Undertow

Under the warmth runs the strain of a small land where the welcome must stretch far on little, where teranga is given freely and not always returned, and where the young must often go abroad to make a way the home cannot give them, the back-way road tempting them across the water. There is the weight of the river's old sorrow carried quietly, a grief not displayed but not forgotten. And under the unhurried calm runs the patience that can shade into waiting too long, the matter talked round and round when it might be settled, and the respect for the elder and the big man that can sit heavy on the one who would speak out of turn.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of belonging and of welcome: the certainty that you are not alone, that the home will hold you, that the bowl will stretch to one more, and that a stranger greeted well is a stranger no longer. It plays on the pride of a small nation that the world underrates, on the deep love of the river and the land along it, on the joy of the kora's run and the roar of the wrestling ground, and on the patient faith that a thing settled by sitting together holds longer than a thing forced. It plays, too, on the ache underneath, of a welcome given on so little, and a son away across the water.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a small, warm, unhurried people, gathered along one river and held together by their word and their welcome: greeting before business, sharing before keeping, sitting before deciding, and singing what others write down. A nation that runs on teranga, sure that the measure of a place is not its size but how it holds a stranger, and that the smile of the coast is the truest thing it owns.
