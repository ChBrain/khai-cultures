# Piece: the royal drums

## Taxonomy

[the kingdoms](position_obwakabaka.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

In the Lubiri and the drum house of the Kabaka, in the shrine and at the kings' tombs, at the coronation and the funeral and the great gathering of the clans: the engoma, the royal drums of Buganda and the other lake kingdoms, each drum with its own name, its own keeper, and its own beat, kept and sounded by the men whose clan duty it has always been. And beyond the palace, the engalabi, the long single-headed drum that leads the dance, sounding wherever the people gather to be a people.

## Load Bearing

It carries the dignity of the kingdom older than the nation. Among the lake kingdoms the drum is no mere instrument but a voice of the throne itself, the royal drums spoken of almost as one speaks of a king, sounded to mark the Kabaka, to call the clans, to honour the dead, each beat a word in a language the people know. To keep the drums is to keep the kingdom whole, its tongue and its memory passed on; and when the work is set down and the engalabi starts up, the same drum that holds the court calls the whole people to the dance, the kingdom's deepest dignity and the country's plainest joy beating on the one skin.

## Apparent

A drum of carved wood and stretched hide, laced taut with cord, struck with the hands or with sticks; the royal ones large and kept with ceremony in the drum house, the engalabi long and narrow and tucked under the arm to lead the dance. In itself it is wood and skin; its whole weight is in whose voice it is taken to be, and in the keepers whose clan it is to mind it and the dancers whose feet it moves.

## Yearbook

- Kept and sounded in the Lubiri and the drum house of the Kabaka, each royal drum with its own name, keeper, and beat, a voice of the throne in [the kingdoms](plot_01_kingdoms.md).
- Beaten at the coronation, the funeral, and the burial of the kings, the same drums with which the country still lays its Kabaka to rest.
- Carried beyond the palace as the engalabi that leads the kiganda dance, the kingdom's dignity and the people's joy on the one skin, sounded at every gathering of the clans.
