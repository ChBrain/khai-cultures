# Pitch: the Ugandan tone

## Taxonomy

The key in which the staging of [Uganda](play_uganda.md) is played. It changes nothing of what happens, not the kingdoms, not the protectorate, not independence, not the long road since. It sets only how it sounds, the register in which the whole show is given.

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Tenor

Mpola mpola sets the pace before a single word is spoken, slowly by slowly, because a Ugandan has learned that the boda-boda which overtakes on the inside is rarely the one that gets there first. You ask after a man's people and his health before you ask his business, not as procedure but because here the two questions are, in truth, one question. It is a country of the long handshake and the open pot, where webale and gyebale ko, the thank-yous of work and of rest, pass back and forth all day like a currency that never runs short. Respect runs upward to the elder, the Kabaka, the one who has lived longer and seen more, and is given freely and without complaint, for that is how a person is raised. The group holds the person and the person holds the group: you are who your clan and your village know you to be. Quick to laugh even at hardship, generous past good sense, patient with the slow turning of things, the tone trusts that whatever is troubling will soon enough be sorted, mpola mpola.

## Undertow

Under the warmth runs the weight of all the eyes that hold you: the respect owed upward can sit heavy, and the person who must answer to clan and elder and in-law and neighbour can find little room that is only their own. The patience that carries the country through its hard seasons can slide into a waiting that asks too little and forgives too much; the easy laugh can be the brave face a sober people keeps over griefs it would rather not name aloud. And the openness of the hand can be spent until there is nothing left to give, the one who shares everything left with too little to stand on.

## Nerve

It strikes the chord of belonging: that you are never only yourself, that there is a pot kept for you and a people who claim you, that no one here is meant to eat alone. It plays on the pride of the kingdom and the clan, the dignity of the elder, the joy that breaks out in the drum and the dance the moment the work is set down, and the deep, unboastful confidence of a people who know their land is beautiful and their welcome is real. It plays, too, on the ache beneath: of the room a person never quite has to themselves, and the brave face kept over what is not said.

## Echo

What is left is the image of a people warm, patient, and unbroken: quick to open the pot, quicker to laugh through the hard season, and never done carrying the kingdom and the nation in one heart, the way the crested crane on the flag carries its crown without needing to say so. A country that runs on the belief that a person is held, not alone, and that whatever is wrong will come right in time, mpola mpola, if the people stay together at the same table.
