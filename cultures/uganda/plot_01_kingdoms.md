## Cue

Long before any border was drawn over the land, the green country on the lake and the grasslands is home to great and ancient kingdoms. On the northern shore of Nalubaale, Buganda rises with its Kabaka and his Lubiri on the hill; westward stands Bunyoro-Kitara, which once ruled wide; Tooro and Ankole keep their long-horned cattle in the cool highlands, Busoga sits by the water, and many other peoples besides hold their own ground, each a whole nation with its tongue, its drums, and its clans.

## Action

The kingdoms govern themselves as sovereigns in their own right, with courts, clans, titles, and royal drums, and with the dignity of peoples who answer to no one beyond their own throne. [the Kabaka Mutesa I](persona_mutesa.md) rules Buganda from his Lubiri in the second half of the nineteenth century, receiving the first strangers who reach his court from the coast and down the Nile, weighing them, and keeping his own counsel. The land is one of plenty and of pride, and each people is, first and fully, itself, the country a roof not yet raised over houses that already stand whole.

## Stage

- [the Kabaka Mutesa I](persona_mutesa.md): the king who rules Buganda from the Lubiri as a sovereign in his own right.
- [the kingdoms](position_obwakabaka.md): the old, proud nations over which Uganda will later be drawn.
- [Kampala](place_kampala.md): the hills of Buganda's heartland, the Lubiri and the royal tombs among them.

## Tension

The country begins not as one people slowly becoming a state but as many proud peoples, each whole and sovereign, each the first thing a person is. This is the land's oldest dignity and its hardest inheritance: the kingdoms that give every Ugandan a self older than the nation are the same kingdoms that any later nation will have to hold together, each whole, without asking a single one to stop being itself.
