# Play: Uganda

## Estate

## Name

Uganda, the green country on the equator in the heart of East Africa, landlocked but never short of water, cradling the northern shore of Nalubaale, the great lake the world calls Victoria, and the place where the Nile rises and begins its long walk to Egypt. ISO UG, language en, with Luganda and Swahili and more than thirty other tongues spoken across the land. Winston Churchill called her the Pearl of Africa, and the name stuck, because the eye finds no resting place that is not beautiful. A young nation drawn over old and proud kingdoms, holding many peoples together as one.

## Arc

Buganda never left when the map changed around it: the Kabaka's Lubiri still sits on its hill above Kampala, and the old kingdom's memory sets the compass by which everything newer in the nation, unofficially, checks itself. Uganda was never empty ground waiting for a name; it was already Bunyoro-Kitara that once ruled wide, Tooro and Ankole with their long-horned cattle, Busoga by the water, and Buganda strongest of all among them, each with its own tongue and its own way of greeting the morning, long before any border was drawn across them. The kingdoms knew their own weight, and the nation's oldest argument has never really closed: how to hold so many peoples, each whole in itself, as one Uganda without any one of them losing what it is. A protectorate was raised over the old kingdoms, and then in 1962 the flag with the crested crane went up over Kampala and the country took itself back. The boda-boda now carries that same old hustle at a new speed, threading between the taxis and the rain, matooke steaming in its own leaves at every roadside stall the way it has fed the lake's people since before any of this had a flag. The country has known its hard seasons too, and has never pretended otherwise, but it moves through them the way it moves through traffic: mpola mpola, slowly by slowly, not because it is slow but because arriving in one piece matters more than arriving first. Through it all runs one nerve: a people quick to laugh and slow to give up, who hold the kingdom and the nation in the same heart, and who reckon that a country blessed with this much beauty owes it to itself to make the many into one.

## Triggers

**[Plot 1: the kingdoms](plot_01_kingdoms.md)**

Long before the borders were drawn, the great kingdoms rise on the lake and the grasslands; [the Kabaka Mutesa I](persona_mutesa.md) rules Buganda from his Lubiri on the hill, and [the kingdoms](position_obwakabaka.md) hold the land as proud nations in their own right.

**[Plot 2: the protectorate](plot_02_protectorate.md)**

The kingdoms are gathered under one protectorate and one new name, the railway reaches [Jinja](place_jinja.md) and the lake, and the many peoples find themselves drawn, not always by their own choosing, inside a single border that will one day have to be made into [the many held as one](position_culture_obuntu.md).

**[Plot 3: independence (1962)](plot_03_independence.md)**

In 1962 the flag with the crested crane goes up over [Kampala](place_kampala.md) and Uganda takes herself back; the same season [Okot p'Bitek](persona_okot.md) sings the old tongue and the old ways back into pride, and the young nation sets out to make the many one.

**[Plot 4: the Pearl renewed](plot_04_pearl_renewed.md)**

Through hard seasons and good, the country holds its warmth and finds its voice in the world; [Geoffrey Oryema](persona_oryema.md) carries the home song abroad and [Joshua Cheptegei](persona_cheptegei.md) runs the nation's name onto the world's track, the [Pearl of Africa](position_pearl.md) shining again.

## Stakes

Whether a young nation drawn over old and proud kingdoms can hold its many peoples, each one whole in itself, as one Uganda without any of them losing what it is; whether [the kingdoms](position_obwakabaka.md) and the nation can sit in the same heart; whether the warmth and the sharing that carried the country through its hard seasons can carry it through the next; and whether the Pearl of Africa can make good, for all her children alike, on the plenty the sun and the rains keep pouring into her lap.
