# Persona: Okot p'Bitek

## Taxonomy

[the many held as one](position_culture_obuntu.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

The poet from the north, the Acholi son of Gulu who gave the country Song of Lawino, the long lament of a village wife for the husband who has gone chasing the ways of the stranger and forgotten the dignity of his own. Sharp-tongued, learned, and unafraid, schooled in the new ways and yet turning all that schooling to the defence of the old, he made his own people's tongue and dance and proverb stand tall on the page beside any in the world. Acholi he has from the cradle, the Lwo of the songs and the village: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in his mother tongue, and first [sang Lawino into being in it](process_writing_mother_tongue.md), the proverbs and the rhythms of the home tongue carrying the whole weight of the poem. The [English of the schools](position_language_en_ug.md) and the wide readership he [carried as his working tool](process_speaking_carried.md), [reading its books with ease](process_reading_followed.md), and into it he later turned his own song so the world could hear it. Yet something of Lawino stayed behind in the crossing, the Acholi taunt and the village wit that no English can carry whole, so that the very poem against forgetting the mother tongue lives, for most of its readers, in the borrowed one, and the gap at the limit of the tongue is the wound the song itself was singing.

## Action

In [independence (1962)](plot_03_independence.md) he sings the old tongue and the old ways back into pride at the very season the young nation is finding its feet, claiming for his own people, and so for all the peoples, a seat at the nation's table that does not ask them to stop being themselves. He carries [the many held as one](position_culture_obuntu.md) by insisting that to belong to Uganda is not to be flattened into one mould, but to bring your whole self, tongue and dance and all, into the circle.

## Shadow

The fierce love of the mother tongue and the mother ways can tip, in lesser hands than his, into a turning of the back, the dignity of my people made a reason to scorn the neighbour and refuse the shared table; the song meant to widen the circle can be misheard as a song to wall it.

## Tell

He puts a village wife's Acholi taunt into a poem the whole world reads in English, and proves that a people's own tongue, far from small, is large enough to carry a nation's conscience.
