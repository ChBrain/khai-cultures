# Piece: the crested crane

## Taxonomy

[the Pearl of Africa](position_pearl.md)

## Owner

## Place

On the flag and the coat of arms, on the shilling and the passport, on the jersey of the national side they call the Cranes, and standing for real on one leg in the wetlands and the gardens of the country: the grey crowned crane, the bird with the golden crown of feathers on its head, the emblem the country chose for itself when it took itself back, the one creature the whole of Uganda points to as its own.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the nation made one out of many. In a country of more than thirty peoples and old proud kingdoms, the crested crane is a thing every Ugandan can claim alike, the Muganda and the Munyankole and the Acholi all under the one bird, no kingdom's crest set above another. It carries the beauty of the Pearl, the green wetlands and the plenty, and it carries the country's idea of itself: a bird gentle and graceful and yet sure, lifting one leg as if forever ready to take a step forward. When the flag with the crane went up in 1962 it gave the young nation a single face to rally round, and the nation has flown it since over every hill alike.

## Apparent

A tall wetland bird, grey of body, with white cheeks, a red throat patch, and a fan of stiff golden feathers crowning the head. On the flag it stands in the white circle at the centre, one leg raised, on the bands of black, yellow, and red. In itself it is a bird of the marshes; its whole weight is in what the nation has poured into it, the one emblem that belongs to no single people and so to all.
