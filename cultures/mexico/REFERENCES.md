# México: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-domain historical sources behind
the plots, personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_es.md](pitch_es.md) is written from Mexico's profile on Geert
Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no number.
The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the record without
entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Mexico score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 81                     |
| Individualism                 | 30                     |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 69                     |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 82                     |
| Long Term Orientation         | 24                     |
| Indulgence                    | 97                     |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published by
Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group; Mexico ranks high on Power Distance,
Uncertainty Avoidance, and Indulgence, and low on Individualism.

## Historical sources

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public historical record. They are credited here and never claimed; the
staging is original work. The gap at the heart of the story - the Conquest, the
colonial caste system, the long deferral of land reform, and modern inequality and
violence - is staged soberly on the public record, as the gap between promise and
reality, never luridly and with no atrocity detail.

The defining persona [el mestizo](persona_el_mestizo.md) is an archetype, not a
historical individual: a composite that carries the tension at the heart of the
nation between its indigenous and its Spanish halves, drawing on Jose Vasconcelos'
idea of la raza cosmica and on Octavio Paz's reading of the Mexican character, the
mask, and the solitude beneath it.
