# Persona: Jack Mapanje

## Taxonomy

[the many peoples](position_the_peoples.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Projection

A poet and linguist of the lakeshore, a son of the people who turned the riddles and proverbs of the village into verse that the whole English-reading world would come to hear, and who carried the cadence of home into the bridge tongue without ever losing it. Wry, careful, and deep-rooted, he wrote in English yet thought in image and proverb, the way the elders speak when they will not say a thing straight. His Chichewa he [spoke](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thought](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) as the first water of his mind, and the chameleons and the drums and the village riddles of it ran straight into his lines. English, [the bridge tongue](position_language_en_mw.md), he [carried](process_speaking_carried.md) as his written tongue and [read and followed](process_reading_followed.md) the wide world's books in it, the bridge on which a Malawian voice could travel out. He wrote his country in the coloniser's tongue so that the country could be heard at all, pouring the proverb of one language into the grammar of another, and what would not cross over, the exact weight of a Chichewa saying in a Chichewa ear, stayed behind at the gap at the limit of the tongue. He belongs to [the Warm Heart](position_culture_the_warm_heart.md) as the poet who carried its proverbs abroad without ever losing the shape of home in them.

## Action

In [the Warm Heart](plot_04_the_nation.md) he gives the modern nation its poet, proving that a Malawian voice steeped in the proverbs of [the many peoples](position_the_peoples.md) can speak to the world without leaving home behind. He carries the country's tongues forward by writing the village into the world's language and making the world stop and listen.

## Shadow

The poet who must veil his meaning in proverb and riddle is the country's own caution: that the gentle indirection of a careful people can be a shield and a cage at once, that what cannot be said straight may go unsaid too long, and that even the warmest house has rooms where the plain word is dear.

## Tell

He puts a chameleon and a village drum into an English poem and makes a reader an ocean away feel the exact weight of a Chichewa proverb, the lakeshore carried whole into the bridge tongue.
