# Plot: the early voice

## Cue

In the southern highlands a mission-trained pastor, [John Chilembwe](persona_chilembwe.md), has founded his own church and his own schools and watched his people bear too much for too long with too little voice. By 1915, with the world at war and his people pressed hard, he resolves to speak up plainly for their dignity, whatever it costs him.

## Action

In 1915 Chilembwe makes his stand for the dignity of his own people, the first to say openly that a man of this soil is no one's lesser and deserves to be heard and treated as a man. The stand is put down and he loses his life for it, but the plain word he spoke does not die with him. He is remembered ever after, soberly and with honour, as an early father of the nation, his face borne on the country's money, the first clear voice for the equal worth of his people, told here without dwelling on the sorrow of how it ended.

## Stage

- [John Chilembwe](persona_chilembwe.md): the southern pastor who stands up for the dignity of his people and becomes an early father of the nation.
- [the many peoples](position_the_peoples.md): the conviction he speaks for first and plainly, that the peoples of the land are no one's lesser.
- [Lilongwe](place_lilongwe.md): the central ground of the nation that would later grow from the dignity he was first to claim.

## Tension

A gentle and patient people reaches the place past which dignity will not be made to bend, and one man says so plainly and pays for it. The country's warmth and its long patience are shown to have a floor beneath them, a worth that will not be bargained away, and the nation that would one day take its own name finds in him its earliest and soberest father, the proof that the open hand is not a soft head.
