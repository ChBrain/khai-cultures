# Piece: nsima

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

On every table in the country, morning, noon, and night: the thick white porridge of pounded maize, cooked stiff and smooth in the pot and turned out in firm portions, eaten with the right hand, pinched and rolled and dipped in the relish, the ndiwo, that sits beside it. In the village home and the city flat, at the chief's gathering and the roadside stall, nsima is the meal a Malawian means when he says he has eaten, the centre of the plate the whole country shares around.

## Load Bearing

It stands for the country's daily life and its very idea of being fed: a Malawian will tell you that whatever else he has eaten, he has not truly eaten until he has had his nsima. It is the staple that holds the body and the custom that holds the people, shared from the one plate so that to eat together is to belong together, the Warm Heart made a thing you can taste. Without it the table has no centre and the guest no portion already waiting, and the whole daily practice of welcome would have nothing to gather round.

## Apparent

A mound of plain white maize porridge, stiff and steaming, turned out onto a shared plate, eaten with the fingers of the washed right hand. In itself it is the simplest of foods, maize flour and water and the patience to turn it smooth; its whole weight is in what the country gathers around it, the relish dipped, the hand washed, the guest fed, and the meaning of having eaten at all.

## Yearbook

- The staple of the country since maize took root on the land, the firm porridge that a Malawian counts as the only true meal.
- Eaten from the shared plate with the right hand, the relish beside it, the daily form of the country's welcome and belonging.
- Stretched and thinned through the hungry season and shared first with the one who has none, the food in which the country's hardship and its open hand are both made plain.
