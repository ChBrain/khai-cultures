## Cue

In the nineteenth century the explorer and missionary David Livingstone makes his way up the Shire River and reaches the great lake, coming through the highlands of the south. He looks out on its night water, scattered with the lamps of the fishermen in their canoes, and gives it the name the country has loved ever since: the Lake of Stars.

## Action

The missions follow the explorer to the lake and the highlands, planting churches and schools and printing the local tongues into books for the first time. The town of Blantyre rises in the south, named for the missionary's Scottish birthplace, and the cool highlands are laid out in the tea estates that still spread below Mulanje. The wider world reaches the lake, bringing the school, the printed word, and the bridge tongue, and the older order of the many peoples meets it, taking up some of the new and keeping much of the old, the long encounter handled here as it was lived, soberly and with dignity.

## Tension

The wider world arrives at the lake, and with it the school, the book, and the bridge tongue that will one day let a country of many tongues speak as one, and also the long unequal encounter of that age. The country takes what serves it, the literacy and the common tongue, and keeps its own dances and its own deep customs beneath, the meeting of the two carried soberly, neither denied nor dwelt on, as the ground from which the nation would later rise.
