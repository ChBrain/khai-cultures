# Plot: the Warm Heart

## Taxonomy

[play_malawi](play_malawi.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

By the middle of the twentieth century the land of many peoples is ready to govern itself. In 1964 it takes its own name, Malawi, from the old Maravi of the central plain, and raises its own flag, the rising sun over the black and red and green, and sets up its parliament and its capital on the central ground at [Lilongwe](place_lilongwe.md).

## Action

The self-governing nation makes its way in the world as the Warm Heart of Africa, a name earned by the friendliness the country is known for everywhere. It holds its north and its south and its many tongues in one house, with English the neutral bridge between them; its singers, [Wambali Mkandawire](persona_wambali.md) among them, carry the warmth of the lakeshore out onto the world's stages, and its writers, [Jack Mapanje](persona_mapanje.md) and [Legson Kayira](persona_kayira.md), carry the village and the lake into the world's tongue. The Gule Wamkulu of the Chewa, the Great Dance of the masked spirit-dancers, is honoured by the whole world as a masterpiece to be kept, and the country, poor by the map and rich in welcome, makes its name on the one wealth it has always had.

## Stage

- [Wambali Mkandawire](persona_wambali.md): the singer who carries the warmth of the country out onto the world's stages and home again.
- [the Warm Heart](position_warm_heart.md): the friendliness that earns the country the name the whole continent uses for it.
- [Lilongwe](place_lilongwe.md): the central capital from which the self-governing nation takes its name and governs.

## Tension

A country the map keeps poor and small takes its own name and stakes its place in the world not on power or wealth but on warmth, holding its many peoples in one house and carrying its songs and its dances out to the world. The open question is set for good: whether the heart that earned the name can be kept open against the hard years, the thinning lake, and the growing need, or whether hardship will at last ask too much of the Warm Heart of Africa.
