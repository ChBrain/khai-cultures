# Position: Massachusetts English

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Massachusetts English carries the non-rhotic Boston accent, dropping the r after a vowel so "car" becomes "cah," then reinserting an r between vowels where the spelling never put one ("idea-r-of it"). It carries the broad, flattened a of "aunt" and "bath" heard from Plymouth to Cambridge, and a lexicon of its own: wicked as an intensifier, rotary for a traffic circle, packie for a liquor store.

## Orders

It forces the speaker to drop every postvocalic r on principle, then insert one between vowels where the accent's own rule demands it, marking native speech the moment a single "car" or "idea" is said aloud. It forces the broad a onto a fixed set of words that a rhotic, flat-vowelled speaker from elsewhere never learns to hear correctly.

## Loses

It loses its dropped r and broad a to the flattened, national broadcast standard that erodes non-rhoticity with each younger, more mobile generation. It loses ground, too, to the Latinate register of the university and biotech corridor, which lays a specialist, imported vocabulary over the plain, argumentative civic speech of the town meeting and the parish hall.

## Drives

It shapes whoever grows up speaking it to treat the r as a movable piece, dropped after a vowel and inserted between two others, and to argue in the clipped, direct rhythm of the town meeting. It drives a Bostonian to mark insider from outsider by accent alone, keeping the accent even as the institutions around it speak a global, r-full English.
