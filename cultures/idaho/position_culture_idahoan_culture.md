# Position: Idahoan culture

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Membership in a state carved out of dry volcanic plains and high mountain range, where the [Snake River](place_snake_river_canal.md) turned desert into farmland and the peaks above it turned into resorts and tribal homeland at once. It carries [agrarian industry](position_agrarian_industry.md), the canal-built wealth of potato and crop production; it carries [mountain wilderness](position_mountain_wilderness.md), the stewardship owed to forest, ridge, and river by those who work and those who merely visit; and beneath both it carries the older claim of the Nez Perce, whose sovereignty the land's newer industries were built over. Its tongue is [Idahoan English](position_language_en_us_id.md), spoken with the drawl of the canal yard and the plant floor alike. This is what an Idahoan carries without ever having chosen it: a state that must earn its water and answer to its mountains before it can call either one its own.

## Orders

That water be treated as the scarcest and most contested resource, worth engineering canals and aquifer plans to protect; that the mountain slopes be worked and skied but not stripped bare, since the wilderness gives the state its second industry as surely as the plains give it its first; that the harvest come before comfort in a land whose wealth was built by irrigation, not given by rainfall; and that the older claim of tribal sovereignty be reckoned with rather than assumed settled, since the retreat of 1877 is not a closed chapter but a debt still carried.

## Loses

Ground where the fast, technical talk of canal engineers and irrigation technicians drowns out the patient, treaty-bound rhythm of tribal land stewardship, so that river ecosystem preservation gets treated as secondary to commercial potato yields and speedway tourism. It loses ground, too, where the mountain counties and the farming counties fail to see themselves in each other, wilderness zoning read by one as protection and by the other as a threat to family land use, and where the Snake River Aquifer, the resource that built the state, is drawn down faster than it can be protected.

## Drives

It drives the Idahoan to build wealth out of hard, dry ground through engineering rather than through luck, to log the pine and farm the potato while still answering for the mountain and the river above the canal. It drives the state to hold two identities in one hand, the agrarian plain and the alpine slope, and to keep asking whether an aquifer plan drawn up for one can actually protect the other. And it drives whoever is raised here to measure a season not by comfort but by whether the harvest, the herd, or the resort came through it whole.
