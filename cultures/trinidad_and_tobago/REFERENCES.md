# Trinidad and Tobago: References

The provenance behind this culture: the Hofstede source data that tunes the pitch
(kept here, never in the prose) and the public-record sources behind the plots,
personas, places, and pieces.

## Hofstede source data

The pitch [pitch_en.md](pitch_en.md) is written from Trinidad and Tobago's profile
on Geert Hofstede's model of national culture and names no dimension and carries no
number. The scores and their source are recorded here so the data is on the record
without entering the prose.

| Dimension                     | Trinidad and Tobago score (approx.) |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Power Distance                | 47                                  |
| Individualism                 | 16                                  |
| Motivation toward Achievement | 58                                  |
| Uncertainty Avoidance         | 55                                  |
| Long Term Orientation         | 13                                  |
| Indulgence                    | 80                                  |

Source: Geert Hofstede, Hofstede Insights (the Country Comparison tool and the
six-dimensions model of national culture). Scores are approximate, as published by
Hofstede Insights / The Culture Factor Group; Trinidad and Tobago's Indulgence
score is among the higher in the published data, fitting the festive Trini tone.

## Sources on the public record

The plots, the historic personas they cast, the places, and the pieces are drawn
from the public record. They are credited here and never claimed; the staging is
original work. The hard history at the heart of the story - slavery, Indian
indenture, and the divisions of race - is staged soberly on the public record as
the gap between the promise and the reality, with the plural society and its arts
at the centre and no atrocity detail.

The one defining archetype, [the panman](persona_the_panman.md), is not a
historical individual but a composite: the maker, tuner and player of the
steelpan, carrying the genius of the yards, the banned drum driven into steel, and
the ancestral African tongues broken in the barracks and borrowed back in
fragments.
