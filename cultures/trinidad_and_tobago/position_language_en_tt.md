# Position: the Trinidadian tongue

## Taxonomy

Parent group: positions

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Has

Trinidadian speech runs two systems in one mouth: the standard English of the school and the page, and an English-lexicon Creole built on an African and Bhojpuri grammar underneath borrowed English words. The Creole lets a speaker do things the standard cannot: serial verbs stacked without a conjunction ("run go tell she"), a habitual marked by "does" or "does be" where the standard has no plain way to say a thing happens as a rule, and a completive "done" that closes an action the way no simple English tense can. It carries picong, the art of saying a barbed thing two ways at once, and calypso's double entendre, both built on the Creole's tolerance for a word to carry two meanings walking side by side.

## Orders

The two systems force a constant choice of register: a speaker must judge, sentence by sentence, whether the setting calls for the acrolect near the standard or the basilect deep in Creole, and code-switch between them inside a single conversation, sometimes a single sentence. The Creole orders its own grammar regardless of the choice: no obligatory plural "-s" where the standard demands one, a copula that can drop out entirely ("she happy," no "is"), and tense and aspect carried by a small set of preverbal particles rather than by the verb's own ending. The lyric forms, calypso and picong, order a further discipline: the line must scan to a rhythm and still land a second, hidden sense.

## Loses

What the Creole flattens, the standard cannot easily restore: its habitual and completive particles have no single clean equivalent in the schooled English laid over it, so a fluent bilingual speaker still hunts for the word when writing formally. What the standard flattens runs the other way: the register that reads as "correct" strips out the particles, the serial verbs, and the double-voiced picong, leaving a plainer sentence with less packed into it. And beneath both, the African and Bhojpuri languages that fed the Creole its rhythm and its turns of phrase survive only as traces, thinned to a handful of words and a cadence, the fuller tongues themselves gone in a generation or two.

## Drives

Holding both systems trains a speaker to read a room by ear before opening the mouth, since the choice of register is itself a social act, not a neutral one. It builds an instinct for compression, packing tense, aspect, and mood into a short particle rather than a verb ending, and it builds an ear tuned to double meaning, since picong and calypso reward the sentence that says one thing on top and another underneath. Whoever grows up switching between the two hears English itself as layered rather than singular, a single word carrying a plain sense and a slanted one at once.
