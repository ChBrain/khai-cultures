# Piece: the steelpan

## Taxonomy

Parent group: pieces

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Place

In the panyards of Laventille and across the islands, on the Savannah stage at Panorama and in orchestras around the world: a concave drum sunk and tuned from a discarded fifty-five-gallon oil drum, its face hammered into note-faces a player strikes with rubber-tipped sticks, the national instrument of Trinidad and Tobago. From the yards of the poor to the concert hall, the sound the nation knows itself by.

## Load Bearing

It stands for [mas, pan and calypso](position_mas_pan_and_calypso.md) and for the whole genius of the place: the only acoustic instrument invented in the 20th century, beaten not in a workshop but in the yards of a poor and dispossessed people out of the very oil drums the wealth of the island came in. Banned and beaten down before the nation crowned it, the pan carries the proof that a people with nothing can make from castoff an orchestra the world will copy and never quite equal. In a callaloo held by no single blood, the pan is the shared instrument every Trini can claim. Without this drum the road would have no sound and the small place no clearest gift to the world.

## Apparent

A steel oil drum, its top sunk into a bowl and its surface hammered and grooved into a pattern of tuned note-faces, set on a stand and struck with two stick. In itself it is industrial scrap reshaped; its weight is entirely in the ear and the hammer of the tuner who hears the music in the steel, and the band that turns a yard of drums into one voice.

## Yearbook

- Beaten up in the yards of Port of Spain in the 1930s and 1940s, after the colony banned the skin drum and the people drove the rhythm into bamboo and then into steel.
- Once outlawed and looked down on as the music of the yard, then crowned the national instrument of Trinidad and Tobago.
- Carried out to the world and into orchestras everywhere, the only acoustic instrument the 20th century invented.
