## Projection

The Nobel laureate born into the Indo-Trinidadian world of the cane and the temple, grandson of the indentured, who wrote "A House for Mr Biswas" out of his father's life and made the world read the island he could not wait to leave. Exacting, mordant, restless, and unsparing - of the colony, of the world, and of himself - he turned the small place into great literature and never forgave it for being small. English he has whole and honed to a blade: he [speaks](process_speaking_mother_tongue.md) and [thinks](process_thinking_mother_tongue.md) in the mother tongue of the empire that schooled him, and he [drafts it](process_writing_drafted.md) into a prose so precise it won the colony a place in world letters. The Bhojpuri of his grandparents, the Hindi-dialect his Indo-Trinidadian elders brought from the ships, he only [hears](process_hearing_mother_tongue.md) at the family table and never owns, [reading it only haltingly](process_reading_caught.md) and catching what he can - the rituals in a tongue half-remembered, the prayers in words he cannot truly read. He wrote the Indian world of the callaloo into the Englishman's English and won every prize the metropolis could give, while the Bhojpuri his line carried off the ships thinned in a single generation into the scent of incense and a few words at a wedding - so the gap at the limit of the tongue is the ancestral language he could hear at home and never speak, the India he carried in his name and lost in his mouth.

## Action

In [the Energy Nation](plot_04_the_energy_nation.md) he writes the Indo-Trinidadian world into world letters and holds the cosmopolitan present up to the cold light, the laureate of [the callaloo nation](position_the_callaloo_nation.md) who shows both its richness and its wounds. He makes the small twin islands the matter of great books and the world read the place through his unsparing eye.

## Shadow

The clear eye that misses nothing can grow cold and proud: the son of the colony who scorned it, who called the new nations half-made and gave the picong no quarter, the brilliance that won the world by leaving home and judging it from the heights. The truth-teller can become the man who sees only the flaw, and reads the callaloo as a thing that failed rather than a thing being made.

## Tell

He writes his father's defeated, striving life into one of the century's great novels and signs it with the name of a village in India he will never see - honouring the world that made him in the very act of leaving it behind.
