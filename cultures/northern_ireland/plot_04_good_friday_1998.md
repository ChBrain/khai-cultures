# Plot: The Troubles and the Good Friday Agreement 1998

## Taxonomy

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

From the late 1960s a civil-rights movement and the failure to resolve the place's divisions give way to three decades of conflict. The Troubles cost some three and a half thousand lives across all communities and touched nearly every family, on every side.

## Action

After long and patient effort, the political leaders of both traditions are brought to the table. [John Hume](persona_hume.md), the constitutional nationalist, and [David Trimble](persona_trimble.md), the unionist, lead their communities toward a settlement, and on Good Friday 1998 the Belfast Agreement is signed and then endorsed by referendum on both sides of the border. It sets up power-sharing between the traditions, rests the constitutional question on the consent of the people, and acknowledges that one may be British or Irish or both. Hume and Trimble share the Nobel Peace Prize that year. The peace is partial and fragile, but it is real, and it is made together.

## Stage

- [John Hume](persona_hume.md): the nationalist leader who carries his tradition to the settlement.
- [David Trimble](persona_trimble.md): the unionist leader who carries his to meet it.
- [the peace](position_the_peace.md): the power-sharing settlement made by consent of both communities.

## Tension

The Agreement does not declare a winner, and that is its strength and its difficulty at once. It ends the war without ending the difference, asking two traditions to govern together and to hold their rival hopes by consent rather than by force - a settlement that must be chosen again and again to hold.
