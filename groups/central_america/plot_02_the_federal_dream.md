# Plot: the Federal Dream

## Taxonomy

[play_central_america](play_central_america.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

The single colonial province breaks free of Spain in one act, and the isthmus has a choice: to stay one nation as it was one colony, or to come apart into many. For a brief, bright generation it tries to stay one, and a dream of a united Central America takes shape.

## Action

In 1821 the isthmus wins its independence from Spain, leaving Spanish rule as a single province as it had lived under it. After a short passage through the Mexican empire, the isthmus forms the Federal Republic of Central America, a union of states reaching across the land. Its great champion is Francisco Morazan, who fights to hold the federation together under one constitution and one isthmian nation. But distance, rivalry between the states, and the strain between liberal and conservative factions pull the union apart, and by 1841 the Federal Republic has dissolved into separate sovereign republics: [Guatemala](../../cultures/guatemala/play_guatemala.md), [Honduras](../../cultures/honduras/play_honduras.md), [Costa Rica](../../cultures/costa_rica/play_costa_rica.md), [Nicaragua](../../cultures/nicaragua/play_nicaragua.md), and El Salvador. The single colony has become a row of small nations, and Morazan's dream of one Central America passes into memory, an unfinished idea that every later union will reach back toward.

## Stage

- [Guatemala](../../cultures/guatemala/play_guatemala.md): the largest state and the old colonial capital, center of gravity of the federation.
- [Honduras](../../cultures/honduras/play_honduras.md): Morazan's homeland and a heartland of the federal cause.
- [Costa Rica](../../cultures/costa_rica/play_costa_rica.md): the quiet southern state that went its own peaceable way.
- [Nicaragua](../../cultures/nicaragua/play_nicaragua.md): the land of lakes that broke for its own sovereignty.

## Tension

For one generation the isthmus holds together as a single nation, the closest it has ever come to Morazan's dream, and then it cannot. The federation fractures not by foreign hand but by its own rivalries and the borders the states could not erase, leaving a row of small republics and a powerful memory of what was almost one country. That broken dream is the wound and the promise the grouping carries forward: the proof that union is possible and the proof of how easily it falls apart.
