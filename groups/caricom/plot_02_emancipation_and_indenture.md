# Plot: Emancipation and Indenture

## Taxonomy

[play_caricom](play_caricom.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

Slavery ends in the British Caribbean, and the plantations that depended on it must find new hands. The planters look across the oceans, and a new wave of arrivals, indentured labourers from India and Asia, comes to the fields, remaking the peoples of the region into the plural societies they remain.

## Action

Across 1834 to 1838 the British Empire abolishes slavery and then ends the apprenticeship that followed it, freeing the enslaved across [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md), [Trinidad and Tobago](../../cultures/trinidad_and_tobago/play_trinidad_and_tobago.md), [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md), and the other colonies. To keep the plantations working, the planters turn to indenture, bringing labourers under contract from India and elsewhere in Asia across the following decades, and the Dutch do the same in [Suriname](../../cultures/suriname/play_suriname.md). The result is the plural society that defines so much of the modern region: in Trinidad and in the Guianas, the descendants of enslaved Africans and of indentured Indians come to live side by side, joined by smaller communities of other origins, in nations of more than one people and more than one faith. Out of emancipation and indenture, treated here as a matter of public record, the Caribbean becomes one of the most thoroughly mixed regions on earth.

## Stage

- [Trinidad and Tobago](../../cultures/trinidad_and_tobago/play_trinidad_and_tobago.md): the classic plural society of African and Indian descent.
- [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md): the mainland nation shaped by the same two great migrations.
- [Suriname](../../cultures/suriname/play_suriname.md): the Dutch colony where indenture drew labourers from Asia into a plural people.
- [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md): where emancipation reshaped a largely African-descended society.

## Tension

Emancipation freed the enslaved and indenture peopled the plantations anew, and together they made the plural societies that are the region's strength and its strain alike. The mixing of African, Indian, and other peoples into one Caribbean is the source of much of its culture and creativity; it is also, in some nations, a fault line of politics and identity. Staged here soberly and from the public record, this is the chapter in which the community's peoples take their modern shape, neither one people nor many strangers, but plural nations bound by a shared and complicated descent.
