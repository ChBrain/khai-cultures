## Name

CARICOM, the Caribbean Community: the integration bloc of the small nations of the Caribbean and its South American shoulder, bound by a shared plantation past, by cricket and the University of the West Indies, and by the common cause of small states facing a large world. Its members are the sovereign nations of the islands and the Guiana mainland, spanning English, French-Creole, and Dutch-speaking peoples.

## Arc

The Caribbean Community, the integration bloc of the small nations of the Caribbean and its South American shoulder. Born of a shared history, the sugar islands and the Guiana mainland were built on the same plantation-and-slavery economy, peopled by enslaved Africans and then by indentured labourers from India and Asia into plural societies, and decolonized largely from Britain, with Haiti from France and Suriname from the Netherlands. The region first tried union in the short-lived West Indies Federation, and after it failed the nations built CARICOM in 1973 to face the world together as small states. They are bound by cricket, the one West Indies team for which the islands play as one, by the University of the West Indies, by the Caribbean Single Market and Economy, and by the common vulnerability of small island states to hurricanes and a rising sea. The community spans English, French-Creole, and Dutch-speaking nations across islands and mainland; it is divided by distance, by the pull of larger neighbours, and by the difficulty of binding a dozen-plus small states into one bloc. From Jamaica and Barbados in the north to Trinidad off the South American coast, from the volcanic Windwards to Belize on the Central American shore, and out to Guyana and Suriname on the mainland, the community gathers peoples shaped by the same history into one body that is more than the sum of its small parts, and still less than a single nation. A group that shows how far a shared past and a shared sea can carry a family of small states, and where the binding begins to strain.

## Triggers

**The shared plantation-and-slavery past**

The deepest ground of the community is a common history: the sugar islands and the Guiana mainland were built on the same plantation-and-slavery economy, peopled by enslaved Africans and then by indentured labourers, and shaped into plural societies by the same hard inheritance. That shared past, treated soberly and as a matter of public record, is what makes nations of three colonial tongues recognize one another as kin.

**Cricket and the University of the West Indies**

Two institutions carry the region's unity in everyday life: the one West Indies cricket team, for which [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md), [Barbados](../../cultures/barbados/play_barbados.md), [Trinidad and Tobago](../../cultures/trinidad_and_tobago/play_trinidad_and_tobago.md), [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md), and the smaller islands play as one nation on the field, and the University of the West Indies, the shared regional university that trains the community's people across campuses in several member states.

**The Caribbean Single Market and Economy**

The CSME is the community's economic spine, the project to bind the small national markets into one space for the free movement of goods, services, capital, and skilled people, so that states too small to face the world alone can meet it together as a single market.

**Small-state solidarity**

The members are small states that share a common vulnerability to hurricanes, to a rising sea, and to the weight of great powers and large neighbours, and CARICOM is their instrument for facing those threats with one voice, the cooperation of the small standing together where none could stand alone.

## Stakes

Whether a dozen-plus small nations, scattered across islands and a mainland and split among three colonial tongues, can be one community strong enough to face hurricanes, great powers, and a rising sea together, or whether distance, size, and the pull of larger neighbours keep CARICOM a cooperation of the small rather than a union.
