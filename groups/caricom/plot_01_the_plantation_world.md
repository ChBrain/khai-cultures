# Plot: the Plantation World

## Taxonomy

[play_caricom](play_caricom.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

Across the islands and the Guiana mainland, European powers carve the land into estates for a single crop. Sugar makes the Caribbean the most valuable colonial ground of its age, and the world it builds, the plantation world, is the shared root from which every member of the community grows.

## Action

From the seventeenth century onward, [Barbados](../../cultures/barbados/play_barbados.md) and then [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md) and the other sugar islands are remade into a landscape of plantations, and the same model is carried to the Guiana mainland in [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md). The economy rests on enslaved African labour, and over generations a society is built on that foundation across the whole region, from the Anglophone islands to the French colony that would become [Haiti](../../cultures/haiti/play_haiti.md). The crops and the colonizers differ at the edges, but the shape is the same everywhere: a small planter class, a large enslaved population, and a single export bound for distant markets. This is the common inheritance of the community, treated here soberly and as a matter of public record, the plantation world that made these scattered colonies into peoples with a shared past long before they had any shared institutions.

## Stage

- [Barbados](../../cultures/barbados/play_barbados.md): the early laboratory of the sugar plantation, the model carried across the region.
- [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md): the largest sugar island, a centre of the plantation economy.
- [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md): the Guiana mainland, where the plantation model crossed from islands to continent.
- [Haiti](../../cultures/haiti/play_haiti.md): the French colony built on the same foundation, the most profitable of all before its revolution.

## Tension

The plantation world is the shared origin of every nation in the community, and it is a hard one: a regional economy built on enslaved labour and a single crop, staged here soberly and without dwelling on its cruelties, as a matter of public record. That common root is what binds these scattered peoples into a recognizable family, yet it is also a wound carried into the modern era, the foundation on which independence, integration, and the long work of becoming more than colonies would all have to be built.
