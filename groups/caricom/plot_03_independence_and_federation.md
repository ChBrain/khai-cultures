# Plot: Independence and Federation

## Taxonomy

[play_caricom](play_caricom.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

As the age of empire ends, the British Caribbean reaches for nationhood, and at first for a single nationhood. The dream is one federated West Indian nation; when that dream breaks apart, the islands and the mainland win their independence one by one, and then build a looser bond in its place.

## Action

In 1958 the British colonies of the Caribbean join in the West Indies Federation, an attempt to bring the islands into a single nation before independence. But the federation cannot hold: the larger members pull away, [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md) votes to leave, [Trinidad and Tobago](../../cultures/trinidad_and_tobago/play_trinidad_and_tobago.md) follows, and by 1962 the federation is dissolved. Independence comes instead nation by nation, beginning with Jamaica and Trinidad and Tobago in 1962, [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md) in 1966, [Barbados](../../cultures/barbados/play_barbados.md) in 1966, and the smaller islands over the years that follow. Yet the case for working together does not die with the federation. The newly independent states, too small to face the world alone, build looser instruments of cooperation, and in 1973 the Treaty of Chaguaramas establishes the Caribbean Community, CARICOM, to bind the region in trade and common cause without binding it into one state. The single nation failed; the community was born from its lesson.

## Stage

- [Jamaica](../../cultures/jamaica/play_jamaica.md): whose departure broke the federation and whose independence opened the era.
- [Trinidad and Tobago](../../cultures/trinidad_and_tobago/play_trinidad_and_tobago.md): independent in 1962 and a founder of the community.
- [Barbados](../../cultures/barbados/play_barbados.md): an early independent state and a founder of CARICOM.
- [Guyana](../../cultures/guyana/play_guyana.md): the mainland member, home to the community's secretariat.

## Tension

This is the chapter in which the region chooses its shape: not the single West Indian nation that the federation dreamed of, but a community of sovereign small states. The failure of the federation is the founding lesson of CARICOM, the proof that distance, size, and the pull of separate interests would not allow one nation, but that they need not prevent one community. The tension set here runs through everything that follows: how much union a family of small states can bear, and whether cooperation can ever deepen into the unity that the federation could not achieve.
