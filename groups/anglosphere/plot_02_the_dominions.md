# Plot: the Dominions

## Taxonomy

[play_anglosphere](play_anglosphere.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai-cultures

## Cue

The lesson of the American break is learned. Rather than hold its remaining settler colonies by force, the mother country lets them grow into self-governing nations within the family, keeping the Crown and the kinship while granting them their own parliaments and their own course.

## Action

Across the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries the settler colonies become self-governing dominions: [Canada](../../cultures/canada/play_canada.md) is confederated in 1867, [Australia](../../cultures/australia/play_australia.md) federates in 1901, and [New Zealand](../../cultures/new_zealand/play_new_zealand.md) takes dominion status in 1907. Each keeps the British Crown as head of state, the common law, and the English tongue, while writing its own democracy and, in time, its own foreign policy. The Statute of Westminster in 1931 confirms them as equals of Britain under the Crown, free nations bound by allegiance rather than rule, and the old empire begins its long turn into a Commonwealth of nations. The English-speaking world is now a family of self-governing states, not a single empire, and the bond is becoming a kinship of choice.

## Stage

- [Canada](../../cultures/canada/play_canada.md): the first dominion, the model for self-government within the family.
- [Australia](../../cultures/australia/play_australia.md): the federation of colonies into a continental nation under the Crown.
- [New Zealand](../../cultures/new_zealand/play_new_zealand.md): the dominion at the far edge of the world, keeping the tie across the greatest distance.

## Tension

The family avoids a second rupture by letting its members go free while keeping them close, and the dominions grow into nations without firing on the mother country as America had. But self-government is built on settler nations whose own first peoples were dispossessed in the founding, a reckoning each will carry into its later century; and the more fully the dominions become themselves, the more their own continents and interests will one day pull against the kinship that made them.
